Chinese Yam and Goji Berry Soup — Warming Nourishment for Autumn

🥘 Recipe · 7 min read

Introduction: A Bowl of Autumn Warmth

As the leaves turn and the air takes on a crisp edge, autumn calls for a shift in our eating habits. In Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM), the transition from summer to autumn is a time to protect the body's qi (vital energy) and gently support the Lungs and Spleen — two organ systems that are especially vulnerable to the dryness and cooling of the season. One of the most beloved seasonal recipes in the Chinese culinary tradition is Chinese Yam and Goji Berry Soup (Shan Yao Gou Qi Tang).

This soup is more than just comfort food. It is a balanced, nourishing tonic that warms without overheating, moisturises without creating dampness, and supports digestion while replenishing energy. Whether you are dealing with a touch of autumn fatigue, a dry throat, or simply seeking a grounding meal as the days grow shorter, this soup offers a gentle, effective remedy rooted in thousands of years of food-as-medicine wisdom.

In the sections that follow, we will explore the TCM properties of the key ingredients, the traditional history behind them, a complete step-by-step recipe, and guidance on when — and for whom — this soup is most beneficial.

TCM Properties of Key Ingredients

Understanding why this soup works so well begins with the energetics of its core ingredients. In TCM, foods are classified by their thermal nature, flavour, and the organ meridian they target. Let us look at each component.

Chinese Yam (Shan Yao, 山药)

Chinese yam is one of the most revered ingredients in TCM food therapy. It is classified as sweet in flavour and warm-neutral in nature. Traditionally, it is believed to enter the Spleen, Lung, and Kidney meridians. Its primary actions include:

  • Tonifying the Spleen Qi — supporting healthy digestion, firm stools, and steady energy levels after meals.
  • Nourishing Lung Yin — moistening dryness, soothing dry cough, and supporting respiratory health.
  • Strengthening Kidney Qi — gently fortifying the lower back, knees, and urinary system over time.

What makes Chinese yam particularly special is its dual nature: it is both a food and a medicine. It is gentle enough for daily use yet potent enough to be prescribed by TCM practitioners for chronic digestive weakness and respiratory dryness.

Goji Berries (Gou Qi Zi, 枸杞子)

Goji berries are sweet and neutral in nature, entering the Liver and Kidney meridians. In TCM, they are famed for:

  • Nourishing Liver and Kidney Yin — supporting healthy vision, joint lubrication, and reproductive vitality.
  • Benefiting the complexion — traditionally believed to brighten the eyes and promote radiant skin.
  • Moistening dryness — their gentle sweetness makes them an ideal companion to Chinese yam in autumn soups.

Goji berries are a staple in Chinese households, added to soups, teas, and congee throughout the colder months. Their vibrant red colour is also considered auspicious, adding a symbolic layer of vitality to the dish.

Optional Additions

Red dates (Hong Zao, 红枣) — sweet and warm, these enter the Spleen and Stomach meridians. They are often added to nourish the blood, calm the spirit, and balance the flavour of the soup with a natural sweetness.

Astragalus root (Huang Qi, 黄芪) — sweet and slightly warm, this herb enters the Spleen and Lung meridians. It is traditionally used to strengthen the immune system and raise the body's defensive wei qi. A few slices added to the simmering pot make the soup more tonifying, especially for those prone to seasonal colds.

Traditional Uses: A Legacy from the Shen Nong Ben Cao Jing

The use of Chinese yam as a medicinal food is documented in the Shen Nong Ben Cao Jing (Divine Farmer's Materia Medica), the foundational text of Chinese herbal medicine, compiled around 200–300 CE. In this classic work, shan yao is listed among the "superior" herbs — substances considered safe for long-term use and capable of promoting vitality and longevity.

The text describes Chinese yam as a remedy that "supplements the deficiencies of the body, boosts qi, strengthens the Spleen and Stomach, and benefits the Kidneys." Its classification as a superior herb means it was traditionally recommended for daily consumption, not only during illness but also as a preventive tonic for maintaining health.

Goji berries, too, have a long-standing place in Chinese medicine and folklore. They are mentioned in the Shen Nong Ben Cao Jing and are said to "strengthen the bones and sinews, calm the spirit, and prolong life when taken over a long period." Together, yam and goji form a classic pairing — one strengthens the Spleen and Lungs, the other nourishes the Liver and Kidneys — making the soup a harmonising tonic for the entire body's foundational energies.

Complete Recipe: Chinese Yam and Goji Berry Soup

This recipe is straightforward and forgiving. It requires about 10 minutes of active preparation and approximately 1 hour of gentle simmering. The result is a subtly sweet, savoury broth that feels like a warm hug on a cool autumn evening.

Ingredients

  • 250 g fresh Chinese yam (or 80 g dried shan yao slices — available at Asian grocery stores or online)
  • 30 g goji berries (dried, about 2 heaped tablespoons)
  • 6–8 red dates (optional, for sweetness and blood nourishment)
  • 3–4 slices fresh ginger (to warm the digestion and balance the soup's cooling elements)
  • 1.5 litres water or light vegetable broth
  • Salt to taste (a pinch at the end)
  • Optional: 300 g pork ribs (blanched), 4–5 slices astragalus root, or 20 g lotus seeds

Step-by-Step Instructions

  1. Prepare the yam. If using fresh Chinese yam, peel it carefully — the skin can irritate the hands, so wear gloves if needed. Slice into 1–2 cm rounds. If using dried shan yao slices, rinse them briefly under cold water.
  2. Rinse the goji berries and red dates. Soak the goji berries in warm water for 5–10 minutes to soften. Slit the red dates with a knife to release their flavour during simmering.
  3. Blanch the meat (if using). If adding pork ribs, place them in a pot of cold water, bring to a boil, and cook for 3 minutes. Drain and rinse to remove impurities.
  4. Combine and simmer. In a large soup pot, add the yam, ginger slices, red dates, astragalus (if using), lotus seeds (if using), and the blanched pork ribs. Pour in 1.5 litres of water or broth. Bring to a boil, then reduce the heat to low, cover, and simmer gently for 45–50 minutes.
  5. Add the goji berries. Goji berries should not be boiled for long, as their nutrients and flavour are delicate. Add them in the final 5–8 minutes of cooking.
  6. Season and serve. Add a small pinch of salt to taste — the soup will already carry natural sweetness from the yam and dates. Ladle into bowls and enjoy warm.

Total time: ~60 minutes (10 min prep + 50 min simmer). Serves 4 as a side or 2 as a light meal with rice or noodles.

When to Drink This Soup

In TCM dietary therapy, timing is as important as ingredients. Chinese Yam and Goji Berry Soup is considered a seasonal tonic for autumn and early winter. It is particularly recommended when you notice any of the following signs:

  • Dryness — dry skin, dry throat, or a dry, tickling cough that comes with cooler weather.
  • Fatigue or low energy — feeling unusually tired after meals or struggling with the seasonal shift.
  • Poor appetite — especially if food feels heavy or digestion has become sluggish.
  • Weak digestion — loose stools, bloating after eating, or a sense of "food sitting like a stone" in the stomach.
  • Frequent colds or weakened immunity — those who tend to catch every seasonal bug may benefit from the Spleen- and Lung-strengthening actions of this soup.

It may be enjoyed 2–3 times per week during the autumn months. Because the ingredients are classified as superior herbs — safe for long-term use — this soup can be made regularly without concern, provided the precautions below are observed.

Precautions

While Chinese Yam and Goji Berry Soup is gentle and well-tolerated by most, there are a few situations where it is traditionally advised to avoid or modify it:

  • Acute illness. This soup is not recommended during the acute phase of a cold, flu, or fever. The tonifying nature of yam and goji is believed to "trap" pathogenic factors in the body, potentially prolonging illness.
  • Digestive bloating or dampness. If you have a thick, greasy coating on your tongue, a heavy sensation in the body, or pronounced bloating, this soup may be too tonifying. In TCM, these are signs of "dampness," and nourishing soups are reserved for when digestion is clearer.
  • Inflammatory skin conditions. Some TCM sources suggest that goji berries may exacerbate certain "heat" conditions such as active acne or skin rashes. If you have an inflammatory skin condition, consult a qualified practitioner before consuming goji berries in significant amounts.
  • Pregnancy and nursing. The base ingredients are generally considered safe, but if adding astragalus or any medicinal herbs, consult a healthcare provider first.

Variations to Suit Your Needs

One of the wonderful qualities of this soup is its adaptability. Here are three common variations:

  • Yam, Goji, and Pork Rib Soup — adds a savoury depth and extra Qi- and blood-nourishing qualities from the meat. Blanch the ribs first for a clean, clear broth.
  • Astragalus-Infused Version — add 4–5 slices of huang qi (astragalus root) during the initial simmer. This variation is especially suited to those with weak immunity or who experience frequent seasonal colds.
  • Lotus Seed Variation — add 20 g of dried lotus seeds (rinsed and soaked) along with the yam. Lotus seeds support the Spleen, calm the shen (spirit), and are traditionally recommended for those who feel anxious or have trouble sleeping.

Frequently Asked Questions

What are the main ingredients in Chinese Yam and Goji Berry Soup?

The core ingredients are fresh or dried Chinese yam (shan yao) and goji berries (gou qi zi). Optional additions include red dates (hong zao), astragalus root (huang qi), lotus seeds, or pork ribs for extra nourishment and flavour. The soup is typically simmered with ginger slices to warm the digestion.

Who should drink Chinese Yam and Goji Berry Soup?

This soup is particularly suited to people with weak digestion, poor appetite, fatigue, or those recovering from illness. In TCM terms, it is beneficial for individuals with Spleen Qi deficiency (poor digestion, loose stools, low energy) and those needing gentle Lung or Kidney nourishment. It is a gentle, balanced recipe suitable for most adults during the cooler months.

When is the best time to drink this soup?

In TCM, this soup is ideal during autumn and early winter, when the weather turns cool and dry. It may be enjoyed 2–3 times per week as a nourishing meal or side dish. Many traditional practitioners recommend it at the first sign of autumn dry cough or when appetite begins to wane with the colder weather.

Can I add meat or other ingredients to this soup?

Yes. Pork ribs are a popular addition that adds savoury depth and extra Qi-nourishing qualities. Astragalus root (huang qi) can be added to strengthen the immune system, while lotus seeds support the Spleen and calm the mind. Red dates contribute sweetness and blood-nourishing benefits. For a vegan version, simply use the base ingredients with water or a light vegetable broth.

Are there any precautions or contraindications?

In TCM practice, this soup is not recommended during acute illnesses such as colds with fever or flu, or when there is pronounced digestive bloating or dampness (e.g., a thick, greasy tongue coating). Those with inflammatory skin conditions should consult a TCM practitioner before consuming goji berries in large amounts. As always, consult a qualified healthcare provider before making dietary changes, especially if you are pregnant, nursing, or managing a chronic condition.
