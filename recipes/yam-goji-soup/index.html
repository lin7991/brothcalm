Chinese Yam and Goji Berry Soup — Warming Nourishment for Autumn

🥘 Recipe · 7 min read

A Gentle Bowl for the Changing Season

As the last warmth of summer gives way to crisp autumn air, the body naturally begins to shift its needs. In Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM), this transition calls for foods that are warming, grounding, and easy to digest — foods that support the Spleen and Stomach as they work to convert the season's abundance into lasting energy. Few dishes embody this principle more beautifully than Chinese Yam and Goji Berry Soup.

This simple, soothing soup brings together two time-honoured ingredients: huai shan (dried Chinese yam) and gou qi zi (goji berries). Together they create a subtly sweet, fragrant broth that feels like a warm embrace on a cool autumn day. Whether you are new to TCM cooking or a seasoned practitioner, this recipe is a wonderful addition to your seasonal repertoire.

TCM Properties of the Key Ingredients

Chinese Yam (Shan Yao / Huai Shan)

Chinese yam holds a cherished place in TCM food therapy. Its properties are described as sweet and neutral, and it is traditionally believed to enter the Spleen, Lung, and Kidney meridians. Unlike some stronger herbs, yam is considered a gentle, daily-use ingredient — a food-first tonic that works gradually and harmoniously.

  • Spleen Qi Tonic: In TCM, the Spleen is responsible for transforming food into energy (Qi) and blood. Yam is traditionally used to support healthy digestion, reduce occasional sluggishness after meals, and build foundational energy.
  • Lung Moistener: Autumn is the season of the Lungs in the Five Element framework. Yam's neutral, moistening quality is thought to help ease dry coughs and support respiratory health during the cooler months.
  • Kidney Supporter: Yam is also traditionally regarded as a gentle kidney tonic, helping to strengthen the lower back and knees and support the body's constitutional reserve (Jing).

Goji Berries (Gou Qi Zi)

Goji berries are one of the most well-known tonic herbs in TCM, prized for their bright colour and nutrient-dense profile. Their properties are described as sweet and neutral, and they are traditionally believed to enter the Liver and Kidney meridians.

  • Liver and Kidney Yin Tonic: Goji berries are traditionally used to nourish Yin and Blood, supporting the Liver's role in smooth Qi flow and the Kidney's role in storing essence.
  • Vision Support: In TCM, the Liver opens into the eyes. Goji berries have been used for centuries to support eye health, especially during autumn when dryness can affect the eyes.
  • Gentle Warmth: Although neutral in temperature, goji berries add a subtle warming quality that makes them an ideal companion for yam in autumn soups.

Historical and Traditional Context

Chinese yam is recorded in the Shen Nong Ben Cao Jing (The Divine Farmer's Materia Medica, ~200 CE), one of the oldest pharmacopoeias in the world. It is classified as a superior herb — one that can be taken over long periods without harm and that supports overall vitality. Goji berries appear in the same classical text, praised for their ability to "harmonise the interior" and "fortify the sinews and bones."

Throughout Chinese culinary history, these two ingredients have been paired in soups and congees designed for post-illness recovery, digestive weakness, and seasonal transitions. The combination is considered particularly suitable for people who feel easily fatigued, have poor appetite, or experience loose stools — all signs that the Spleen Qi may need gentle support.

The Recipe: Chinese Yam and Goji Berry Soup

This recipe yields a light, slightly sweet broth that can be enjoyed on its own or as the base for a heartier meal. It is designed to be simple, forgiving, and adaptable — the kind of recipe you can make without precise measurements once you know the basic ratio.

Ingredients

Ingredient Amount Notes
Dried Chinese yam (huai shan) 30 g (about 10-12 slices) Available at Chinese herbal shops or online; look for large, pale slices
Dried goji berries 15 g (about 2 tbsp) Choose plump, deep-red berries without clumping
Red dates (hong zao) 3-4, sliced (optional) Adds gentle sweetness; also a spleen tonic in TCM
Fresh water 1 litre (4 cups) Filtered or spring water recommended
Rock sugar or honey To taste (optional) Only if you prefer a sweeter broth

Step-by-Step Instructions

  1. Rinse the ingredients. Briefly rinse the dried yam slices and goji berries under cool running water to remove any dust. Drain well.
  2. (Optional) Soak. If your dried yam is very hard, soak the slices in warm water for 20 minutes to rehydrate slightly. This shortens cooking time.
  3. Combine and simmer. Place the yam slices and red dates (if using) in a pot with 1 litre of water. Bring to a boil over medium-high heat, then reduce to a gentle simmer. Cover and cook for 30 minutes.
  4. Add the goji berries. Goji berries are delicate and can become bitter if over-cooked. Add them in the last 10 minutes of simmering.
  5. Season and serve. After 40-50 minutes total, the broth should be fragrant, slightly thickened, and pale golden. Taste and add a small amount of rock sugar or honey if desired. Serve warm.

Cooking Tips

  • Don't rush the simmer: The flavour develops slowly as the yam releases its natural starches and minerals into the water. A gentle, unhurried simmer yields the best results.
  • Use a thermal pot: For an even deeper flavour, bring the soup to a boil, then transfer to a thermal (vacuum-insulated) pot and let it steep for 2-3 hours. This method is traditional and energy-efficient.
  • Reheating: This soup reheats beautifully. Make a larger batch and store it in the refrigerator for up to 3 days.
  • For a savoury version: Replace the dried yam with 200 g fresh Chinese yam (peeled and cut into chunks) and simmer with pork bones or chicken pieces for 1.5 hours. Add goji berries in the last 10 minutes.

Variations to Explore

Once you are comfortable with the basic recipe, try these simple variations to address different needs or simply vary the flavour:

Vegan-Adaptable Option

The basic recipe above is already vegan. For a richer, more savoury vegan broth, add a few slices of fresh ginger and 2-3 dried shiitake mushrooms along with the yam. The mushrooms add umami depth without any animal products.

Add Red Dates (Hong Zao)

Red dates are themselves a gentle spleen tonic in TCM. Adding 3-4 sliced pitted dates to the simmering broth increases the sweetness naturally and provides additional Qi-supporting energy. This is the most common variation in traditional households.

Add Astragalus (Huang Qi)

For those who want to strengthen the body's defensive Qi (wei qi) — especially helpful during cold and flu season — add 10 g of sliced astragalus root alongside the yam. Astragalus has a mild, earthy flavour that blends well with the other ingredients. Remove the astragalus slices before serving, as they are woody and not meant to be eaten.

Lotus Seed and Lily Bulb Version

Add 15 g dried lotus seeds (lian zi) and 10 g dried lily bulb (bai he) for a version that is traditionally thought to calm the mind and support lung health. This variation is especially nice for evening consumption on cool autumn nights.

Best Time to Eat: Autumn and Beyond

In TCM, each season corresponds to an element and a pair of organ systems. Autumn belongs to the Metal element and the Lung and Large Intestine. During this season, the body's energy moves inward and downward — a natural time for consolidation, rest, and nourishment.

This yam and goji soup is particularly suited to early autumn (late September through November in the Northern Hemisphere), when the weather is still transitioning and the body benefits from gentle warming without overheating. It may be enjoyed 2-3 times per week as part of a balanced seasonal diet.

Beyond autumn, the soup is also traditionally recommended:

  • During convalescence — after illness, when the digestive system is weak and needs easy-to-assimilate nourishment.
  • In early spring — as a gentle liver and kidney tonic to support the body's transition into the season of Wood.
  • For daily maintenance — as a mild, balanced tonic that can be rotated into your weekly routine without overpowering other foods.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I use fresh Chinese yam instead of dried?

Yes. Fresh Chinese yam (often sold as nagaimo or Chinese yam root in Asian groceries) can be substituted. Use about 200 g fresh yam, peeled and cut into chunks. The cooking time will be shorter (about 20-25 minutes) and the flavour will be milder and more delicate. Note that fresh yam may cause mild skin irritation — wear gloves when peeling.

Is this soup safe for children?

In TCM tradition, both yam and goji berries are considered gentle and safe, and yam congee is often one of the first foods introduced to babies during weaning in Chinese culture. However, it is always advisable to consult a healthcare professional before introducing new foods or herbs to young children, and to start with small amounts. For young children, reduce goji berries to 5-8 berries per serving.

Can I drink this soup every day?

Yes, this soup is mild enough for daily consumption in moderate amounts (one bowl per day). In TCM, yam (shan yao) is classified as a superior herb — traditionally considered safe for long-term use. However, variety in the diet is always recommended, and those with specific health conditions should consult their practitioner.

What does the soup taste like?

The flavour is subtle, mildly sweet, and earthy with a hint of floral notes from the goji berries. The broth becomes slightly thicker and silkier as the yam releases its natural starches. It is not a strongly flavoured soup — think of it as a comforting, nourishing tea-like broth that pairs well with simple rice dishes or steamed vegetables.

Safety and Precautions

This recipe is intended as a nourishing food, not a medicinal treatment. The information provided is based on Traditional Chinese Medicine principles and should not be construed as medical advice. Please observe the following:

  • Consult a qualified healthcare provider before making dietary changes if you are pregnant, nursing, or managing a chronic condition.
  • Individuals with a known allergy to yam or goji berries should avoid this recipe.
  • Goji berries may interact with blood-thinning medications (such as warfarin). If you take prescription medications, speak with your doctor before consuming goji berries regularly.
  • If you experience any digestive discomfort or allergic reaction, discontinue use and consult a healthcare professional.
