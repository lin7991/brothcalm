Pumpkin and Red Date Congee — Warming the Spleen in Autumn

🥘 Recipe · 6 min read

The Autumn Bowl: Why This Congee Belongs on Your Table

As the heat of summer fades and the crisp air of autumn settles in, the body undergoes a subtle but profound transition. In Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM), this seasonal shift marks the movement from yang to yin — from outward expansion to inward gathering. The lungs take center stage as the organ of autumn, but the Spleen (which governs transformation and transportation in TCM theory) also deserves special attention during this time. A weakened Spleen can leave the body vulnerable to the dampness and coolness that autumn brings.

Pumpkin and red date congee — a velvety, gently sweet rice porridge — is one of the most cherished seasonal dishes in TCM food therapy. It is believed to warm the middle burner, strengthen Spleen Qi, and provide gentle nourishment as the weather turns cool. Made from humble, everyday ingredients, this congee has been enjoyed across generations in Chinese households as a comforting breakfast or a restorative evening meal.

TCM Properties of the Key Ingredients

Pumpkin (南瓜, nán guā)

In TCM, pumpkin is classified as sweet in flavor and warm in nature. It enters the Spleen and Stomach meridians. Traditionally, pumpkin is believed to support the Spleen's digestive function, gently warm the middle burner, and harmonise the Stomach. Its natural sweetness makes it particularly agreeable to the digestive system — in TCM theory, the sweet flavor corresponds to the Earth element and the Spleen/Pancreas network, which is why sweet (but not overly sugary) foods are often recommended for Spleen support.

Red Dates / Jujubes (红枣, hóng zǎo)

Red dates, or Chinese jujubes, are a cornerstone of TCM food therapy. They are sweet and warm, entering the Spleen and Stomach meridians, and are traditionally used to tonify Qi (vital energy) and nourish Blood. Red dates are often included in post-illness recovery formulas and warming soups because they are believed to restore vitality in a gentle, sustained way. They also harmonise the properties of other ingredients in a formula — a role that TCM texts describe as "harmonising the middle."

Rice (粳米, jīng mǐ)

The base of any congee, rice is neutral in nature, slightly sweet, and enters the Spleen and Stomach meridians. In TCM, well-cooked rice congee (known as 粥, zhōu) is considered one of the most easily digestible and nourishing foods available. A classic TCM saying holds that "rice congee is the best medicine for an ailing Spleen." Its neutral, harmonising quality makes it an ideal canvas for the more active properties of pumpkin and red dates.

Together, these three ingredients form a balanced formula: pumpkin warms and supports the Spleen, red dates tonify Qi and Blood, and rice harmonises and carries the nourishment deep into the middle burner.

Traditional Uses and Classical References

Red dates have been documented in Chinese medicine since at least the Shen Nong Ben Cao Jing (《神农本草经》), the oldest extant Chinese pharmacopoeia, composed around 200–300 CE. That classical text classifies red dates as a "superior" herb — one that is safe for long-term consumption and supports overall health. It describes red dates as "sweet in flavor, warm in nature … chiefly for calming the middle burner, nourishing the Spleen, and boosting Qi."

Pumpkin, while not as ancient in the Chinese pharmacopoeia as red dates (it arrived in China from the Americas via the Columbian Exchange), has been integrated into TCM food therapy for centuries. Its warm, sweet nature makes it a natural fit for autumn eating, when the body needs gentle warmth to counter the descending coolness of the season.

Autumn Eating in TCM: The Earth Phase

In the Five Element (五行, wǔ xíng) framework of TCM, each season is associated with an element and a pair of organ systems. Autumn corresponds to the Metal element and the Lung/Large Intestine network. However, the Spleen (associated with the Earth element) plays a critical supporting role during seasonal transitions. The Earth element is said to "generate" Metal — meaning a well-nourished Spleen provides the foundation for strong Lung Qi.

This is why autumn is considered an ideal time for Spleen-nourishing foods. The Spleen's ability to transform food into Qi and Blood is believed to be most efficient when supported with warm, cooked, sweet foods. Raw, cold, or greasy foods, by contrast, are traditionally discouraged in autumn because they are thought to burden the Spleen at a time when it is already working to support the transition toward yin.

Complete Recipe: Pumpkin and Red Date Congee

This recipe yields approximately 4 servings and can be prepared in about 50 minutes. The congee should be thick, creamy, and naturally sweet — no sugar necessary if your pumpkin and dates are of good quality.

Ingredients

  • 1 cup (150 g) short-grain white rice — jasmine or sushi rice work well
  • 300 g pumpkin (about 2 cups, diced) — kabocha or sugar pumpkin preferred
  • 8–10 red dates (jujubes), pitted and halved
  • 6 cups (1.4 L) water or a mild vegetable broth
  • Optional: 1 small knob of ginger, thinly sliced (3–4 slices)
  • Optional: 1–2 tbsp rock sugar or honey, to taste

Step-by-Step Instructions

  1. Wash the rice — Rinse the rice in cold water until the water runs clear. Drain and set aside.
  2. Prepare the pumpkin — Peel the pumpkin, remove the seeds and fibrous centre, and cut the flesh into 1-cm cubes. Smaller cubes break down faster and create a creamier texture.
  3. Soften the red dates — If using dried red dates, soak them in warm water for 10 minutes, then drain, pit, and halve each date.
  4. Combine and cook — In a large heavy-bottomed pot, combine the rice, pumpkin, red dates, and water or broth. Add ginger slices if using. Bring to a boil over medium-high heat, then reduce to a gentle simmer.
  5. Simmer low and slow — Cover partially and simmer for 35–45 minutes, stirring occasionally to prevent sticking. The congee is ready when the rice grains have fully broken down and the porridge has a thick, creamy consistency. If it becomes too thick, add more hot water a ladleful at a time.
  6. Sweeten (optional) — Stir in rock sugar or honey to taste, if desired. Serve warm.

Tips for Best Results

  • Use a heavy-bottomed pot or a clay pot (砂锅, shā guō) for even, gentle heat — this helps the rice release its starches and creates the signature creamy congee texture.
  • Stir frequently in the last 15 minutes of cooking. This is when the congee thickens fastest and is most prone to sticking.
  • If you prefer a thinner congee (稀粥), use 7–8 cups of water. For a very thick, porridge-like consistency (稠粥), use 5 cups.
  • Leftover congee thickens significantly when refrigerated. Thin it with water or broth when reheating.

Variations

With Ginger (姜枣南瓜粥)

Adding 3–4 thin slices of fresh ginger to the pot amplifies the warming effect. Ginger is believed in TCM to "warm the middle and dispel cold," making this variation particularly well-suited for the first truly chilly days of autumn or for anyone who feels cold easily.

With Astragalus (黄芪南瓜粥)

Add 15 g of dried astragalus root (黄芪, huáng qí) to the cooking water at the beginning. Simmer for 10 minutes before adding the rice and other ingredients, then remove the astragalus slices before serving. This variation is traditionally believed to deepen the Qi-tonifying effect and support the body's defensive energy (wei qi), which is particularly relevant as the cold and flu season approaches.

With Goji Berries (枸杞南瓜粥)

Add 1 tablespoon of goji berries (枸杞, gǒu qǐ) in the last 5 minutes of cooking. Goji berries are sweet and neutral, entering the Liver and Kidney meridians. They are traditionally used in TCM to nourish Liver and Kidney Yin and support the eyes. This variation adds a subtle tart-sweet contrast to the congee and pairs beautifully with the pumpkin's natural sweetness.

Who Benefits Most from This Congee

Pumpkin and red date congee is especially well-suited for people who experience signs of Spleen Qi deficiency. In TCM, Spleen Qi deficiency may manifest as:

  • Fatigue, especially after eating
  • Loose stools or a tendency toward bloating
  • Weak appetite or digestive sluggishness
  • A pale complexion
  • Feeling cold in the hands and feet
  • Difficulty concentrating

This congee is also recommended for those transitioning into cooler weather — its warm, dense nourishment is believed to help the body adjust from the expansive, yang energy of summer to the more contained, yin energy of autumn. Additionally, it may be beneficial during post-illness recovery, when the digestive system is often too weak to handle complex or heavy foods but still needs concentrated nourishment. The gentle nature of congee makes it one of the first foods traditionally reintroduced after illness.

Precautions and Considerations

While pumpkin and red date congee is generally very safe, a few groups may wish to approach it thoughtfully.

Spleen deficiency with dampness. In TCM theory, "dampness" refers to a pattern characterised by a heavy feeling in the body, excess phlegm, a sticky taste in the mouth, or a sense of sluggishness. For those with signs of dampness, the sweet, nourishing nature of this congee may potentially contribute to the feeling of heaviness. Adding a few slices of ginger or a small piece of dried tangerine peel (陈皮, chén pí) can help offset this by supporting Qi circulation and transforming dampness.

Blood sugar considerations. Both pumpkin and red dates contain natural sugars. While the glycemic impact of a whole-food congee is generally gentler than refined sugar, those managing diabetes or blood sugar concerns may wish to consume this dish in smaller portions, reduce or omit the rock sugar, and pair it with a source of protein or healthy fat (such as a handful of nuts or a poached egg on the side) to slow glucose absorption.

Excess heat signs. Individuals who tend toward "excess heat" patterns — marked by a red complexion, dry mouth, irritability, or constipation — may find that too much of this warming congee amplifies these sensations. For such individuals, reducing the number of red dates to 4–5 and adding a handful of mung beans or a splash of pear juice can help balance the warmth.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I eat pumpkin and red date congee every day in autumn?
Yes, traditionally in TCM this congee is mild enough for daily consumption during autumn for most people. A small bowl a few times per week is a gentle way to support Spleen Qi during the seasonal transition. Those with blood sugar concerns or signs of dampness may wish to enjoy it less frequently.

What makes pumpkin and red dates a good pair in TCM food therapy?
Both pumpkin and red dates are sweet and warm in nature, and both enter the Spleen and Stomach meridians. In TCM theory, their complementary properties work together to strengthen the middle burner — pumpkin gently supports Spleen Qi while red dates tonify Qi and nourish Blood. Rice adds a harmonizing, neutral base, creating a complete and balanced porridge.

Is pumpkin and red date congee suitable for children?
This congee is generally considered suitable for children in TCM food therapy, as it is mild, warming, and easy to digest. The natural sweetness from pumpkin and red dates makes it appealing for young eaters. Reduce or omit any added rock sugar and ensure the congee is well-cooked to a soft, smooth texture. As always, consult a healthcare provider when introducing new foods to your child's diet.

Can I add other ingredients to this congee for different benefits?
Absolutely. In TCM food therapy, congee is an excellent base for building custom remedies. Ginger slices add warming yang energy and help settle the stomach. Astragalus (huang qi) deepens the Qi-tonifying effect. Goji berries add Blood-nourishing and liver-supporting properties. A small piece of dried tangerine peel (chen pi) can help regulate Qi if you experience bloating.
