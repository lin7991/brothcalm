Herbal Chicken Soup (药膳鸡汤) — Nourish Your Qi

🥘 Recipe · 8 min read

In Traditional Chinese Medicine, a bowl of herbal chicken soup (药膳鸡汤) is much more than comfort food — it is a carefully balanced formula designed to tonify Qi (vital energy), nourish Blood, and strengthen the body's defenses. For centuries, this golden broth has been the go-to remedy for recovery, postpartum care, and seasonal wellness.

Made by slowly simmering a whole chicken with medicinal herbs like astragalus (huáng qí), jujube dates (hóng zǎo), ginger (shēng jiāng), and goji berries (gǒu qǐ), this soup is a perfect example of the TCM principle that food and medicine share the same origin — yào shí tóng yuán.

Why Chicken in TCM?

In TCM, chicken (jī) is classified as warm in nature and sweet in flavor. It enters the Spleen, Stomach, and Kidney meridians. Traditionally, it is considered a superior Qi and Blood tonic — ideal for people who feel weak, fatigued, pale, or are recovering from illness.

A saying in TCM goes: "A hen that has lived for several years is the most tonifying." Older chickens (called lǎo mǔ jī) are believed to have deeper nourishing qualities and are preferred for medicinal soups.

Complete Recipe: Herbal Chicken Soup (药膳鸡汤)

Ingredients

Serves 4–6

1 whole chicken (3–4 lbs / 1.5–2 kg), preferably an old hen
30g astragalus root (huáng qí)
10–12 jujube dates (hóng zǎo), pitted
15g goji berries (gǒu qǐ)
5 slices fresh ginger (about the size of a quarter)
10g dang gui / angelica root (dāng guī) — optional, skip if pregnant
2–3 dried shiitake mushrooms — optional
Sea salt, to taste
10 cups (2.5 L) filtered water

Step-by-Step Instructions

  1. Prep the chicken: Remove the skin if you prefer a lighter broth. Rinse the chicken inside and out with cold water. Cut into large pieces.
  2. Blanch (optional but recommended): Place the chicken in a large pot, cover with cold water, and bring to a boil. Boil for 3 minutes, then drain and rinse. This removes impurities and results in a clearer broth.
  3. Combine: Place the blanched chicken, astragalus, jujube, ginger, and shiitake (if using) in a large soup pot. Add the filtered water.
  4. Simmer: Bring to a boil over high heat, then reduce to the lowest simmer. Cover and cook for 2–3 hours (stovetop) or 4–6 hours (slow cooker on low).
  5. Add goji and dang gui: In the last 30 minutes, add the goji berries and dang gui (if using). Goji berries are delicate and overcooking can make them sour.
  6. Season: Remove the astragalus root slices (they are woody and not meant to be eaten). Add salt to taste.
  7. Serve: Ladle into bowls with pieces of chicken meat. Garnish with fresh scallions if desired.
  8. Store: Keeps for 3–4 days in the refrigerator. Flavor deepens overnight.

When to Drink This Soup

🥶 Cold Season & Early Signs of Illness

At the first sign of chills, fatigue, or feeling "under the weather," a bowl of this soup can help expel Wind-Cold and strengthen the body's defensive Qi (wèi qì). The warming nature of chicken and ginger combined with astragalus makes it a gentle but powerful immune-supporting meal.

🛌 Recovery & Convalescence

After illness, surgery, or prolonged physical exertion, the body's Qi and Blood are typically depleted. This soup provides easily digestible nourishment that the body can absorb without spending too much energy on digestion.

🤱 Postpartum Care

In Chinese tradition, new mothers are given herbal chicken soup (often with dang gui and goji) for the first 30 days after childbirth. This practice, known as zuò yuè zi (sitting the month), restores Blood and Qi lost during delivery and supports lactation.

TCM Perspective: Why It Works

  • Chicken — warms the middle Jiao, tonifies Qi and Blood
  • Astragalus (huáng qí) — strengthens Wei Qi, lifts Spleen Qi
  • Jujube (hóng zǎo) — nourishes Blood, harmonizes the formula
  • Ginger (shēng jiāng) — warms the Stomach, helps digestion
  • Goji (gǒu qǐ) — nourishes Liver and Kidney Yin, supports vision
  • Dang gui (dāng guī) — the classic Blood-tonifying herb

Safety & Precautions

Skip dang gui (angelica root) during pregnancy, as it may stimulate uterine contractions. If you are taking blood-thinning medication, consult your healthcare provider before consuming large amounts of dang gui or astragalus. This soup is generally safe for most people in moderate amounts.
