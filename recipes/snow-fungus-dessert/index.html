Snow Fungus and Goji Berry Dessert Soup — The TCM Beauty Tonic

🥘 Recipe · 7 min read

Snow fungus and goji berry dessert soup is one of Traditional Chinese Medicine's most beloved beauty tonics — a silky, gently sweet soup that has been cherished for centuries for its remarkable ability to nourish Yin, hydrate the skin, and support lung health. Known in Chinese as bái mù ěr (银耳), snow fungus is prized for its natural collagen-like texture and its ability to absorb the flavours of its companion ingredients while contributing a wealth of its own therapeutic benefits.

In this article, we explore what makes snow fungus such a treasured ingredient in TCM food therapy, why it pairs so perfectly with goji berries and red dates, and — most importantly — how to prepare this exquisite dessert soup in your own kitchen.

What Is Snow Fungus (Tremella fuciformis)?

Snow fungus, also called silver ear mushroom or white jelly fungus, is a gelatinous fungus that grows on the bark of broadleaf trees in tropical and subtropical regions. When dried, it appears as an unassuming cluster of pale yellow, ear-shaped fronds. But once rehydrated and simmered, it transforms into something truly remarkable — a translucent, jelly-like substance that expands many times its dry volume, creating a soup with a luscious, almost velvety mouthfeel.

Scientifically classified as Tremella fuciformis, snow fungus is rich in dietary fibre, amino acids, and a type of polysaccharide known as tremellan or glucuronoxylomannan. Modern research has shown these compounds to have antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and immune-modulating properties. In the culinary traditions of China, Japan, and Southeast Asia, snow fungus has been used for centuries not only as a food ingredient but also as a medicinal mushroom.

Snow Fungus in Traditional Chinese Medicine

In the framework of Traditional Chinese Medicine, snow fungus is classified as having a sweet flavour and a neutral temperature. It is primarily associated with the Lung and Stomach meridians. Its core functions are to:

  • Nourish Yin — Snow fungus is one of the most effective Yin-tonifying foods in the TCM pantry. Yin deficiency, which tends to increase with age or prolonged stress, manifests as dryness — dry skin, dry eyes, a dry cough, and thirst. By replenishing Yin fluids, snow fungus helps the body maintain moisture and lubrication from within.
  • Moisten the Lungs — The Lung is the organ most associated with the skin in TCM, and dry environmental conditions (such as autumn winds or air-conditioned rooms) can deplete Lung fluids. Snow fungus helps moisten the Lungs, which in turn supports healthy, glowing skin.
  • Strengthen the Stomach and generate fluids — Its neutral, moistening nature makes it suitable for almost anyone, even those with sensitive digestion. It helps generate body fluids (Jin Ye), which are essential for overall hydration and metabolic function.

Traditionally, snow fungus has also been used to support those recovering from chronic illness or experiencing fatigue, as its gentle, building nature helps restore the body's fundamental fluids without creating stagnation or heat.

Why Goji Berries? The Perfect Pair

Goji berries (gǒu qǐ zi, 枸杞子) are one of the most widely used herbs in TCM. They are classified as sweet and neutral, entering the Liver and Kidney meridians. Their function is to nourish Liver and Kidney Yin, brighten the eyes, and — importantly for our dessert soup — support blood health.

When combined with snow fungus, goji berries create a synergistic tonic:

  • Snow fungus focuses on the Lungs and overall Yin moisture, while goji berries nourish the Liver and Kidneys — together they address the three organs most involved in skin, hair, and vitality.
  • Goji berries add a gentle tart-sweetness that perfectly balances the neutral, earthy snow fungus, creating a flavour profile that feels both nourishing and indulgent.
  • The deep red of the goji berries against the translucent white of the snow fungus makes for a visually stunning dish — beautiful food that feels as good as it looks.

Many recipes also include red dates (hóng zǎo, 红枣), which nourish the blood and calm the spirit, and dried longan (lóng yǎn ròu, 龙眼肉) for additional sweetness and heart-nourishing properties. These optional additions enhance the tonic effect of the soup, making it even more comprehensive as a daily beauty food.

Snow Fungus and Goji Berry Dessert Soup Recipe

This recipe serves 4 and takes approximately 2 hours from start to finish (mostly hands-off simmering time).

Ingredients

  • 15 g (½ oz) dried snow fungus (Tremella fuciformis) — look for pale yellow, ear-shaped fronds with a mild mushroom scent
  • 30 g (1 oz) goji berries — about 3 tablespoons
  • 6-8 red dates (jujubes) — pit removed, sliced open
  • 15 g (½ oz) dried longan (optional) — for extra sweetness and heart nourishment
  • 30 g (1 oz) rock sugar — or to taste. Traditional Chinese yellow rock sugar (bing tang) is ideal, but light brown sugar or a small amount of honey (added after cooking) also works
  • 1.5 litres (6 cups) filtered water
  • ½ teaspoon dried tangerine peel (chén pí, optional) — adds a subtle citrus note and helps regulate Qi

Step-by-Step Instructions

Step 1: Rehydrate the snow fungus. Place the dried snow fungus in a large bowl and cover generously with cold water. Soak for 30–60 minutes, or until the fronds have softened and expanded significantly. A well-rehydrated snow fungus should feel pliable and gelatinous to the touch, with no hard or brittle spots remaining.

Step 2: Clean and prepare. Drain the rehydrated snow fungus and rinse gently under cold running water. Trim off the hard, dark yellow base where the fronds were attached to the tree — this part remains tough even after long cooking. Tear the fungus into bite-sized florets (about 3–5 cm pieces). Smaller pieces yield more surface area for the gelatinous texture to develop, while larger pieces retain a pleasant chewiness.

Step 3: Initial simmer. Place the prepared snow fungus in a large soup pot or clay pot with 1.5 litres of filtered water. Bring to a boil over medium-high heat, then reduce to a gentle simmer. Cover and cook for 45 minutes to 1 hour. During this time, the snow fungus will release its natural polysaccharides, and the water will gradually turn slightly viscous and translucent.

Step 4: Add red dates and longan. After the initial simmer, add the sliced red dates and dried longan (if using). Continue to simmer gently, covered, for another 20–25 minutes. The red dates will soften and release their natural sweetness into the broth, and the soup will deepen in colour to a warm, golden-amber hue.

Step 5: Add goji berries and rock sugar. Add the goji berries and rock sugar. Simmer for a final 10 minutes only — goji berries should not be boiled for too long, as their delicate nutrients and flavour are best preserved with minimal cooking. (If using dried tangerine peel, add it along with the goji berries for a subtle aromatic lift.)

Step 6: Adjust and serve. Taste the soup and adjust sweetness to your preference. Remove from heat. The soup can be served warm immediately, or allowed to cool and served chilled. The gelatinous texture will continue to develop as it cools.

Step 7: (Optional) Enhance for extra collagen. For an even more luxurious texture, you can use a hand blender to lightly purée about one-third of the soup before serving. This releases additional polysaccharides and creates a thicker, more velvety consistency without the need for any thickening agents.

Beauty and Skin Benefits in TCM

The connection between snow fungus and beautiful skin is not merely folk wisdom — it is rooted in the fundamental TCM principle that internal moisture reflects external radiance.

In TCM, the Lung governs the skin and body hair. When Lung Yin is sufficient and Lung fluids are abundant, the skin appears supple, hydrated, and luminous. When Lung Yin is depleted — due to dry environments, aging, stress, or insufficient rest — the skin may become dry, dull, or prone to fine lines.

By nourishing Lung Yin, snow fungus helps maintain the skin's moisture barrier from the inside out. Its natural polysaccharides are believed to support the body's own hyaluronic acid production, helping the skin hold onto moisture more effectively.

Goji berries complement this by nourishing the Blood, which in TCM is said to "moisten the skin." Healthy, abundant blood gives the complexion a rosy, lively glow — the difference between dullness and vibrancy. Together, snow fungus and goji berries address both moisture (Yin) and vitality (Blood), two pillars of youthful, healthy-looking skin.

Regular consumption of this dessert soup is traditionally recommended for those seeking to support skin elasticity, reduce the appearance of fine lines, and maintain a dewy, well-hydrated complexion — especially during dry seasons or in air-conditioned environments.

When to Eat and Who Benefits Most

Snow fungus and goji berry dessert soup is suitable for all ages and constitutions, but it is particularly helpful for:

  • Those with Yin deficiency — signs include dry skin, dry eyes, dry cough, night sweats, restlessness, and a thin pulse
  • Women seeking beauty nourishment — especially during or after menstruation, when Yin and Blood are temporarily depleted
  • People living in dry climates — or those who spend long hours in air-conditioned rooms, which accelerate fluid loss
  • Individuals recovering from illness — the gentle, nourishing nature of the soup helps rebuild the body's fundamental fluids without taxing the digestive system
  • Older adults — as Yin naturally declines with age, this soup provides gentle, ongoing support for hydration and skin health

Best times to enjoy: The soup is wonderful year-round but is especially recommended during autumn and winter, when the environment is driest and the Lungs are most susceptible to dryness according to TCM seasonal theory. It can be enjoyed as a mid-morning snack, an afternoon dessert, or a light evening treat. Avoid consuming it too close to bedtime if you are sensitive to sugar.

When to use caution: While snow fungus is generally very safe, those with a tendency toward coldness or phlegm-dampness (symptoms: a feeling of heaviness, loose stools, a swollen tongue with a thick coating) should go easy on the sweetener and may wish to add 2–3 slices of fresh ginger to the simmer to offset the cooling, moistening nature of the soup. As always, consult a qualified TCM practitioner for personalised advice.

Tips for the Perfect Bowl

  • Don't skip the soak. Proper rehydration is the most critical step. A partially rehydrated snow fungus will never achieve that silky, gelatinous texture, no matter how long you simmer it.
  • Use a clay pot if you have one. Clay or earthenware pots distribute heat evenly and enhance the gentle, slow extraction of the snow fungus's polysaccharides, resulting in a more luxurious texture than metal pots can achieve.
  • Rock sugar matters. Traditional Chinese yellow rock sugar (bing tang) has a subtle, caramel-like depth that white sugar lacks. It dissolves cleanly without clouding the broth. If you cannot find it, unrefined coconut sugar or a touch of maple syrup (added after cooking) makes a fine substitute.
  • Make it in advance. Snow fungus dessert soup tastes even better the next day, as the flavours continue to meld and the texture becomes even more gelatinous. Store it in the refrigerator in a sealed container for up to 4 days.
  • Get creative with additions. In different regions of China, this soup is customised with lotus seeds (for calming the spirit), lily bulb (for additional Lung moisture), or fresh pear slices (for extra Yin-nourishing power). Feel free to experiment within the TCM framework.
