Mung Bean Porridge — Cooling Summer Congee for Heat Relief

🥘 Recipe · 6 min read

A Bowl of Cool Comfort for Hot Days

On the hottest days of summer, when the sun beats down and your body feels heavy with heat, there is no food more welcome in the Chinese kitchen than a bowl of mung bean porridge (绿豆粥 / lǜ dòu zhōu). This humble, jade-green congee has been a staple of summer wellness for centuries — not simply because it tastes good, but because it works with your body's natural rhythms in ways that modern nutrition is only beginning to appreciate.

In Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM), mung bean porridge is classified as a cooling, or "cold-natured," food. It is one of the most trusted dietary remedies for summer-heat (暑热 / shǔ rè), that particular kind of sluggish, irritable, thirst-heavy discomfort that comes from prolonged exposure to high temperatures. Whether you are suffering from mild heat exhaustion, feeling feverish, or simply looking for a light, nourishing meal that won't weigh you down, mung bean porridge offers a time-honoured solution.

TCM Properties of Mung Beans

In the TCM framework, every food carries a thermal nature (气 / qì), a flavour profile, and a meridian affinity. Mung beans (Vigna radiata) are classified as follows:

  • Thermal nature: Cold (凉 / liáng) — the most cooling of the bean family
  • Flavour: Sweet (甘 / gān)
  • Meridian entry: Heart (心 / xīn) and Stomach (胃 / wèi)
  • Key actions: Clears summer-heat, drains dampness (利湿 / lì shī), detoxifies (解毒 / jiě dú)

The cold nature of mung beans makes them especially suited for heat-related conditions — including thirst that cannot be quenched, dark and scanty urine, irritability, restlessness, red complexion, and a sensation of heat in the body. By entering the Heart meridian, mung beans can also calm the spirit and ease the agitation that often accompanies summer heat. Their sweet flavour, meanwhile, harmonises with the Spleen and Stomach, making them easy to digest — when cooked properly.

Mung beans are also well known in TCM for their detoxifying properties. They have traditionally been used to help the body clear toxins, reduce inflammation, and support the skin during hot weather flare-ups. Modern research has confirmed that mung beans are rich in antioxidants, flavonoids, and polyphenols that support these traditional uses.

Traditional Uses Through History

Mung beans have a long and respected history in Chinese medicine. They are mentioned in the Ben Cao Gang Mu (《本草纲目》 / Compendium of Materia Medica), the monumental 16th-century pharmacopoeia compiled by the great physician Li Shizhen. In this classic text, mung beans are described as a food that "cools the blood, dispels summer-heat, and removes toxins." Li Shizhen recommended them specifically for conditions arising from summer heat and for food poisoning.

Throughout Chinese history, mung bean porridge has been used for:

  • Heat stroke (中暑 / zhòng shǔ) — mild cases with symptoms of dizziness, fatigue, and excessive thirst
  • Summer fevers — when the body feels hot but there is no identifiable infection
  • Thirst and dry mouth — especially when accompanied by a desire for cold drinks
  • Skin eruptions — such as heat rash, acne, and boils during hot weather
  • Damp-heat digestive issues — including feeling heavy, bloated, and lethargic

In folk tradition, mung bean soup (the cooking water alone, without the beans) was often given to children and the elderly during heatwaves as a gentle, hydrating tonic.

Complete Recipe: Mung Bean Porridge (绿豆粥)

This recipe yields a smooth, lightly sweet porridge that can be served warm or at room temperature — ideal for summer mornings or as a light evening meal.

Ingredients

  • ½ cup (100 g) mung beans (绿豆 / lǜ dòu)
  • ¼ cup (50 g) jasmine or short-grain rice (大米 / dà mǐ)
  • 6 cups (1.4 L) water
  • 1–2 tablespoons rock sugar (冰糖 / bīng táng) or to taste
  • Optional: 2 tablespoons lotus seeds (莲子 / lián zǐ), pre-soaked

Step-by-Step Instructions

  1. Rinse and soak the mung beans. Place the mung beans in a bowl and cover with water. Swish them around with your fingers, then drain. Repeat until the water runs clear. Soak the rinsed beans in fresh water for at least 2 hours — or overnight if you can. Soaking softens the beans and reduces cooking time.
  2. Rinse the rice. Wash the rice in cold water until the water runs clear. Drain well. If using lotus seeds, soak them alongside the mung beans.
  3. Combine in a pot. Add the soaked mung beans, rice, lotus seeds (if using), and 6 cups of water to a heavy-bottomed pot or slow cooker. Bring to a boil over high heat.
  4. Simmer gently. Once boiling, reduce the heat to low, cover the pot with the lid slightly ajar (leave a small gap to prevent overflowing), and let it simmer for 45–60 minutes. Stir occasionally to prevent the grains from sticking to the bottom.
  5. Check for doneness. The porridge is ready when the mung beans have burst open and become soft and creamy, and the rice has broken down into a smooth congee consistency. The liquid should be slightly thickened and faintly green-tinged.
  6. Sweeten and serve. Stir in rock sugar until dissolved. Taste and adjust sweetness. Serve warm, at room temperature, or even slightly chilled — though avoid ice-cold servings, as TCM advises against extreme cold for the digestive system.

Cooking Tips

  • Don't overcook the colour. For the most vibrant and visually appealing porridge, avoid boiling the mung beans at high heat for too long. A gentle simmer preserves the delicate green hue.
  • Thicker or thinner? For a thicker porridge, reduce water to 5 cups. For a thinner, soup-like consistency that is especially hydrating, use 7 cups.
  • Rice or no rice? Some traditional recipes omit the rice and make a straight mung bean soup (绿豆汤 / lǜ dòu tāng). Adding rice makes it a proper congee that is more filling and gentler on the stomach.
  • Salt option. While the classic preparation is sweet, a savoury version is possible — omit the sugar and top with a pinch of salt, sesame oil, and chopped spring onions.

Delicious Variations

Mung Bean and Winter Melon Porridge

For especially hot and humid days, add 200 g of diced winter melon (冬瓜 / dōng guā) to the pot along with the beans and rice. Winter melon is even more cooling than mung beans and is traditionally used to promote urination and drain dampness. The result is a deeply hydrating, refreshing porridge that is believed to help with water retention and summer bloating.

Mung Bean and Coix Seed Porridge

For those dealing with dampness (湿 / shī) — symptoms like heavy limbs, a feeling of fullness, cloudy urine, or sticky stools — replace half the rice with coix seed (薏苡仁 / yì yǐ rén, also known as Job's tears). Coix seed is one of TCM's primary dampness-draining ingredients, and combined with mung bean's cooling action, it makes a powerful summer remedy for the damp-heat pattern.

Mung Bean and Lily Bulb Porridge

Add a handful of dried lily bulb (百合 / bǎi hé) during the last 15 minutes of cooking. Lily bulb is sweet and cooling, enters the Heart and Lung meridians, and is traditionally used to calm the mind and soothe a dry cough. This version is especially good for those who feel irritable, restless, or unable to sleep during hot summer nights.

Precautions and Who Should Be Cautious

Mung bean porridge is a therapeutic food, and like all therapeutic foods, it is not suitable for everyone in every season. The following individuals should approach it with caution:

  • Spleen deficiency cold pattern (脾虚寒 / pí xū hán). If you tend to have cold hands and feet, loose or watery stools, poor appetite, a pale complexion, and a feeling of coldness in the abdomen, mung bean porridge may be too cooling for you. The cold nature of the beans can further weaken an already deficient Spleen.
  • Pregnancy with cold symptoms. While small amounts are generally safe, pregnant women with a cold constitution should consume mung bean porridge only occasionally and in modest quantities.
  • During illness with chills. If you have an illness characterised by chills, body aches, and aversion to cold (rather than heat), mung bean porridge is not recommended.
  • Avoid overconsumption in cooler months. Mung bean porridge is a seasonal food best enjoyed during late spring, summer, and early autumn. Eating it regularly in winter may disturb the body's internal warmth.

If you are unsure whether mung bean porridge suits your constitution, start with a small bowl and observe how your body responds. A traditional TCM trick: add 2–3 slices of fresh ginger (生姜 / shēng jiāng) while cooking. Ginger is warming and counteracts the cold nature of the beans, making the dish more balanced for sensitive digestive systems.

Final Thoughts

Mung bean porridge is a beautiful example of how TCM food therapy works — using the natural properties of everyday ingredients to support the body through the changing seasons. It is simple to prepare, deeply satisfying, and carries generations of wisdom in every spoonful. The next time summer heat leaves you feeling drained and irritable, let this cooling green congee restore your equilibrium, the way it has done for countless people before you.

As the old Chinese saying goes: "One bowl of green bean soup in summer keeps the doctor away."
