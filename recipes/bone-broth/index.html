Bone Broth — The Collagen-Rich Restorative

📖 Recipe 🕐 7 min read

Few preparations bridge the gap between ancestral kitchen wisdom and modern nutritional science as elegantly as bone broth. What was once simply "stock" simmered from leftover bones has re-emerged as a cornerstone of the healing-diet movement — and for good reason. Long-simmered bone broth delivers a dense payload of collagen, gelatin, glycine, proline, and a spectrum of minerals that support joint health, gut integrity, skin elasticity, and immune function.

In Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM), bone broth holds an even more exalted position: it is understood as a direct tonic for Kidney Essence (Jing) — the body's deepest reservoir of vitality, longevity, and constitutional strength. This article explores bone broth through both a nutritional and a TCM lens, with a complete recipe and practical guidance for making your own at home.

Bone Broth Through the Lens of TCM

TCM Principle: In Traditional Chinese Medicine, the Kidneys store Jing (精) — the fundamental essence that governs growth, development, reproduction, and aging. Jing is our deepest constitutional reserve, and it is said to be housed in the bones and manifested in the teeth, hair, and marrow.

The connection between bones and kidneys in TCM is not metaphorical — it is physiological. The Suwen (Plain Questions), one of the foundational texts of Chinese medicine, states plainly: "The kidneys govern the bones, produce marrow, and open into the ears." When Kidney Essence is abundant, the bones are strong, the marrow is full, the teeth are firm, and the hair is lustrous. When Jing is depleted — through overwork, chronic illness, aging, or stress — the bones can become brittle, joints may ache, and recovery slows.

Bone broth, simmered slowly from marrow-rich animal bones, is considered one of the most direct foods for nourishing Kidney Jing. The logic is elegant: the very substance that gives structure to the bones of the animal becomes a medicinal food that supports the structural integrity of the person who consumes it. The marrow itself is seen as the richest part — a direct analogue to the marrow that the Kidneys are said to produce.

Why Collagen Matters in TCM Terms

The collagen and gelatin that leach from bones, joints, and connective tissue during a long simmer correspond, in TCM thinking, to substances that build Yin (the cooling, moistening, structural aspect of the body). Many individuals who would benefit from bone broth present with signs of Yin or Jing deficiency: dry skin, brittle nails, creaky joints, low back ache, tinnitus, insomnia, and fatigue.

Because bone broth is warming in nature but deeply nourishing to fluids, it is considered suitable for a wide range of constitutional types — especially when balanced with neutral or cooling adjuncts such as ginger, carrot, or celery. The addition of tonic herbs like astragalus (Huang Qi) and goji berries (Gou Qi Zi) moves the broth squarely into the realm of medicinal food, amplifying its ability to build Qi (vital energy) alongside Jing.

Joint Health, Gut Healing & Beyond

Modern research corroborates what TCM has described for millennia. The collagen in bone broth breaks down into gelatin, which is rich in the amino acids glycine and proline. Glycine plays a critical role in collagen synthesis (the body's own repair of connective tissue), supports detoxification pathways in the liver, and acts as an inhibitory neurotransmitter that promotes restful sleep.

For joint health, the glycosaminoglycans (GAGs) found in connective tissue — glucosamine, chondroitin, and hyaluronic acid — enter the broth during a long simmer. These compounds are the building blocks of articular cartilage and synovial fluid. Regular consumption has been associated with reduced joint pain, improved mobility, and decreased markers of inflammation in osteoarthritis.

For gut healing, the gelatin in bone broth supports the mucosal lining of the digestive tract. It binds to water, helping maintain the gel-like matrix that protects the gut wall, and glycine has been shown to reduce intestinal inflammation. This makes bone broth a staple of elimination diets and protocols aimed at repairing leaky gut syndrome.

🍲 The Complete Bone Broth Recipe

Beef or chicken · yields ~3 litres · hands-on time 20 min · total time 12–48 hours

Ingredients

  • 1.3–1.8 kg beef bones (marrow bones, knuckles, oxtail) or 1 whole chicken carcass + 500 g chicken feet
  • 2 carrots, roughly chopped
  • 2 celery stalks, roughly chopped
  • 1 large onion, quartered (skin on for colour)
  • 1 head garlic, halved crosswise
  • 5 cm piece fresh ginger, sliced into coins
  • 2 tbsp apple cider vinegar (or any vinegar) — essential for mineral extraction
  • 2 bay leaves
  • 1 tbsp black peppercorns
  • Cold filtered water — enough to cover bones by 5 cm

Optional TCM additions: 30 g astragalus root (Huang Qi), 30 g goji berries (Gou Qi Zi), 3 slices dried rehmannia (Shu Di Huang)

Method

  1. Roast the bones (optional, beef only). If using beef bones, arrange them on a baking tray and roast at 200 °C (400 °F) for 30 minutes until deeply browned. This adds colour and a richer, roastier flavour. For chicken, skip this step.
  2. Place bones in a pot. Transfer the bones to a large stockpot (8–10 litre capacity) or a slow cooker. Add the carrots, celery, onion, garlic, ginger, bay leaves, and peppercorns.
  3. Add vinegar and water. Pour in the vinegar and enough cold filtered water to cover the bones by about 5 cm. The vinegar is not for flavour — the acid helps draw calcium, magnesium, phosphorus, and other minerals out of the bones. Let the pot sit for 30 minutes before heating.
  4. Bring to a gentle boil, then reduce. Bring the pot to a gentle boil over medium-high heat. As soon as it reaches a boil, reduce the heat to the lowest simmer possible — just an occasional bubble breaking the surface. Never boil vigorously; it emulsifies the fat and makes the broth greasy and cloudy.
  5. Skim the scum. In the first 30–60 minutes, a greyish foam (the "scum") will rise to the surface. This is coagulated protein and impurities. Skim it off with a fine-mesh skimmer or a ladle. Do not skip this step — leaving scum on yields a murky, off-flavoured broth. One good skim is usually enough; a second pass at 90 minutes catches any stragglers.
  6. Add optional herbs (last 2 hours). If using astragalus or goji berries, add them during the final 2 hours of simmering. Astragalus needs time to release its polysaccharides but becomes bitter if boiled for 24 hours; goji berries break down if cooked too long.
  7. Simmer long and low.
    — Beef broth: 24–48 hours. The longer end extracts maximum collagen and minerals.
    — Chicken broth: 12–24 hours. Chicken bones are finer and release their gelatin faster.
    — Slow cooker: Set to Low and let it run. Check water level every 8–12 hours; top up with boiling water if bones become exposed.
  8. Strain. Remove the large bones and vegetables with tongs, then pour the broth through a fine-mesh sieve lined with cheesecloth into a large heatproof container or multiple jars.
  9. Cool and refrigerate. Let the broth cool to room temperature (no more than 2 hours), then refrigerate. As it chills, the collagen will set into a wobbly, jelly-like consistency — your signal that the broth is well made.

Storage & Shelf Life

Gel test: If your refrigerated broth doesn't set into jelly, it either wasn't simmered long enough, had too much water, or used bones that were too clean (lack of connective tissue). It's still nutritious — just use it more quickly, as it will spoil faster.

Refrigerator (4 °C / 40 °F)

  • Beef broth: 4–5 days. Skim the solidified fat cap before using if you prefer a leaner broth; the fat itself is flavourful and can be reserved for cooking.
  • Chicken broth: 3–4 days. Its higher protein content makes it spoil slightly faster.
  • Always use a clean utensil when scooping to avoid introducing bacteria.

Freezer (≤ −18 °C / 0 °F)

  • Freeze in 500 ml or 1 L mason jars (leave 2.5 cm headspace for expansion), silicone ice-cube trays for single-serving portions, or zip-top freezer bags laid flat.
  • Lasts 6–9 months at peak quality; safe indefinitely.
  • To thaw: overnight in the fridge or on the stove over low heat. Do not microwave — it can scorch the gelatin and create hot spots.

Canning (pressure canner only)

Bone broth is a low-acid food and must be processed in a pressure canner (not a water-bath). Follow your canner's instructions for stock: typically 25 minutes per litre at 11 PSI (adjust for altitude). Properly canned broth is shelf-stable for 12–18 months.

Pro Tips for a Superior Broth

  • Include chicken feet or pig trotters. These are the single richest sources of gelatin. One or two chicken feet added to a chicken carcass will transform the gel set.
  • Vinegar is non-negotiable. Without acid, mineral extraction is dramatically reduced. Apple cider, raw unfiltered, or brown rice vinegar all work. Don't worry — the vinegar flavour cooks off completely.
  • Don't salt until serving. Salt added during simmering concentrates as the broth reduces, often making it too salty. Season each bowl or recipe individually.
  • Second batch. After straining, you can re-cover the bones with water and simmer for another 12–24 hours to get a lighter "second broth." It will have less collagen but still offers minerals and flavour.
  • Ginger in TCM: Fresh ginger (Sheng Jiang) is warming and dispersing — it helps the body assimilate the heavy, yin-nourishing quality of the broth and prevents it from creating dampness or phlegm. This is why ginger is a near-universal addition across Asian bone-broth traditions.

How to Use Bone Broth

Beyond sipping it from a mug (the simplest and most therapeutic use), bone broth is a foundation for countless dishes:

  • Use it as the base for soups, stews, and congee (xi fan / jook)
  • Cook rice, quinoa, or grains in it instead of water
  • Deglaze pans after searing meat — adds instant depth
  • Sip it warm with a pinch of salt and fresh herbs as a morning tonic
  • Use as the liquid in braised greens, lentils, or beans
  • Freeze into cubes and add to stir-fries or sauces for a mineral hit

Whether you approach bone broth as a nourishing ancestral food, a collagen-rich functional beverage, or a Kidney-tonifying medicinal preparation in the TCM tradition, the principle is the same: low and slow transforms humble bones into liquid gold. A single batch pays dividends across a week of meals — and, over months of regular consumption, may quietly replenish reserves of vitality you didn't even know you were running low on.

— Brewed with patience, sipped with intention.
