Papaya and Snow Fungus Soup — The TCM Beauty Tonic

🥘 Recipe · 7 min read

A Bowl of Beauty, Rooted in TCM Tradition

Across the Cantonese-speaking world, few home soups enjoy the reputation of papaya and snow fungus soup (木瓜银耳汤, mù guā yín ěr tāng). Served warm, gently sweet, and silky smooth, this dessert-like soup has been passed down through generations as a go-to beauty tonic. Mothers teach daughters, grandmothers share secrets — and the bowl remains a staple at family tables from Hong Kong to San Francisco's Chinatown.

In Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM), this soup is not simply a pleasant treat. It is a thoughtfully composed medicinal recipe designed to nourish yin (滋阴), moisten dryness, and support the skin from the inside out. The two main ingredients — papaya and snow fungus — work together synergistically, each contributing distinct energetic qualities that, when combined, offer more than either could alone.

Let us explore the TCM wisdom behind this classic soup, how to prepare it at home, and why it continues to be one of the most beloved recipes in Chinese food therapy.

The TCM Properties of Papaya (木瓜)

Before we discuss the papaya you find in supermarkets — the large, orange-fleshed tropical fruit — it is worth noting that TCM traditionally distinguishes between two types:

  • Chaenomeles fruit (木瓜, mù guā) — a small, yellow, hard fruit used primarily in medicinal formulas for joint health and digestion.
  • Papaya (番木瓜, fān mù guā) — the larger tropical fruit we know today, introduced to China via trade routes and later adopted into food therapy.

For the soup in this article, we refer to the familiar tropical papaya (Carica papaya), which has become a staple in TCM-informed home cooking.

Energetic Profile

  • Nature: Neutral to slightly warm
  • Taste: Sweet
  • Meridians entered: Spleen, Stomach

Traditional Uses

In TCM food therapy, papaya is traditionally believed to:

  • Support digestion — the enzyme papain is recognized as aiding protein breakdown, which aligns with the TCM view that papaya harmonizes the Stomach and Spleen.
  • Promote lactation — green papaya is a renowned ingredient in postpartum soups for nursing mothers.
  • Nourish fluids — the fruit's high water content and natural sweetness make it useful for mild thirst and dryness.

Selection Tips

For this soup, choose a papaya that is ripe but still firm — the flesh should yield slightly to gentle pressure but not be mushy. The skin should be predominantly yellow with some green patches. Overly ripe papaya will disintegrate during simmering, while under-ripe fruit will lack sweetness.

The TCM Properties of Snow Fungus (银耳)

Snow fungus (Tremella fuciformis), also known as white fungus, silver ear, or snow ear, is perhaps the most celebrated beauty ingredient in Chinese food therapy. Its translucent, gelatinous appearance and neutral flavour make it a versatile base for both sweet and savoury preparations.

Energetic Profile

  • Nature: Neutral
  • Taste: Sweet and bland (淡)
  • Meridians entered: Lung, Stomach, Kidney

Traditional Uses

Snow fungus is traditionally valued for its ability to:

  • Nourish yin and moisten the Lungs — TCM associates the Lungs with the skin and body hair. Nourishing Lung yin is believed to directly benefit the skin's hydration and appearance.
  • Generate fluids (生津) — the gelatinous texture is seen as a sign of its moisturizing properties. In TCM, foods that are slippery or gelatinous are often associated with fluid-nourishing qualities.
  • Support the Stomach — snow fungus is considered gentle and easy to digest, making it suitable for those with weak digestive function.

Modern Perspective

Contemporary research has identified that snow fungus is rich in polysaccharides, particularly Tremella polysaccharides, which have demonstrated hydrating and antioxidant properties in laboratory studies. Snow fungus is also a good source of dietary fibre and contains several amino acids. While these findings are preliminary, they offer interesting parallels to the traditional TCM view of snow fungus as a yin-nourishing, skin-supporting food.

Selection and Preparation

Dried snow fungus is widely available in Asian grocery stores and online. When selecting:

  • Choose pieces that are pale yellow to off-white — bright white pieces may have been bleached.
  • Look for large, intact flowers with a faint mushroom-like aroma.
  • Avoid any pieces with dark spots or a musty smell.

To prepare: soak dried snow fungus in cool water for 2-4 hours (or overnight in the refrigerator). It will expand significantly — about 5-6 times its dried volume. Trim off the hard yellow base, then tear the fungus into bite-sized florets.

Why Papaya and Snow Fungus Work Together

The synergy of this soup lies in the complementary actions of its two main ingredients:

  • Papaya brings warmth and sweetness to support the Spleen and Stomach, aiding digestion and ensuring the body can absorb nutrients efficiently.
  • Snow fungus provides deep yin nourishment to the Lungs and Kidneys, directly addressing dryness at its root.

Together, they address both the production and distribution of body fluids. The soup also commonly includes goji berries (枸杞) and red dates (红枣), which further support blood nourishment and the even delivery of moisture throughout the body.

Complete Recipe: Papaya and Snow Fungus Soup

Ingredients

  • 1 medium ripe papaya (about 500g / 1 lb), peeled, seeded, and cut into 2cm chunks
  • 15g (about ½ oz) dried snow fungus (1 large flower)
  • 10-12 goji berries (枸杞)
  • 3-4 red dates (红枣), pitted
  • 30-40g rock sugar (or to taste)
  • 1.2 litres (5 cups) water
  • Optional: 5-6 sliced almonds (南北杏) for additional lung support

Step-by-Step Instructions

  1. Prepare the snow fungus: Soak the dried snow fungus in cool water for 2-4 hours until fully rehydrated and soft. Trim off the hard yellow base and tear into small florets. Rinse well.
  2. Prepare the papaya: Peel the papaya, cut it in half lengthwise, and remove the seeds. Cut the flesh into 2cm chunks.
  3. Rinse the goji berries and red dates: Give them a quick rinse under cool water. If using red dates, make a small cut in each to help release their flavour.
  4. Simmer the snow fungus first: In a large pot, bring the water to a boil. Add the snow fungus, reduce heat to medium-low, and simmer for 30 minutes. Snow fungus takes longer to soften than papaya, so it needs a head start.
  5. Add papaya and red dates: After 30 minutes, add the papaya chunks and red dates. Continue simmering for another 20-25 minutes, until the papaya is tender but still holding its shape.
  6. Add goji berries and rock sugar: Add the goji berries and rock sugar in the final 5 minutes. Stir gently until the sugar dissolves completely.
  7. Serve: Ladle into bowls and serve warm. The soup can also be enjoyed at room temperature or chilled as a refreshing summer dessert.

Variations

  • With lotus seeds (莲子): Add 20g of pre-soaked lotus seeds along with the snow fungus for additional heart-calming properties.
  • With dried longan (龙眼干): Add 10 dried longan arils in the last 10 minutes of cooking for extra sweetness and blood-nourishing benefits.
  • Coconut version: Replace half the water with coconut water for a tropical twist that further supports hydration.
  • Savory version: Omit the rock sugar, add a pinch of salt, and include a few slices of fresh ginger — this is less common but enjoyed by those who prefer a mildly savoury soup.

When to Enjoy This Soup

In TCM, timing matters. This soup is traditionally recommended:

  • During autumn — the season of dryness (燥), when the body's yin fluids are most easily depleted and the skin may feel tight or dry.
  • In winter — when indoor heating creates a dry environment that can affect the Lungs and skin.
  • After illness — to help restore body fluids and support recovery.
  • As a weekly maintenance soup — many families enjoy it once or twice a week year-round for general skin and lung health.

Precautions and Considerations

While generally safe and gentle, this soup may not be suitable for everyone:

  • Cold-damp conditions: Those with signs of cold-damp (cold hands and feet, loose stools, a feeling of heaviness) may find this soup too moistening. TCM practitioners sometimes suggest adding a few slices of fresh ginger to offset the cooling effect.
  • Excessive phlegm: If you have copious, watery phlegm, the moistening nature of snow fungus may not be appropriate. Consult a TCM practitioner.
  • Diabetes: The rock sugar in this soup can raise blood sugar. Those managing diabetes should omit or replace the sugar with a small amount of monk fruit sweetener (罗汉果).
  • Pregnancy: This soup is generally considered safe during pregnancy. However, green (unripe) papaya should be avoided during pregnancy as it contains higher levels of latex compounds. Always consult your healthcare provider.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I use fresh snow fungus instead of dried?

Fresh snow fungus is becoming more widely available. Use about 50g fresh for every 15g dried called for in the recipe. Skip the long soaking — just rinse and trim the base, then add directly.

How should I store leftovers?

Store in an airtight container in the refrigerator for up to 3 days. The soup will become more gelatinous as it cools — this is natural and a sign of the snow fungus's beneficial polysaccharides. Reheat gently on the stovetop or in a microwave.

Can I make this in a slow cooker?

Yes. Soak the snow fungus first, then combine all ingredients except goji berries and rock sugar in a slow cooker. Cook on low for 3-4 hours. Add goji berries and sugar in the last 15 minutes.

Is this soup suitable for children?

Yes, in moderation. The gentle, sweet flavour is usually well-liked by children. Start with a small bowl and observe tolerance. Children under two years old should not consume rock sugar in significant amounts.
