Congee (粥) — The Ultimate Healing Porridge

🥘 Recipe · 8 min read

There is a reason congee — the warm, silky rice porridge known as zhōu (粥) in Mandarin and jook in Cantonese — has been the first meal offered to the sick, the elderly, and the recovering for thousands of years. It is not simply food. In the tradition of Chinese medicine, congee is regarded as a gentle, restorative tonic — a bowl of warmth that asks almost nothing of your digestive system while giving everything back in nourishment.

Whether you are nursing a cold, soothing an upset stomach, or simply craving a meal that feels like a hug from the inside, congee delivers. Here is everything you need to know about this humble yet profoundly healing dish — from its TCM roots to a complete recipe you can make tonight.

What Is Congee?

At its simplest, congee is rice simmered in a large quantity of water or broth until the grains have broken down into a soft, creamy porridge. The ratio of liquid to rice is much higher than for steamed rice — typically 6:1, 8:1, or even 10:1 depending on how thick or thin you like it — and the cooking time is measured in hours, not minutes.

Unlike Western rice pudding, congee is savoury, not sweet. It is served as a canvas for a wide range of toppings and mix-ins: shredded chicken, preserved egg (皮蛋, pídàn), century egg, ginger, scallions, fried shallots, soy sauce, sesame oil, white pepper, and more. Across China and throughout East and Southeast Asia, every family has its own version, its own ritual, its own memory of congee bubbling on the stove.

But what makes congee truly extraordinary is not its versatility — it is what this simple porridge does for the body.

Why Congee Is the Ultimate Healing Food in Traditional Chinese Medicine

Gentle on Digestion

In TCM, the Stomach and Spleen are considered the "root of post-natal Qi" — the engine that transforms food into the energy and blood your body needs to function. When this system is strong, you feel energised, grounded, and resilient. When it is weak — due to illness, stress, overwork, or poor diet — you feel tired, bloated, and depleted.

Congee is considered one of the most easily digestible foods in the TCM food therapy canon. The long, slow cooking process breaks down the starch molecules in the rice into simple sugars that the body can absorb with minimal digestive effort. This is why congee is traditionally the first solid-like food given to people recovering from illness, surgery, or digestive distress. It nourishes without burdening.

Nourishes the Spleen

The Spleen in TCM is said to prefer warmth, moisture, and foods that are cooked thoroughly. Cold, raw, or greasy foods are believed to weaken Spleen function, leading to fatigue, loose stools, poor appetite, and what practitioners call "dampness" — a sluggish, heavy feeling in the body.

Congee, with its warm, soft, fully cooked nature, is considered a quintessential Spleen-nourishing food. The rice itself is classified as sweet and neutral in TCM, making it a gentle tonic that supports the Spleen and Stomach without introducing cold or heat extremes. When congee is cooked until the rice forms a silky "fat" on the surface (known as the mi you or "rice oil"), it is believed to be especially restorative for the Stomach lining.

Supports Qi

Qi (气) — the vital life force that animates the body — is produced primarily from the food and air we take in. When the Spleen and Stomach are functioning well, Qi is abundant and flows smoothly. When they are compromised, Qi production suffers, and you feel depleted.

Congee is believed to support healthy Qi production by providing easily assimilated nourishment that strengthens the digestive organs without depleting their energy. In TCM, this is described as a food that "tonifies the Middle Burner" (the digestive region of the body). It is not a stimulant — it does not force the body to do more. It simply provides the raw material for Qi in a form the body can readily use.

This is why congee is the classic recovery food not just for physical illness, but also for periods of emotional stress, grief, or exhaustion. When your Qi is depleted, congee is there to help rebuild it, one spoonful at a time.

The Complete Congee Recipe

Below is a master recipe for basic congee that you can customise with any of the variations that follow. Use a heavy-bottomed pot — a Dutch oven or stainless steel pot works beautifully — to prevent scorching during the long simmer.

Basic Plain Congee

Prep time: 5 minutes
Cook time: 60–90 minutes
Serves: 4

Ingredients

  • ½ cup (100 g) white rice — short-grain, jasmine, or medium-grain
  • 6 cups (1.4 L) water or chicken broth
  • ½ teaspoon sea salt
  • Optional: 1 teaspoon sesame oil for drizzling

Step-by-Step Instructions

  1. Rinse the rice: Place the rice in a fine-mesh strainer and rinse under cold running water until the water runs clear. This removes excess starch and prevents the congee from becoming overly gluey.
  2. Optional step for faster cooking: After rinsing, drain the rice and toss it with 1 teaspoon of sesame oil. Let it sit for 10 minutes. This step, borrowed from Cantonese congee technique, helps the grains break down faster.
  3. Combine and bring to a boil: In a heavy-bottomed pot, combine the rinsed rice and 6 cups of water or broth. Bring to a boil over medium-high heat, stirring occasionally.
  4. Reduce to a simmer: Once boiling, reduce the heat to the lowest setting so the congee is barely bubbling. A gentle, lazy simmer is ideal — too vigorous and the water will evaporate too quickly.
  5. Cook uncovered: Leave the pot uncovered (or slightly ajar) and cook for 60–90 minutes. Stir every 10–15 minutes, scraping the bottom to prevent sticking. As the congee thickens, stir more frequently.
  6. Adjust consistency: If the congee becomes too thick before the rice has fully broken down, add a splash of hot water or broth. If it is too thin, continue simmering uncovered for another 10–15 minutes.
  7. Season and serve: Once the rice has broken down into a creamy, porridge-like consistency, stir in ½ teaspoon of salt (or to taste). Ladle into bowls and top as desired.

Common Garnishes and Toppings

  • Thinly sliced scallions (green onions)
  • Julienned fresh ginger
  • Toasted sesame oil drizzle
  • Light soy sauce or tamari
  • Fried shallots or garlic chips
  • White pepper
  • Chopped fresh cilantro
  • Toasted sesame seeds
  • Century egg or preserved egg (皮蛋, pídàn) — quartered

Congee Variations

Once you have mastered the basic congee, the possibilities are endless. Here are four essential variations, each with its own purpose and flavour profile.

Plain Congee (白粥, báizhōu)

The simplest and most restorative version. Plain congee is traditionally what is served to someone who has been ill for days and needs the gentlest possible nourishment. Serve it with a small dish of pickled vegetables (榨菜, zhàcài) or fermented tofu (fǔrǔ) on the side. The congee itself is unadorned — just rice, water, and a whisper of salt. This is congee in its purest healing form.

Chicken Congee (鸡粥, jīzhōu)

Add to the base recipe: Replace half the water with chicken broth. Add 1 boneless, skinless chicken breast (about 150 g) to the pot during the last 20 minutes of cooking. Remove the chicken, shred it with two forks, and return the shredded meat to the congee. Finish with a drizzle of sesame oil, sliced scallions, and a few drops of soy sauce. This version is more substantial and is traditionally believed to be Qi-building and strengthening.

Ginger-Scallion Congee (姜葱粥, jiāngcōng zhōu)

Add to the base recipe: While cooking the plain congee, add 3 thin slices of fresh ginger to the pot at the beginning. (Ginger is warming in TCM and is believed to help expel cold, making this version ideal for the early stages of a cold or when you feel chilled.) When serving, top generously with sliced scallions and a pinch of white pepper. For extra benefit, grate an additional ½ teaspoon of fresh ginger directly into the bowl just before eating.

Medicinal Congee with Goji and Jujube (枸杞红枣粥, gǒuqǐ hóngzǎo zhōu)

Add to the base recipe: When you add the rice to the pot, also add 5–6 dried red jujubes (红枣, hóngzǎo, also called Chinese red dates) — pitted and halved. During the last 10 minutes of cooking, stir in 1 tablespoon of dried goji berries (枸杞, gǒuqǐ). Jujubes are traditionally used in TCM to nourish the blood and calm the spirit, while goji berries are believed to support the Liver and Kidneys and improve vitality. A light drizzle of honey or a pinch of rock sugar (冰糖, bīngtáng) can be added for sweetness, though the jujubes themselves impart a gentle sweetness. This variation is often recommended for those who feel run-down, experience dizziness, or have difficulty sleeping.

Tips for the Perfect Bowl of Congee

  • Use leftover rice for faster congee: Already-cooked rice will break down in about 20–30 minutes, making this a great way to transform leftover plain rice into a quick breakfast.
  • Soak the rice: Soaking raw rice for 30 minutes before cooking can reduce the cook time by about 15–20 minutes.
  • Stir frequently toward the end: As the congee thickens, it becomes prone to sticking. Stir more often in the final 20 minutes.
  • Go low and slow: The best congee is cooked at the gentlest possible simmer. A rapid boil will cause the liquid to evaporate too fast and the texture to become uneven.
  • Make it in a slow cooker or Instant Pot: For hands-off congee, combine rice and water in a slow cooker (low for 6–8 hours) or an Instant Pot (high pressure for 20 minutes, then natural release for 20 minutes).
  • The "rice oil" matters: In TCM, the thin, milky layer that forms on top of well-cooked congee — called mi you (米油) or "rice oil" — is considered the most nourishing part. Do not skim it off. Stir it into the congee for maximum benefit.
  • Adjust the ratio to your preference: For a thinner congee, use 8 cups of liquid per ½ cup of rice. For a very thick, almost risotto-like congee, use 4 cups. The 6:1 ratio in the recipe above produces a classic medium-thick congee.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I make congee with brown rice?

Yes, but expect a longer cooking time (2–3 hours) and a nuttier, less creamy texture. Brown rice is more difficult to digest than white rice in TCM terms, so it is less ideal when you are purposefully seeking a gentle, healing meal. For everyday eating, it works fine — just increase the liquid to 7–8 cups and simmer longer.

Can I freeze congee?

Yes. Congee freezes well for up to 3 months. Let it cool completely, then portion into freezer-safe containers. To reheat, thaw overnight in the refrigerator, then warm gently on the stove or in the microwave with a splash of water or broth to loosen it. The texture will remain creamy.

Is congee gluten-free?

Yes, congee made with rice is naturally gluten-free. However, if you add soy sauce, tamari, or broth, check that those ingredients are gluten-free if this is a concern for you.

Can congee help with a cold or flu?

Traditionally in TCM, warm congee — particularly the ginger-scallion variation — is believed to help expel external "Wind-Cold" pathogens and support the body's recovery during seasonal illness. The warmth, easy digestibility, and hydrating nature of congee also make it a practical, comforting choice when appetite is low.

Final Thoughts

Congee is proof that the most profound healing foods are often the simplest. A bowl of rice and water, transformed by patience and gentle heat, becomes something far greater than the sum of its parts: a tonic for the Spleen, a balm for the Stomach, and a steady source of warm, accessible Qi.

Whether you make plain congee for a quiet morning, chicken congee to fortify a loved one after a long week, or medicinal congee with goji and jujube to restore your energy, know that you are participating in a tradition that has nourished people for millennia. There is wisdom in that bowl. All you have to do is take the first spoonful.
