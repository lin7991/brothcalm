Mugwort Tea (Ai Ye Cha) — Warming the Womb and Digestion

🍵 Tea & Tonic · 5 min read

A Forgotten Herb with Deep Roots

There is a quiet herb that grows along roadsides and meadow edges — unassuming, silvery-green, fragrant when brushed. You may have walked past it a hundred times. Its Latin name is Artemisia vulgaris, and in Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM) it is called Ai Ye (艾叶) — mugwort. For thousands of years, this humble leaf has been dried, bundled, steeped, and burned in the service of warmth: warmth for the womb, warmth for the stomach, and warmth for a body that has held too much cold.

Mugwort tea, or Ai Ye Cha (艾叶茶), is one of the most approachable ways to experience this herb's grounding energy. Unlike the intense smoke of moxibustion (the TCM practice of burning mugwort near acupuncture points), a cup of mugwort tea is gentle — fragrant, slightly bitter, and deeply warming from the first sip.

In this article, we explore the traditional uses of Ai Ye Cha, its TCM energetics, how to brew it properly, and who may benefit most from adding this ancient tea to their wellness routine.

The TCM Energetics of Ai Ye (Mugwort)

To understand what mugwort tea does, it helps to understand how TCM classifies it. Every herb in the Chinese materia medica carries a unique energetic signature — a combination of temperature, flavour, and organ affinity.

Nature, Flavour, and Meridian Entry

  • Nature (Qi): Warm
  • Flavour (Wei): Acrid (pungent) and Bitter
  • Meridian Entry (Gui Jing): Liver, Spleen, and Kidney

The warm nature of Ai Ye makes it a classic herb for what TCM calls "dispelling cold" (祛寒, qū hán). When cold settles in the lower abdomen — whether from climate, diet, or constitution — it can manifest as menstrual cramps, sluggish digestion, lower back tension, or a feeling of deep chill that blankets and blankets don't touch. Mugwort addresses this cold at its root.

The acrid flavour moves and disperses; the bitter flavour drains and descends. Together, they help mugwort reach the lower jiao (the abdominal cavity that houses the digestive and reproductive organs) and gently warm it from within.

Why the Liver, Spleen, and Kidney Meridians Matter

These three organ networks are central to reproductive and digestive health in TCM. The Liver governs the free flow of Qi (vital energy) and stores blood — both of which are essential for a smooth menstrual cycle. The Spleen is the root of post-natal Qi and governs transformation and transportation of nutrients. The Kidneys store Jing (essence) and provide the foundational warmth for the entire body. By entering all three, mugwort tea can simultaneously regulate menstruation, strengthen digestion, and reinforce the body's core warmth.

Traditional Uses of Mugwort Tea

Warming the Womb and Supporting Menstrual Health

The most celebrated traditional use of Ai Ye in TCM is in gynaecology. Mugwort is one of the classic herbs for what TCM practitioners call "warming the womb" (暖宫, nuǎn gōng). When cold-dampness has invaded the uterine channel, it may lead to:

  • Menstrual cramps with a dull, heavy, or dragging quality
  • Delayed or scanty menstruation
  • Clotted, dark menstrual blood
  • A sensation of cold in the lower abdomen
  • Difficulty conceiving (in the context of a cold-womb pattern)

In these cases, a warm cup of mugwort tea, especially in the days leading up to and during the menstrual period, is traditionally believed to encourage blood flow, ease cramping, and restore a sense of warmth and ease to the pelvis.

Mugwort is frequently combined with other warming, blood-regulating herbs in TCM formulas. One well-known combination pairs Ai Ye with Dang Gui (Angelica sinensis) and Hong Hua (Safflower) for gynaecological support. For a simpler at-home tea, mugwort can be blended with ginger, dates, or a cinnamon stick to amplify its warming qualities.

Dispelling Cold-Dampness from Digestion

Ai Ye is not only for the womb. Its ability to warm the middle jiao (the spleen and stomach region) makes it a valuable digestive aid, particularly when the digestive fire has been dampened by cold and dampness.

Signs that your digestion might benefit from the warming action of mugwort tea include:

  • Loose, watery stools
  • A feeling of cold in the stomach or abdomen after eating
  • Poor appetite with a preference for warm foods and drinks
  • Abdominal bloating that feels better with a hot compress
  • Fatigue and sluggishness after meals

In TCM thinking, the Spleen thrives on warmth and dislikes dampness. By warming the Spleen meridian and drying dampness, mugwort tea traditionally helps the body transform food into usable Qi rather than letting it stagnate as phlegm or dampness.

External Use in Moxibustion

While this article focuses on the tea, it is worth noting that Ai Ye is most widely known in TCM as the herb used in moxibustion (艾灸, ài jiǔ) — the practice of burning dried mugwort near acupuncture points to warm them. A related home practice is using mugwort in foot baths or sitz baths to draw warmth downward and relieve pelvic cold. If you have access to dried mugwort leaves, a handful simmered in a basin of hot water makes a deeply grounding foot soak before bed.

How to Brew the Perfect Cup of Mugwort Tea

Brewing mugwort tea is simple, but a few details make the difference between a pleasant, aromatic cup and a bitter, over-extracted one.

Ingredients

  • 1–2 teaspoons dried mugwort leaves (Ai Ye) — available at TCM herb shops or online
  • 1 cup (240 ml) filtered water
  • Optional: a thin slice of fresh ginger, 1 teaspoon of honey, or a cinnamon stick

Instructions

  1. Rinse the leaves. Place the dried mugwort in a tea strainer or cup and give it a quick rinse with hot water. This wakes up the herb and removes any dust.
  2. Heat the water. Bring water to just under a boil — about 90–95°C (195–205°F). Avoid a rolling boil, which can make the tea overly bitter.
  3. Steep. Pour the hot water over the mugwort leaves and cover. Steep for 5–7 minutes. The longer you steep, the stronger (and more bitter) the flavour.
  4. Strain and serve. Remove the leaves, stir in honey if desired, and sip slowly. The tea is best enjoyed warm, not hot.

Tip: Mugwort's flavour is distinct — earthy and herbaceous, reminiscent of dried oregano or sage with a subtle piney undertone. If you are new to it, start with 1 teaspoon per cup and add ginger or honey to mellow the bitterness.

Who Should Drink Mugwort Tea — and Who Should Avoid It

Best suited for:

  • People with cold-type constitutions who often feel chilly, especially in the lower body
  • Those experiencing period pain with a cold, dull, cramping quality
  • Individuals with sluggish digestion that improves with warmth
  • Anyone looking to incorporate a warming, grounding herbal tea into their seasonal routine, particularly in autumn and winter

Contraindications and Cautions

  • Pregnancy: Mugwort is contraindicated in pregnancy. It is traditionally believed to stimulate uterine contractions and may increase the risk of miscarriage. Avoid all forms of mugwort tea during pregnancy.
  • Excess heat patterns: If you tend to run hot — signs include a red face, feeling overheated, a dry mouth, a tendency toward constipation, or irritability — mugwort's warm nature may exacerbate these symptoms.
  • Allergies: Mugwort is in the Asteraceae family. If you are allergic to ragweed, chrysanthemums, marigolds, or daisies, you may react to mugwort as well.
  • Long-term daily use: Because of its acrid, dispersing nature, mugwort tea is best used in courses rather than as an everyday beverage for months on end. Listen to your body and take breaks.

As always, check with a qualified TCM practitioner or healthcare provider before beginning any herbal regimen, especially if you are managing a chronic health condition or taking prescription medications.

A Cup of Warmth in Cold Seasons

There is something quietly powerful about sipping a tea whose history stretches back two millennia. Mugwort has been found in the burial sites of ancient Chinese nobles, referenced in the earliest herbal compendia, and used continuously in folk medicine across Europe and Asia. It is one of those rare herbs that connects us to our ancestors — women who brewed it for their monthly cycles, farmers who burned it to ward off illness, monks who drank it to aid meditation.

When you brew a cup of mugwort tea, you are not just extracting flavour from dried leaves. You are participating in a tradition of warming the body from the inside out — a tradition that understands that cold, whether physical or emotional, can be met with gentle, persistent heat.

Pour yourself a cup on a chilly evening. Let the earthy, aromatic steam rise. Feel the warmth settle into your belly and spread outward. That is Ai Ye at work.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I drink mugwort tea during my period?

Yes — many women in TCM tradition drink mugwort tea during menstruation, particularly if they experience cold-type cramps. Start with a small cup and see how your body responds. If cramping worsens or you feel overheated, discontinue use.

Where can I buy dried mugwort leaves?

Dried Ai Ye is available at Chinese herb shops, some health food stores, and online retailers. Look for whole dried leaves rather than powdered herb for the best-quality tea. Reputable TCM suppliers will list the harvest year and origin.

Can I grow mugwort and dry it myself?

Yes. Mugwort (Artemisia vulgaris) grows easily in most temperate climates. Harvest the leaves before the plant flowers, tie them in small bundles, and hang them in a dry, dark, well-ventilated space. Once fully dry, crumble the leaves and store in an airtight jar away from direct light.

Does mugwort tea interact with medications?

Mugwort may interact with anticoagulant (blood-thinning) medications, sedatives, and drugs that affect uterine activity. If you take any prescription medication, consult your doctor or a qualified TCM practitioner before drinking mugwort tea regularly.
