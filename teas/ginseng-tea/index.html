Ginseng Tea — Restoring Qi the Traditional Way

🍵 Tea & Tonic · 5 min read

Introduction: The Root of Vitality

In the world of Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM), few herbs command the reverence that ginseng does. Known as rén shēn (人參) — literally "man root" for its humanoid shape — ginseng has been prized for thousands of years as a premier tonic for vitality, longevity, and the restoration of qi, the body's vital life force.

Ginseng tea is among the most accessible and time-honored ways to experience this remarkable root. A simple steaming cup of ginseng tea carries centuries of herbal wisdom, offering a warming, grounding ritual that supports energy, focus, and resilience. Whether you are new to TCM or a seasoned practitioner of food therapy, understanding how to prepare and enjoy ginseng tea properly can add a powerful tool to your wellness routine.

This guide explores the traditional properties of ginseng, its historical uses, a complete recipe for preparing the tea at home, and the important considerations that come with working with such a potent herb.

TCM Properties of Ginseng

In TCM, every herb is understood through its energetics — its temperature, flavor, and the organ systems (meridians) it influences. Ginseng is one of the few herbs classified as a superior tonic, meaning it is considered both nourishing and relatively safe for long-term use when taken appropriately.

Energetics (Temperature)

Ginseng is classified as slightly warming (wēn). It gently raises the body's internal warmth without being aggressively heating like some other herbs. This makes it especially well-suited for individuals who feel cold, fatigued, or depleted — conditions that TCM associates with yang deficiency or qi deficiency.

Flavors

Ginseng has two primary flavors in TCM:

  • Sweet (gān): Sweet flavors in TCM are tonifying and harmonizing. They strengthen the body's foundational energy and support the spleen and stomach, the organs of digestion and assimilation.
  • Slightly bitter (kǔ): Bitter flavors drain and descend. In ginseng, this quality directs energy downward, preventing the herb from being overly stimulating or dispersing qi upward in an uncontrolled way.

Together, these flavors give ginseng its unique ability to both build qi and settle the mind — a rare combination among tonic herbs.

Meridians (Organ Systems)

Ginseng primarily enters three meridians:

  • Spleen — The foundation of post-natal qi (the energy we derive from food and digestion). Ginseng strengthens the spleen's ability to transform and transport nutrients, which is why it is traditionally used for poor appetite, digestive weakness, and fatigue after illness.
  • Lung — Ginseng tonifies lung qi, supporting respiration and the body's defensive energy (wèi qì). TCM practitioners have traditionally recommended it for shortness of breath, weak cough, and frequent colds.
  • Heart — By calming the shen (spirit) and tonifying heart qi, ginseng is believed to support mental clarity, focus, and emotional balance. This is why it has a long history of use among scholars, meditators, and those recovering from mental exhaustion.

Traditional Uses: A Historical Perspective

The written history of ginseng in Chinese medicine stretches back over 2,000 years. The Shen Nong Ben Cao Jing (The Divine Farmer's Materia Medica), one of the oldest and most revered herbal texts in China, classifies ginseng as a "superior" herb — the highest grade reserved for substances that nourish life and are non-toxic when used correctly.

According to this ancient text, ginseng "mainly tonifies the five viscera, stabilizes the spirit, calms the soul, stops palpitations, and clears the eyes. It opens the heart, benefits understanding, and when taken over time, lightens the body and extends the years."

Throughout Chinese imperial history, ginseng was reserved for emperors, nobility, and the elite. Wild mountain ginseng (shān shēn) was considered the most potent and was worth its weight in silver. Today, cultivated ginseng is far more accessible, though wild-simulated and older roots are still highly prized by connoisseurs.

Ginseng tea specifically became popular as a daily tonic among scholars and officials who needed sustained mental energy for long hours of study or work. The traditional preparation — a slow simmer to extract the root's essence — was considered a meditative practice in itself, aligning with the TCM principle that how you prepare your medicine matters as much as the ingredients themselves.

Complete Recipe: How to Make Traditional Ginseng Tea

Making ginseng tea is simple, but the details matter. Here is a traditional preparation that maximizes both flavor and the root's beneficial properties.

Ingredients

  • 3–5 thin slices of dried Asian ginseng root (Panax ginseng), about 1–2 grams total — look for roots with a pale yellowish-white color and a fragrant, earthy aroma
  • 2 cups (500 ml) of filtered water — avoid tap water with strong chlorine or mineral content, which can interfere with the taste
  • Optional: 3–4 dried goji berries or a thin slice of dried licorice root for added sweetness and synergy
  • Optional: 1 teaspoon raw honey (add after brewing, never to boiling water)

Equipment

  • A small ceramic or glass teapot (avoid reactive metals like aluminum)
  • A ceramic teacup or heatproof mug
  • A small fine-mesh strainer (optional)

Traditional Hot Steep Method

  1. Rinse the ginseng — Briefly rinse the dried ginseng slices under cool water to remove any dust. Do not soak them.
  2. Prepare the water — Bring filtered water to a full boil in a kettle, then let it cool for about 30 seconds. The ideal steeping temperature is around 195–205°F (90–96°C), just off a rolling boil.
  3. Steep — Place the ginseng slices in your teapot or mug. Pour the hot water over them, cover, and steep for 5–7 minutes. A longer steep yields a stronger, more bitter tea; a shorter steep is milder and sweeter.
  4. Strain and serve — Remove the ginseng slices or pour through a strainer into your cup. The slices can be reused once more for a second, milder infusion later in the day — simply add fresh hot water and steep for 7–10 minutes.
  5. Optional additions — If using goji berries, add them to the teapot alongside the ginseng. If using honey, stir it in after the tea has cooled slightly, once it is warm but no longer steaming.

Traditional Simmer Method (Stronger Extraction)

For a more traditional and potent preparation favored by TCM practitioners, simmer the ginseng rather than steeping it:

  1. Place the ginseng slices in a small saucepan with 2 cups of cold filtered water.
  2. Bring to a gentle boil, then reduce heat to the lowest setting, cover, and simmer for 15–20 minutes. The liquid will reduce slightly and take on a pale golden color.
  3. Strain and serve. The simmered root slices can be chewed and eaten, as they still contain valuable constituents — this is a traditional practice for getting the most out of the herb.

Variations

  • Ginseng & Jujube Tea — Add 2–3 dried red dates (jujubes), pitted and sliced, during simmering. This adds a natural sweetness and supports the spleen in TCM.
  • Ginseng & Astragalus Tea — Add 3–4 thin slices of astragalus root for an extra qi-tonifying boost. This combination is traditionally used during seasonal transitions or after illness.
  • Ginseng & Ginger Tea — Add 2 thin slices of fresh ginger during simmering for a warming, circulation-supporting variation, especially helpful in cold weather.

When to Drink Ginseng Tea and Who Benefits Most

Best Times to Drink

In TCM, timing your ginseng tea matters as much as the preparation. The traditional recommendation is:

  • Morning or early afternoon — Ginseng provides a gentle, sustained lift in energy without the crash associated with caffeine. Drinking it in the morning aligns with the body's natural yang (active) phase and supports focus throughout the day.
  • Avoid late evening — Because ginseng is slightly warming and energizing, drinking it after 4 PM may interfere with sleep, especially for those who are sensitive to tonifying herbs.
  • During seasonal transitions — The changing of seasons, particularly from autumn into winter, is a traditional time to build qi reserves with warming tonics like ginseng tea.

Who Benefits Most

Ginseng tea is traditionally considered most beneficial for people who show signs of qi deficiency, which in TCM includes symptoms such as:

  • Persistent fatigue or low energy, even after adequate rest
  • Weak digestion, poor appetite, or loose stools
  • Shortness of breath or a weak, soft voice
  • Frequent colds or slow recovery from illness
  • Mental fog, difficulty concentrating, or memory lapses
  • Cold hands and feet

It is also traditionally used for older adults looking to maintain vitality, students or professionals needing sustained mental endurance, and anyone recovering from a prolonged illness, surgery, or physical exhaustion.

Precautions and Contraindications

While ginseng is a gentle tonic when used appropriately, it is a potent herb and not suitable for everyone. In TCM, correct use depends on proper diagnosis of the individual's constitution.

Who Should Avoid or Limit Ginseng Tea

  • Those with excess heat or inflammation — If you tend toward red face, irritability, insomnia, strong thirst, or frequent acne, ginseng's warming nature may aggravate these symptoms. American ginseng (Panax quinquefolius), which is cooling, may be a better alternative.
  • During acute illness with fever — Ginseng is traditionally avoided during acute infections with fever, as its tonifying action is believed to "trap" pathogenic factors in the body. Wait until fever has resolved and you are in the recovery phase.
  • High blood pressure (uncontrolled) — While research on this is mixed, TCM traditionally advises caution with Asian ginseng for those with hypertension, particularly in large or frequent doses. Consult a qualified practitioner.
  • Pregnancy and breastfeeding — Ginseng is generally not recommended during pregnancy, especially in the first trimester. The warming, moving nature of the herb is traditionally considered too stimulating during this delicate period. Always consult your healthcare provider.
  • Autoimmune conditions — Because ginseng is believed to modulate the immune system, individuals with autoimmune disorders should consult a qualified TCM practitioner before regular use.

General Guidelines

  • Start with a low dose — 2 to 3 thin slices per cup — and observe how your body responds.
  • Avoid taking ginseng tea with caffeinated beverages, as the combination may be overly stimulating.
  • Cycle your use — traditional practice often recommends 2 to 3 weeks of daily use followed by a 1-week break.
  • Do not take ginseng continuously for more than three months without consulting a TCM practitioner.

Frequently Asked Questions

What does ginseng tea taste like?

Ginseng tea has a distinctly earthy, slightly bitter taste with subtle sweet and licorice-like undertones. The flavor is often described as woody and aromatic. Many people find it an acquired taste and often add a small amount of honey or goji berries to balance the bitterness.

How much ginseng tea can I drink per day?

In traditional use, one to two cups per day is considered a moderate and beneficial amount. A typical serving uses 3 to 5 thin slices of dried ginseng root (or 1 to 2 grams of powder) steeped per cup. It is best to start with a lower dose and observe how your body responds before increasing intake.

Can I drink ginseng tea every day?

Many people in TCM traditions drink ginseng tea daily for a period of time, especially during colder months or times of low energy. However, it is not typically recommended as a year-round daily beverage. Traditional practice often suggests cycling on and off — for example, drinking it daily for two to three weeks followed by a one-week break — to prevent the body from building tolerance.

Is ginseng tea safe during pregnancy?

Ginseng is generally not recommended during pregnancy, particularly in the first trimester. In TCM, certain warming herbs like ginseng are used with caution during pregnancy because they may influence the body's energy in ways that are not well studied in this context. If you are pregnant, nursing, or trying to conceive, consult a qualified healthcare provider or TCM practitioner before consuming ginseng tea.

What is the difference between Asian ginseng and American ginseng in tea?

Asian ginseng (Panax ginseng) is considered warming and stimulating in TCM, making it ideal for restoring depleted yang qi and boosting energy. American ginseng (Panax quinquefolius) is considered cooling and nourishing, better suited for those with heat signs or yin deficiency. The choice depends on your constitution and what you are trying to address. When in doubt, a TCM practitioner can help determine which type is appropriate for you.
