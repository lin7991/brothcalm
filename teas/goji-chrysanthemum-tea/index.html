Goji and Chrysanthemum Tea — Bright Eyes Formula

🍵 Tea & Tonic · 5 min read

There is a Chinese saying that the eyes are the windows to the liver. In Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM), the Liver meridian opens into the eyes — meaning the health of your eyes reflects the state of your Liver qi and blood. When Liver blood is abundant, the eyes are bright, clear, and resilient. When Liver fire flares up — from long screen hours, stress, or rich food — the eyes become dry, tired, red, or strained.

Goji and Chrysanthemum Tea (gou qi ju hua cha, 枸杞菊花茶) is perhaps the most beloved herbal tea in China for eye health. It pairs two humble ingredients into a golden, fragrant infusion that has been sipped by scholars, monks, and office workers for centuries. One nourishes; the other cools. Together, they form what TCM practitioners call a balanced formula — gentle enough for daily use, yet potent enough to make a real difference to your comfort and clarity.

In this article, we explore the TCM properties of goji and chrysanthemum, explain how the combination works, and share a precise brewing recipe so you can make this bright-eyes tea at home.

TCM Properties of the Two Ingredients

Goji Berries (Gou Qi Zi, 枸杞子) — Sweet, Neutral, Nourishing

Goji berries are classified in TCM as sweet and neutral, entering the Liver and Kidney meridians. Their primary action is to nourish Liver and Kidney yin and blood. This makes them a key herb in vision-supporting formulas — because when Liver blood is sufficient, the eyes receive the nourishment they need to stay bright and relaxed.

The Ben Cao Gang Mu (Compendium of Materia Medica) by Li Shizhen describes goji berries as a superior tonic that "brightens the eyes and calms the spirit." In classical TCM texts, they are listed among the top blood-tonifying herbs, prized not just for vision but for overall vitality, healthy aging, and deep energy reserves.

Goji berries are also considered a mild adaptogenic food in modern wellness circles, being rich in polysaccharides, carotenoids (especially zeaxanthin), and antioxidants. From a TCM perspective, these properties align with the herb's ability to fortify the jing (essence) and support the body's foundational reserves.

Chrysanthemum (Ju Hua, 菊花) — Sweet, Bitter, Cool

Chrysanthemum flowers are classified as sweet and slightly bitter, with a cool nature, and they enter the Lung and Liver meridians. Their signature action is to disperse wind-heat and calm Liver fire — especially Liver fire that rises to the head and eyes.

In TCM theory, when Liver yin is deficient or Liver qi is stagnant, the body responds by generating heat. This Liver fire rises upward, manifesting as red eyes, bloodshot sclera, headaches, irritability, and dizziness. Chrysanthemum's cool, descending nature gently clears this heat without being overly harsh on the digestion.

The Shang Han Lun (Treatise on Cold Damage Disorders) and later classical works feature chrysanthemum in formulas for vision disturbances, headaches, and what we might now call screen-induced eye strain. Modern research has also identified anti-inflammatory and neuroprotective compounds in chrysanthemum extracts, which align nicely with its traditional cooling and calming roles.

How the Combination Works

The genius of goji and chrysanthemum tea lies in how the two ingredients complement each other. Goji supplies and nourishes — it tops up Liver blood and Kidney yin, addressing the root cause of eye fatigue. Chrysanthemum cools and disperses — it clears the active Liver fire that causes redness, dryness, and irritation, addressing the manifestation.

Think of it like watering a plant that has been sitting in the sun too long: goji is the water that restores the soil's moisture, and chrysanthemum creates a gentle shade that prevents the leaves from burning. One addresses the deficiency; the other clears the excess.

This complementary pairing is known in TCM formulary as a balanced approach — one ingredient tonifies while the other purges, so the body can return to harmony without side effects. It is the same principle behind many classic TCM pairs, and it is why this tea is gentle enough for long-term use.

The Classic Recipe

Ingredients

  • 10–15 dried goji berries (about 3–4 g) — preferably organic, from Ningxia or Xinjiang
  • 8–10 dried chrysanthemum flowers (about 2 g) — small white or yellow ju hua varieties, available at Asian grocers or TCM shops
  • 300 ml (about 1¼ cups) water — filtered or spring water is best
  • Optional: 1 small piece of rock sugar (about 5 g) or 1 teaspoon of raw honey, to taste
  • Optional addition: 2–3 dried jujube dates (hong zao) for extra sweetness and blood support

Brewing Instructions

  1. Rinse the ingredients gently under cool running water for a few seconds. Goji berries may have a light dusting from drying; chrysanthemum flowers can contain fine sand or pollen residue.
  2. Place the goji berries and chrysanthemum flowers into a tea cup, a glass teapot, or a heatproof mug. If using jujube dates, slice them open and add them now.
  3. Bring water to a gentle boil (95–100°C / 200–212°F). Pour the hot water directly over the ingredients.
  4. Cover and steep for 5–7 minutes. The water will turn a pale golden colour as the chrysanthemum petals unfurl and the goji berries plump up.
  5. Add sweetener if desired — rock sugar melts in beautifully; honey should be stirred in once the tea has cooled slightly to preserve its beneficial enzymes.
  6. Enjoy warm. Sip slowly, especially in the afternoon when eye fatigue tends to peak. You can resteep the same ingredients once more for a lighter second cup.

Variations to Explore

  • With wolfberry only: Use 12–15 goji berries and skip the chrysanthemum for a purely nourishing, slightly sweeter tea — ideal for cold-weather months.
  • With mint: Add 3–4 fresh mint leaves during steeping for an extra cooling lift that helps with tension headaches.
  • Cold brew version: Place the same ingredients in a large jar with 500 ml cold water and refrigerate overnight. Strain in the morning for a refreshing iced tea that is especially soothing in summer.
  • With mulberries: Add 5–6 dried mulberries (sang shen) alongside the goji to further nourish Liver and Kidney yin.

When to Drink Goji and Chrysanthemum Tea

This tea is particularly beneficial during:

  • Screen-heavy workdays — programmers, designers, writers, and anyone staring at a display for hours will appreciate its soothing effect on tired eyes.
  • Late afternoon slumps — the gentle lift from the brew is calming rather than jittery, making it an excellent alternative to a second coffee.
  • Dry, windy, or heated environments — air-conditioned offices and centrally heated homes both dry out the eyes, and this tea provides internal moisture and cooling.
  • Spring and early summer — in TCM, spring corresponds to the Wood element and the Liver. This is when Liver fire tends to rise, making cooling, blood-nourishing herbs especially appropriate.

Many people make it a daily afternoon ritual around 3–4 p.m., when digestion is complete and the body can focus on absorption. A warm cup at this hour supports both the eyes and the mind, providing a moment of calm in a busy day.

Who Should Drink It — and Who Should Be Cautious

Ideal candidates include people who experience dry, tired, or strained eyes after screen use; those prone to irritability or tension headaches; individuals with a tendency toward heat signs (red face, thirst, restlessness); and anyone who spends long hours in dry or air-conditioned environments.

People who should use caution:

  • Those with cold or weak digestion — if you frequently experience loose stools, cold hands and feet, or a pale tongue, chrysanthemum's cooling nature may be too much. Use only 3–4 flowers or replace it with a warming herb like ginger.
  • Pregnant women — a small amount (3–4 flowers, 8–10 goji berries) is generally considered food-safe, but always consult your healthcare provider.
  • People with low blood pressure — chrysanthemum has a mildly hypotensive effect in some individuals. If you are prone to dizziness or fainting, start with a small amount.
  • Those on blood-thinning medication — goji berries may interact with warfarin in rare cases. Discuss with your doctor if you take anticoagulants.

As with any herbal preparation, start with a small cup and observe how your body responds before making it a daily habit.

FAQ

Can I drink goji and chrysanthemum tea every day?

Yes, this tea is traditionally considered mild enough for daily consumption by most people, especially those who spend long hours in front of screens. In TCM theory, the combination is balanced — goji is neutral and nourishing, while chrysanthemum is cooling but not overly aggressive. A good daily routine is 1 cup in the afternoon. However, if you have a cold constitution or weak digestive system (frequent loose stools, cold hands and feet), it is best to reduce frequency or dilute the chrysanthemum portion.

Is it safe during pregnancy?

In small, food-like amounts the tea is generally considered safe during pregnancy, but it is always best to consult your healthcare provider first. Goji berries are a nourishing food in TCM, while chrysanthemum has cooling properties that some TCM practitioners recommend using in moderation during pregnancy. A conservative approach is to use fewer chrysanthemum flowers (3–4 instead of 8–10) and always discuss with your midwife or doctor.

Can I use fresh flowers or berries instead of dried?

Dried is strongly preferred for this tea. Dried chrysanthemum flowers (ju hua) are specifically prepared for medicinal tea use and carry the full cooling properties described in TCM texts. Fresh chrysanthemum blossoms are often ornamental varieties that may not have the same effect. Likewise, dried goji berries concentrate their sweet, nourishing essence and are available year-round. If you only have fresh goji berries, use about double the quantity and steep at a slightly lower temperature (85°C / 185°F).

Does this tea help with sleep?

Indirectly, yes — but it is not a sedative tea. In TCM theory, eye strain and screen use generate Liver fire and consume Liver blood, which can disturb restful sleep. By soothing the Liver and nourishing blood, this tea may help calm the mind in preparation for sleep. Goji berries are also traditionally associated with nourishing the Kidney and Liver systems, which in TCM play a key role in deep, restorative sleep. For a more direct sleep aid, consider adding a few jujube dates to the brew.

Can I add other ingredients like ginger or honey?

Absolutely. A small piece of rock sugar or a teaspoon of honey is the most common addition to balance chrysanthemum's slight bitterness. You can also add 2–3 jujube dates for extra sweetness and blood-nourishing support, or a few more goji berries if you want a richer flavour. Avoid ginger, though — its warming, dispersing nature contradicts chrysanthemum's cooling direction, and the combination may dull the tea's intended effect on Liver fire.
