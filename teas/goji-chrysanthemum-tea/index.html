Goji Berry and Chrysanthemum Tea — The Classic Liver Cooling Brew

🍵 Tea & Tonic · 5 min read

Introduction

Few herbal combinations in Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM) are as beloved—or as enduring—as the pairing of goji berry (gou qi zi) and chrysanthemum flower (ju hua). Known as a classic liver-cooling brew, this gentle, fragrant tea has been steeped in Chinese wellness culture for centuries. It is the drink you reach for after a long day staring at screens, the cup your grandmother insisted on when your eyes felt tired and dry, and the herbal companion to late-night study sessions.

In TCM, the liver is responsible for the smooth flow of qi (vital energy) and blood throughout the body. When the liver becomes imbalanced—often from stress, poor diet, or excessive screen time—it can generate what practitioners call "liver fire" or "liver heat." This heat tends to rise upward, manifesting as red, irritated eyes, headaches, irritability, and even dizziness. Goji berry and chrysanthemum tea addresses this pattern at its root: the goji nourishes and cools the liver's foundational energy (yin), while chrysanthemum gently disperses the rising heat.

Whether you are new to Chinese food therapy or a seasoned practitioner of TCM-inspired wellness, this tea offers a simple, delicious entry point into the world of herbal balance. Let us explore why this duo works so well, how to prepare it, and when it is traditionally enjoyed.

TCM Properties of Goji Berry and Chrysanthemum

To understand why goji and chrysanthemum are such a powerful pair, it helps to look at each ingredient through the lens of TCM energetics — the temperature, taste, and organ network (meridian) associations that define how an herb or food affects the body.

Goji Berry (Gou Qi Zi)

Nature: Neutral
Taste: Sweet
Meridians: Liver, Kidney

Goji berries are one of the most celebrated tonic herbs in TCM. They are classified as a sweet, neutral substance that enters the Liver and Kidney meridians. Their primary action is to nourish yin—the body's cooling, moistening, foundational substance. In TCM, the Liver stores blood and the Kidneys store essence (jing), and goji is believed to benefit both. When liver yin is adequate, the eyes are well-nourished and vision is clear. When kidney yin is sufficient, the body feels grounded and resilient against stress.

The Shen Nong Ben Cao Jing (The Divine Farmer's Materia Medica), one of the oldest surviving Chinese medical texts, classifies goji berry as a "superior" herb — meaning it is considered safe for long-term use and beneficial for overall vitality. It notes that goji "strengthens the sinews and bones, and brightens the eyes."

Chrysanthemum (Ju Hua)

Nature: Cool
Taste: Sweet, Bitter
Meridians: Liver, Lung

Chrysanthemum flowers bring a cooling, dispersing energy to the blend. Their slightly sweet and bitter taste allows them to both cool the liver and direct that cooling energy upward to the head and eyes — exactly where it is needed when liver fire flares. In TCM terms, chrysanthemum is said to "scatter wind-heat" and "calm the liver and brighten the eyes."

The Ben Cao Gang Mu (Compendium of Materia Medica) by Li Shizhen describes chrysanthemum as an herb that "benefits the five viscera, harmonizes the blood, and clears the eyes." The bitterness helps drain heat downward, while the sweetness moderates the action, making it gentle enough for regular use.

How They Balance Each Other

The genius of this pairing lies in complementary action. Goji berry is neutral and nourishing — it builds the yin foundation so that the liver has the resources it needs to stay cool and calm. Chrysanthemum is cool and dispersing — it gently pushes excess heat out of the liver channel, especially heat rising toward the head and eyes.

Think of it this way: goji berry fills the well, while chrysanthemum releases the steam. One builds; one clears. Together they create a balanced, sustainable approach to liver health that can be enjoyed over the long term.

Traditional Uses

In TCM clinical practice, goji berry and chrysanthemum tea has been traditionally recommended for several interconnected patterns:

Liver Fire Rising

When liver qi becomes stagnant from stress, anger, or frustration, it can transform into heat and rise upward. Symptoms traditionally associated with liver fire include red, bloodshot eyes, a bitter taste in the mouth, headaches (often at the temples or sides of the head), irritability, tinnitus, and dizziness. This tea is a classic "liver-clearer" for these presentations.

Eye Strain and Computer Use

In the modern world, the most common reason people turn to this tea is eye strain from prolonged screen use. Staring at a computer, phone, or tablet for hours on end is a form of "overuse" that, in TCM terms, drains liver blood and liver yin — because the eyes are the "gateway" to the liver. When liver blood is depleted, the eyes become dry, tired, and blurry. Goji berries replenish liver blood and yin, while chrysanthemum clears any heat generated by that strain. The result is a tea that may support visual comfort and reduce the sensation of tired, irritated eyes.

Yin Nourishment

Beyond acute symptoms, regular consumption of this tea is traditionally viewed as a gentle yin tonic. As we age, our yin reserves naturally decline — a process that TCM associates with dryness, restlessness, and sensory decline. By supporting the Liver and Kidney yin, goji and chrysanthemum tea may be a simple, pleasant way to support healthy aging, particularly for the eyes and the nervous system.

How to Make Goji Berry and Chrysanthemum Tea

This recipe yields one generous cup (about 12 oz / 350 ml) of tea. Adjust quantities to taste.

Ingredients

  • 1 tablespoon dried goji berries (about 10–12 g)
  • 5–8 dried chrysanthemum flowers (or 1 heaping teaspoon of loose dried petals)
  • 1 teaspoon rock sugar or honey (optional — traditional recipes often include a touch of sweetness to harmonize)
  • 2 cups filtered water (about 500 ml)

Equipment

  • A small teapot, heatproof glass cup, or mug with a lid (steeping covered is important to retain the volatile compounds)
  • A fine-mesh strainer or tea infuser

Instructions

  1. Rinse the herbs. Place the goji berries and chrysanthemum flowers in a fine-mesh strainer and give them a quick rinse under cool running water to remove any dust or fine debris.
  2. Heat the water. Bring the water to a boil, then let it cool slightly for about 30 seconds. The ideal steeping temperature is around 195–205°F (90–96°C). Water that is too vigorously boiling may make the tea slightly bitter, especially with chrysanthemum.
  3. Combine and steep. Place the rinsed goji berries and chrysanthemum flowers into your teapot or cup. Pour the hot water over them. If using rock sugar, add it now so it dissolves during steeping.
  4. Cover and wait. Cover the teapot or cup with a lid or small saucer. Steep for 5–7 minutes. The longer you steep, the deeper the flavor and the more pronounced the cooling properties, but be careful not to over-steep beyond 8 minutes, as chrysanthemum can turn bitter.
  5. Strain and serve. Pour the tea through a strainer into your drinking cup. The rehydrated goji berries are edible and quite tasty — many people eat them as a little treat after finishing the tea.
  6. Enjoy warm. Sip slowly, ideally in a calm moment. This tea is traditionally enjoyed warm, not hot, to allow the body to absorb its gentle energetics.

Pro Tips

  • You can reuse the herbs for a second, slightly lighter steep — just add more hot water and steep for 5 minutes.
  • If you prefer a cold version in summer, steep double-strength (double the herbs, same water), let it cool, and serve over ice.
  • For a more concentrated medicinal brew, simmer the goji berries in the water for 3–4 minutes before adding the chrysanthemum flowers, then remove from heat and steep covered for 5 minutes.

Variations to Try

Once you are comfortable with the classic recipe, feel free to adapt it to your constitution and taste preferences. Here are several traditional variations:

Add Red Dates for Blood Nourishment

Add 2–3 pitted red dates (hong zao, also called jujube) to the steeping vessel. Red dates are sweet, warm, and enter the Spleen and Stomach meridians. In TCM, they are considered a blood tonic and work beautifully alongside goji to support liver blood. The dates add a natural sweetness that may eliminate the need for rock sugar or honey. Simply tear them open before steeping to release their flavor.

Add Licorice for Harmonizing

Add 1–2 thin slices of dried licorice root (gan cao). Licorice is known as the "harmonizer" in TCM formulas — it moderates the actions of other herbs and helps the body process them more smoothly. It also adds a gentle sweetness. Use sparingly, as licorice is quite potent and may affect blood pressure in sensitive individuals if consumed in large amounts.

Honey Instead of Sugar

If you prefer not to use rock sugar, a teaspoon of raw honey stirred in after steeping (when the tea has cooled slightly) is a wonderful substitute. Honey is considered neutral to slightly warming in TCM and adds a rich, floral sweetness that complements both goji and chrysanthemum nicely.

Goji + Chrysanthemum + Pu-Erh

For those who enjoy a more robust flavor, steep this tea with 1 teaspoon of aged pu-erh tea. Pu-erh is believed to support digestion and help the body process fats, making this a good after-meal variation.

Best Time to Drink

Timing matters in TCM, and when you drink this tea can enhance its effects:

  • Afternoon (1–3 PM): In the Chinese organ clock, the Liver meridian is most active during the early morning (1–3 AM, restful sleep time). The Small Intestine meridian is active in the early afternoon (1–3 PM), which is a supportive window for absorption. Many TCM practitioners recommend drinking liver-supporting teas in the afternoon, especially after lunch, when the body's digestive energy is strong.
  • After prolonged screen time: This is the most intuitive time. Whenever your eyes feel tired, strained, or dry from looking at a computer, phone, or TV, brew a cup. The combination of nourishing goji and cooling chrysanthemum is traditionally believed to directly address screen-induced eye fatigue.
  • During stressful periods: If you are feeling irritable, "hot-headed," or prone to frustration, this tea may support a sense of calm. Its cooling, downward-moving energy is traditionally associated with soothing liver fire and promoting emotional steadiness.
  • Avoid late evening: While this tea is caffeine-free (unless you add pu-erh), drinking large quantities of any liquid right before bed may interrupt sleep. Enjoy it in the afternoon or early evening at the latest.

Precautions and Considerations

While goji berry and chrysanthemum tea is generally considered safe for most people, there are a few situations where caution is warranted:

Cold Spleen and Stomach Constitution

In TCM, individuals with a "cold" or "weak" Spleen and Stomach pattern may experience loose stools, bloating, cold hands and feet, and a tendency to feel chilly. Because chrysanthemum is cool in nature, it may aggravate these symptoms if consumed in large quantities. If this describes you, try using fewer chrysanthemum flowers (3–4 instead of 5–8) and more goji berries (which are neutral and warming in comparison). You can also add 1–2 slices of fresh ginger to the steep to balance the cooling effect.

Pregnancy and Nursing

Both goji berries and chrysanthemum flowers are commonly used in TCM during pregnancy in small amounts — chrysanthemum tea is even a traditional remedy for pregnancy-related hypertension in some Chinese communities. However, there are no large-scale modern studies confirming its safety during pregnancy. If you are pregnant, planning to become pregnant, or nursing, it is always best to consult your healthcare provider or a qualified TCM practitioner before adding any herbal tea to your daily routine.

Allergies

Individuals with known allergies to plants in the Asteraceae family (including ragweed, daisies, marigolds, and sunflowers) may experience a reaction to chrysanthemum. If you have known sensitivities, try a very small amount first and discontinue use if any irritation or allergic symptoms appear.

Blood Sugar Considerations

If you have diabetes or are monitoring your blood sugar, consider omitting the rock sugar or honey. The goji berries themselves contain natural sugars, but in the quantity used for one cup of tea (1 tablespoon), the glycemic load is minimal.

Final Thoughts

Goji berry and chrysanthemum tea is a wonderful example of how TCM food therapy transforms simple, everyday ingredients into targeted wellness tools. It is gentle enough for daily sipping yet purposeful enough to address specific patterns like eye strain, irritability, and liver heat. Whether you are a longtime fan of Chinese herbal traditions or simply looking for a delicious, screen-friendly tea to add to your rotation, this classic brew is well worth a place in your kitchen cupboard.

As with any herbal practice, listen to your body. Notice how you feel after a cup — do your eyes feel more comfortable? Does your mind feel calmer? These small observations are the heart of TCM's personalized approach to wellness. And as the Shen Nong Ben Cao Jing reminds us, the best medicines are those that harmonize with the body's natural rhythms over time.
