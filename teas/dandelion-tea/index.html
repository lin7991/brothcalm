Dandelion Tea (Pu Gong Ying Cha) — Cooling Liver and Detox Brew

🍵 Tea & Tonic · 5 min read

Introduction: A Humble Herb with a Powerful Purpose

Walk across any meadow, lawn, or roadside in spring, and you will almost certainly find it — the bright yellow flower head of the common dandelion. What many dismiss as a stubborn weed has been revered for centuries in Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM) as Pu Gong Ying (蒲公英), a cooling, detoxifying herb with a remarkable range of traditional applications.

Dandelion tea, brewed from the root, leaves, or both, is one of the simplest and most accessible ways to incorporate this potent herb into your wellness routine. Valued for its ability to cool the Liver, clear internal heat, and support the body's natural detoxification pathways, Pu Gong Ying tea holds a special place in the TCM apothecary. Whether you are new to Chinese herbalism or a seasoned practitioner of food therapy, this bright yet grounding brew deserves a spot in your cupboard.

TCM Properties of Dandelion (Pu Gong Ying)

Nature and Flavor

In TCM, every herb is classified by its thermal nature and flavor profile. Dandelion is considered cold in nature and possesses both bitter and sweet flavors. The cold property means it is traditionally used to reduce excess heat in the body — what TCM calls "Heat" patterns. The bitter flavor directs energy downward and inward, helping to drain and dry excess moisture and heat. The sweet flavor adds a mild tonifying quality, making it gentler than purely bitter herbs.

Meridian Entry

Pu Gong Ying is said to enter the Liver and Stomach meridians. This means its therapeutic influence flows through these organ systems. Its affinity for the Liver meridian makes it especially valued for addressing Liver Heat and Liver Fire patterns. Its connection to the Stomach meridian gives it a role in digestive health and clearing Stomach Heat.

Traditional Uses in TCM

Cooling the Liver and Detoxifying

The Liver is responsible for the smooth flow of Qi (vital energy) throughout the body in TCM. When Liver Qi becomes stagnant, it can transform into Heat or Fire, leading to symptoms such as irritability, headaches, red and dry eyes, a bitter taste in the mouth, and even skin eruptions. Dandelion tea is traditionally used to clear Liver Heat and support the Liver's detoxification functions. It is believed to help the body process and eliminate toxins more effectively, particularly when the burden on the liver is high — after heavy meals, during seasonal transitions, or when stress has been building.

Support for Breast Health (Ru Yong)

One of dandelion's most recognized traditional applications in TCM is for breast health, specifically a condition called ru yong (乳痈), which refers to breast abscesses and mastitis. Pu Gong Ying's cooling, detoxifying, and swelling-dispersing properties make it a go-to herb in TCM formulas for addressing breast lumps and inflammation. When taken as a tea during early signs of breast discomfort, it is traditionally believed to help resolve heat and toxins in the breast meridian. This use is so well established that Pu Gong Ying appears in classic TCM formulas dedicated to breast health.

Supporting Digestion and Clearing Stomach Heat

Dandelion's entry into the Stomach meridian makes it useful for digestive complaints rooted in heat. If you experience acid reflux, a burning sensation in the stomach, bad breath, or a tendency toward mouth ulcers, these may be signs of Stomach Heat in TCM. A cup of dandelion tea after a rich meal is traditionally believed to help cool the digestive tract, stimulate bile flow, and ease the digestion of fatty foods. Many people find it gently supportive for sluggish digestion and occasional bloating.

Skin Health and Heat Rashes

Because the skin is seen as an extension of the Lung and Liver systems in TCM, internal heat often finds its way to the surface as acne, rashes, hives, or eczema. Dandelion tea's blood-cooling and detoxifying properties are traditionally used to address skin conditions that stem from internal Heat and toxins. Drinking the tea regularly during flare-ups of heat-related skin issues may help calm the underlying fire and support clearer skin.

How to Make Dandelion Tea

With Fresh Dandelion

If you have access to a clean, pesticide-free patch of dandelions (far from roadsides and treated lawns), fresh leaves and roots make a wonderfully vibrant tea. Gather the leaves and root, wash them thoroughly, and roughly chop them. Use about 10–15 grams of fresh herb per cup (approximately a generous handful). Add to 300 ml of just-boiled water and steep for 8–12 minutes. The fresh leaf tea is light and grassy; the fresh root yields a slightly earthier, more grounding cup.

With Dried Dandelion

Dried dandelion is more concentrated and convenient. You can find dried dandelion root or leaf at most Chinese herbal shops, health food stores, or online. Use 3–5 grams of dried herb per cup (about 1–2 teaspoons). Pour 300 ml of boiling water over the herb, cover, and steep for 5–10 minutes. Strain and enjoy. For a stronger decoction — especially from the root — you can simmer the herb in a small pot of water for 15 minutes before straining.

Drinking Tips

Dandelion tea has an earthy, mildly bitter flavor that many come to appreciate, especially when they associate it with the feeling of lightness and clarity it brings. If you find the bitterness strong, try these tips:

  • Add a thin slice of fresh ginger to warm and balance the cold nature
  • Sweeten with a small amount of raw honey or rock sugar
  • Shorten the steeping time to 3–4 minutes for a milder cup
  • Blend it with other herbs as suggested in the variations below

Variations to Try

Dandelion and Goji Berry Tea

Combine the cooling detox of dandelion with the nourishing sweetness of goji berries (gou qi zi). Goji berries are warming and tonify the Liver and Kidney meridians, making this a beautifully balanced brew. Add 8–10 goji berries to your dandelion tea while steeping. The natural sweetness of the berries rounds out the bitterness beautifully.

Dandelion and Licorice Tea

Licorice root (gan cao) is known in TCM as the "harmonizer" — it moderates the harshness of other herbs and adds a natural sweetness. A small slice of dried licorice root (about 1–2 grams) added to your dandelion tea mellows the bitter edge while also supporting the Spleen and Stomach. This combination is especially pleasant for those new to herbal teas.

Dandelion and Jujube Tea

Red jujube dates (da zao) are a staple in Chinese food therapy, prized for their ability to tonify the Spleen, nourish the blood, and calm the spirit. Adding 2–3 pitted jujubes to your dandelion infusion creates a richer, slightly sweet brew that is both detoxifying and nourishing. This variation is ideal for gentle daily drinking during cooler months when you want some of dandelion's detox benefits without over-chilling the body.

Dandelion and Mint Cooler

For warm weather, steep dandelion tea as usual, let it cool to room temperature, and add a small handful of fresh mint leaves (bo he). Mint also has a cooling nature in TCM and helps disperse Liver Qi stagnation. This iced variation makes a refreshing afternoon beverage that supports both digestion and a calm mood.

Who Should Be Careful with Dandelion Tea

Because dandelion is cold in nature, it is not ideal for everyone. Individuals with a cold or deficient Spleen and Stomach constitution — those who frequently have cold hands and feet, loose stools, poor appetite, or feel chilled easily — should use dandelion tea sparingly or avoid it. Its cooling nature may further weaken their digestive fire.

If you have a known allergy to dandelion or related plants in the Asteraceae family (such as ragweed, chrysanthemums, or marigolds), exercise caution. Pregnant and nursing women should consult a qualified TCM practitioner or healthcare provider before incorporating dandelion tea into their routine, especially in concentrated forms. Those taking diuretic medications or lithium should also seek professional guidance, as dandelion has notable diuretic properties.

Conclusion: A Cup of Cooling Wisdom

Dandelion tea — Pu Gong Ying Cha — is a beautiful example of how TCM transforms what nature freely offers into a tool for wellness. Its cooling, detoxifying, and liver-supporting properties have earned it a time-honored place in the Chinese herbal tradition, and its accessibility makes it one of the easiest herbal teas to prepare at home.

Whether you drink it to cool an irritable Liver after a stressful week, to support digestion after a heavy meal, or simply as a grounding daily ritual, this humble brew carries centuries of wisdom in every cup. Approach it with respect for its potency, awareness of its cooling nature, and an open curiosity about what this golden-flowered herb can offer you.

As with any herbal remedy, listen to your body. If dandelion tea feels too cold or bitter for your constitution, consider the warming variations above, or explore other cooling herbs that align better with your unique pattern. Your body is the ultimate guide.
