Ginger Scallion Tea (Sheng Jiang Cong Cha) — The Classic Cold Remedy

🍵 Tea & Tonic · 5 min read

Introduction: The First Defence Against the Sniffles

There is a moment everyone recognises: you feel a slight chill in the air, a tickle at the back of your throat, and a weariness that settles into your bones. In Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM), this is known as wind-cold invasion — the moment when external cold energy enters the body's protective layer. And across Chinese kitchens, there is one simple, time-honoured response: a steaming cup of ginger scallion tea.

Sheng Jiang Cong Cha (生姜葱茶), or ginger scallion tea, is a pungent, warming infusion that has been used for generations as a first-line comfort remedy at the earliest signs of a cold. Made from just three humble ingredients — fresh ginger, scallions (spring onions), and brown sugar — it is perhaps the simplest and most beloved tea in the Chinese home medicine cabinet.

In this article, we explore what makes ginger scallion tea so effective from a TCM perspective, how to prepare it properly, when to drink it, and what to keep in mind.

What Is Ginger Scallion Tea?

Ginger scallion tea is a hot infusion made by simmering or steeping sliced fresh ginger and chopped scallions (spring onions) in water, then sweetening the result with brown sugar (or sometimes rock sugar). It is not a true "tea" in the sense of Camellia sinensis — it contains no tea leaves — but rather a medicinal tisane or herbal decoction.

The drink is deeply rooted in Chinese food therapy (shi liao, 食疗), where everyday ingredients are classified by their thermal nature and energetic properties. Ginger and scallions are both considered pungent and warming — ingredients that are believed to encourage the body to release cold pathogens through sweating, restoring the body's equilibrium.

TCM Properties: Warming, Pungent, and Expelling

In TCM theory, the common cold is often attributed to an invasion of external pathogenic factors — mainly wind, cold, or heat. When the invading factor is wind-cold, symptoms typically include:

  • Sneezing with clear, watery nasal discharge
  • Mild chills and a desire for warmth
  • Slight headache, often at the back of the neck or temples
  • Body aches and fatigue
  • No thirst, or a preference for warm drinks
  • Thin, white coating on the tongue

Ginger scallion tea is traditionally used for this exact presentation. Here is how each ingredient contributes in TCM terms:

Fresh Ginger (Sheng Jiang, 生姜)

Fresh ginger is one of the most widely used herbs in Chinese food therapy. It is classified as warm, pungent, and dispersing. In TCM, ginger is believed to enter the Lung, Spleen, and Stomach meridians, where it helps release the exterior, warm the middle burner, and stop nausea. When used in a decoction for colds, its pungent nature is thought to open the pores and encourage sweating — the primary mechanism for "expelling" wind-cold.

Scallion (Cong Bai, 葱白)

The white part of the scallion — known as cong bai in Chinese herbalism — is considered pungent and warm, with an affinity for the Lung meridian. In TCM, scallion is believed to have a strong ability to "release the exterior" and help push cold pathogens out. It is considered gentler than ginger but essential for the synergistic effect of the formula. The green tops are sometimes included too, though the white part is traditionally emphasised.

Brown Sugar (Hong Tang, 红糖)

Brown sugar (unrefined cane sugar) is classified as warm and sweet in TCM. It is not merely a sweetener — it is thought to harmonise the formula, nourish the blood, and ease the acrid taste of ginger and scallion. In the context of a cold remedy, it provides quick energy and makes the tea palatable, especially for children.

The Classic Recipe

Making ginger scallion tea at home requires no special equipment and takes under 15 minutes. The proportions below yield one generous serving.

Ingredients

  • 3–4 thin slices of fresh ginger (about 10 grams / ⅓ oz), unpeeled but washed
  • 3–4 scallions (spring onions), white part only or white + light green — sliced lengthwise and bruised gently with the flat of a knife
  • 1–2 tablespoons brown sugar (adjust to taste)
  • 1½ cups (approx. 350 ml) water

Instructions

  1. Slice the ginger. Cut the fresh ginger into thin coins or matchsticks — do not peel the skin, as it is considered warming itself in TCM. Lightly crush the slices with the side of your knife to release more of the active constituents.
  2. Prepare the scallions. Trim off the roots and any dark green tops. Slice the white and light green parts lengthwise into thin strips. Gently bruise them with the flat of a knife blade to open the fibres.
  3. Simmer. Place the ginger and scallions in a small saucepan with the water. Bring to a boil, then reduce the heat to low and simmer, covered, for 8–10 minutes. The liquid should develop a pale golden colour and a pungent, comforting aroma.
  4. Add brown sugar. Stir in the brown sugar until fully dissolved. Taste and adjust — the tea should be pleasantly sweet with a noticeable ginger kick.
  5. Strain and serve. Pour through a fine-mesh strainer into your favourite mug. Drink it while it is still hot, and then bundle up — ideally, get into bed and allow yourself to rest and sweat gently.

When to Drink It: The Window of Opportunity

In TCM, timing is everything. Ginger scallion tea is considered most effective when taken at the very first hint of wind-cold invasion — within hours, not days. The principle is to "sweat out" the pathogen before it settles deeper into the body.

If your symptoms match the wind-cold pattern described above — and particularly if you have just been exposed to cold wind, rain, or air conditioning — drink a cup of ginger scallion tea immediately, then rest under a blanket. Traditional TCM practice encourages drinking the tea and then lying down to encourage a light sweat. This is believed to help release the exterior and allow the body to return to a balanced state.

Variations to Try

While the classic recipe is beautifully simple, there are several common variations depending on availability and preference:

Ginger, Scallion, and Red Date (Jujube) Tea

Add 2–3 dried red dates (hong zao, 红枣) to the simmering water. Red dates are classified as warm and sweet in TCM and are believed to support the Spleen and Stomach, adding a mild sweetness and an extra layer of nourishment to the formula.

Ginger Scallion Tea with Goji Berries

Add a small handful of goji berries (gou qi zi, 枸杞子) in the last 3 minutes of simmering. Goji berries are considered neutral to slightly warm in TCM and are traditionally used to support the Liver and Kidney systems. This is a nice variation when you feel run-down as well as chilly.

Ginger and Scallion Broth (Savory)

Replace the brown sugar with a pinch of salt and a splash of tamari or light soy sauce. This savoury version is sometimes used as a gentle warming broth rather than a sweet tea. It works well as part of a light meal when appetite is low.

With Miso

After straining the ginger and scallion liquid, stir in a teaspoon of white miso paste (do not boil the miso). This adds probiotics and a savoury umami note, making the tea more nourishing while keeping its warming character.

Cautions and Considerations

Ginger scallion tea is a safe and gentle remedy when used appropriately, but it is not for everyone or every situation. Here are important considerations:

When to Avoid Ginger Scallion Tea

  • Wind-heat patterns. If your symptoms include a high fever, sore throat with redness, swollen tonsils, thick yellow phlegm, or a yellow tongue coating, the pathogen is considered "heat" in TCM. Warming remedies like ginger scallion tea are traditionally avoided in these cases, as they may worsen heat symptoms.
  • Yin deficiency with heat signs. People who tend toward dry mouth, night sweats, hot palms and soles, or a red tongue with little coating may find ginger scallion tea overly drying and heating.
  • Excessive sweating. If you are already sweating heavily, the sweating-promoting nature of ginger scallion tea may be excessive and potentially depleting.
  • Pregnancy. Ginger in culinary amounts is generally considered safe during pregnancy, but large amounts of ginger should be used with caution. Consult a healthcare provider before using any herbal remedy during pregnancy.

What If I Sweat Too Much?

In TCM, the goal is a gentle, light sweat — not profuse perspiration. If you find yourself sweating heavily or feeling weak after drinking the tea, you may have used too much ginger or drunk too large a quantity. Reduce the amount of ginger next time or stick to a single cup.

How Long to Continue

Ginger scallion tea is intended for the acute, early stage of a cold — typically 1 to 2 days. If symptoms persist beyond 48 hours, worsen, or are accompanied by a high fever, it is important to consult a healthcare professional. The tea is not intended as a daily preventive drink over the long term.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is ginger scallion tea in TCM?

Ginger scallion tea (sheng jiang cong cha) is a traditional Chinese herbal infusion made from fresh ginger, scallions (spring onions), and brown sugar. In TCM, it is classified as a warming, pungent remedy believed to expel wind-cold from the body's exterior. It is most commonly used at the first signs of a common cold when symptoms suggest cold invasion — such as chills, nasal congestion, and body aches.

When should I drink ginger scallion tea?

Ginger scallion tea is traditionally drunk at the very first signs of a wind-cold invasion — symptoms like sneezing, runny nose with clear discharge, chills, slight headache, and general fatigue. In TCM, timing matters: the sooner you drink it after symptoms appear, the more effective it is believed to be. It is not recommended for heat-type conditions, such as sore throats with redness, yellow phlegm, or high fever.

Can I drink this tea if I have a sore throat?

It depends on the type of sore throat. In TCM, a sore throat accompanied by chills, clear nasal discharge, and no fever may still indicate wind-cold, and ginger scallion tea may be appropriate. However, if the sore throat involves redness, swelling, yellow phlegm, or a high fever, these are signs of wind-heat, and a warming remedy like ginger scallion tea is traditionally avoided, as it could aggravate the condition.

How much ginger scallion tea per day?

Traditionally, 1 to 2 cups (about 250–500 ml) of ginger scallion tea per day is considered sufficient when symptoms first appear. It is meant to be taken in the early stages of a cold, not as a long-term daily beverage. Drinking more than 2 cups may lead to excessive sweating or internal heat. If symptoms persist beyond a few days, it is advisable to consult a healthcare practitioner.

Can children drink ginger scallion tea?

A milder version of ginger scallion tea may be suitable for children in small quantities, using less ginger and less brown sugar. However, children's constitutions differ from adults', and in TCM theory, children are often considered more prone to heat patterns. It is advisable to start with a very small amount (a few tablespoons) and observe how the child responds. Always consult a paediatrician or qualified TCM practitioner before giving herbal remedies to children.
