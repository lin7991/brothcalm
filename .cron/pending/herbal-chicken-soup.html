Herbal Chicken Soup — Nourishing Qi and Blood the TCM Way

🥘 Recipe · 9 min read

Introduction: A Bowl of Ancient Wisdom

There are few things as comforting as a bowl of chicken soup when you are feeling run down, cold, or simply in need of something deeply nourishing. In Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM), this instinct reaches its fullest expression in herbal chicken soup (药膳鸡汤 — yao shan ji tang), a restorative broth that combines the warming essence of chicken with carefully selected medicinal herbs to support the body's vital energy — what TCM calls Qi — and nourish its Blood.

Unlike a quick weekday soup, this is a slow, intentional dish. Every ingredient is chosen for a purpose. The chicken provides a structural foundation of nourishment. The herbs are added not just for flavor but to gently guide the body back toward balance. The result is a golden, fragrant broth that warms you from the inside out and leaves you feeling replenished for hours after the last spoonful.

This article explores the TCM principles behind herbal chicken soup, provides a complete step-by-step recipe, and explains how different herb combinations can support different wellness goals.

The TCM Philosophy Behind Herbal Chicken Soup

In TCM, food is medicine, and medicine is food. Every ingredient carries a thermal nature (hot, warm, neutral, cool, or cold) and a characteristic flavor (sour, bitter, sweet, pungent, or salty), along with a specific affinity for particular organ systems known as meridians.

Chicken: The Warming Foundation

Chicken is considered a warming, sweet food in TCM. It is said to enter the Spleen, Stomach, and Kidney meridians, making it an ideal base for building Qi and Blood. Traditionally, chicken is believed to strengthen the digestive system (Spleen and Stomach), warm the middle burner (the body's core), and help restore energy after illness, fatigue, or prolonged stress.

For the most nourishing broth, TCM texts recommend using an older hen rather than a young broiler. An older bird has a stronger life force and yields a richer, more gelatinous broth — one that carries a deeper concentration of nutrients and flavor.

The Herbal Additions: A Synergistic Formula

The herbs in this soup are not thrown together haphazardly. They follow a TCM formulation logic: one or two chief ingredients address the primary concern, while supporting herbs harmonize the formula and direct its effects to the right places in the body.

Complete Recipe: TCM Herbal Chicken Soup

This recipe yields approximately 6 servings and takes about 2 hours from start to finish. Most of that time is hands-off simmering, so you can go about your day while the broth works its magic.

Ingredients

  • 1 whole chicken (about 1.5–2 kg / 3–4 lbs) — preferably an older hen or free-range bird; quartered or left whole
  • 15–20 g dried astragalus root (黄芪) — in thick, flat slices; these release slowly into the broth
  • 30 g goji berries (枸杞) — about 2 heaping tablespoons; add toward the end to preserve their color and nutrients
  • 6–8 red dates (红枣) — pitted, to release their sweet harmonizing energy
  • 4–5 slices fresh ginger (about 0.3 cm / ⅛ inch thick) — no need to peel; just wash and slice
  • 2 litres water (about 8 cups) — or enough to cover the chicken by 2–3 cm
  • Salt to taste — preferably sea salt or rock salt; add at the end
  • Optional: 10 g dried codonopsis (党参 — Dǎng Shēn) — a gentler alternative to ginseng for Qi deficiency
  • Optional: 3–5 g dried ginseng root (人参 — Rén Shēn) — for a stronger Qi tonic effect; use sparingly
  • Optional: 2–3 cloves garlic — smashed; adds warmth and immune-supporting properties
  • Optional: sliced carrots or daikon radish — adds sweetness and variety to the broth

Step-by-Step Instructions

Step 1: Prepare the Chicken

Rinse the chicken inside and out under cold water. Remove any excess fat, the tail end, and the neck if included (you can save the neck and feet for the broth — they add wonderful gelatin). If using a whole bird, you may leave it intact or cut it into quarters for easier handling. Pat the chicken dry with paper towels.

Step 2: Blanch the Chicken (飞水 — Fēi Shuǐ)

Place the chicken in a large stockpot and cover with cold water. Bring to a boil over medium-high heat. Let it boil for 3–5 minutes — you will see foam and impurities rise to the surface. Drain the chicken and discard the water. Rinse the chicken pieces briefly under warm water to remove any clinging foam. This step removes impurities and results in a clearer, cleaner-tasting broth — a hallmark of well-made TCM soups.

Step 3: Rinse the Herbs (Except Goji Berries)

Give the astragalus slices, red dates, and any optional herbs (codonopsis or ginseng) a quick rinse under cold water to remove dust. You can also briefly soak the astragalus to rehydrate it slightly. Keep the goji berries separate — they go in later.

Step 4: Assemble and Simmer

Return the blanched chicken to a clean pot. Add the rinsed astragalus, red dates, ginger slices, and any optional codonopsis or ginseng. Pour in enough fresh cold water to cover the chicken by about 2–3 cm (roughly 8 cups / 2 litres).

Bring to a boil over high heat, then immediately reduce to the gentlest simmer — you want tiny bubbles breaking the surface, not a rolling boil. Cover the pot partially with a lid (leave a small gap for steam to escape).

Simmer for 1.5 to 2 hours. The chicken should be tender enough to fall apart with a gentle prod, and the broth should have taken on a warm amber color from the herbs. Skim off any fat or foam that rises during the first 30 minutes.

Step 5: Add Goji Berries

About 10–15 minutes before the end of cooking, add the goji berries. Adding them too early can cause them to break apart and turn bitter. They only need a brief steep to soften and release their sweet, slightly tart flavor into the broth.

Step 6: Season and Finish

Remove the pot from the heat. Season with salt to taste — start with 1 teaspoon and adjust gradually. Remember that the broth will concentrate slightly as it sits, so season conservatively at first. If you added carrots or daikon, they will have contributed natural sweetness by now.

Remove the astragalus slices before serving (they are woody and not meant to be eaten). You can shred the chicken meat and return it to the broth, or serve the chicken pieces whole alongside the broth. Traditionally, the meat is eaten as part of the meal — it has absorbed the herbal essence during the long simmer.

Step 7: Serve and Enjoy

Ladle the hot broth into bowls, making sure each serving gets a few goji berries, a red date, and some shredded chicken. Garnish with a sprinkle of fresh chopped scallions or cilantro if desired. Enjoy the soup as a main meal with steamed rice or simply on its own as a nourishing restorative.

Variations for Different Wellness Goals

One of the beauties of TCM herbal soup is that the formula can be adjusted to your individual constitution and seasonal needs. Here are three common variations:

Qi Tonic Variation (for fatigue and low energy)

If you feel consistently tired, have a weak appetite, or catch colds easily, you may have what TCM calls Spleen Qi Deficiency. Double the astragalus to 30 g and add 10 g of codonopsis. You can also include 5–6 sliced shiitake mushrooms, which are traditionally believed to strengthen Qi and support healthy immune function.

Blood Tonic Variation (for pale complexion and dryness)

If you experience dry skin, brittle nails, dizziness, or a pale complexion, the focus shifts to nourishing Blood. Add 15 g of angelica sinensis (当归 — Dāng Guī), known as the "woman's ginseng" for its Blood-nourishing properties, and 10 fresh longan berries (龙眼 — Lóng Yǎn), which are sweet, warm, and support the Heart and Spleen. Increase the goji berries to 40 g.

Warming Winter Variation (for cold hands and feet)

For those who feel perpetually cold, especially in the extremities, increase the ginger to 8–10 slices and add 3–4 smashed garlic cloves. Include 1 small piece of dried tangerine peel (陈 皮 — Chén Pí), about the size of a thumbnail, which helps regulate Qi and warm the Stomach. This version is especially popular during the deep winter months.

When to Enjoy Herbal Chicken Soup

In TCM, timing matters as much as ingredients. Herbal chicken soup is traditionally consumed:

  • During autumn and winter — when the body needs warming, grounding foods to conserve energy
  • In the early stages of recovery — after a cold, flu, or any illness that has depleted Qi and appetite
  • During transitional seasons — spring and autumn are times when Wei Qi (defensive energy) is vulnerable, and a nourishing soup can help the body adapt
  • Weekly as a preventive tonic — many TCM families make a large batch of herbal soup once a week during the colder months, sharing it with the whole household

Because the soup is nutrient-dense and mildly warming, it is not typically consumed in large quantity during the height of summer or by people with signs of excess heat (such as constant thirst, red face, or irritability). As with all TCM food therapy, the key is balance and listening to your body.

Final Thoughts

Herbal chicken soup is far more than a recipe — it is a bridge between the ancient wisdom of TCM and the modern kitchen. It asks only for a few quality ingredients, a pot, time, and a quiet intention to nourish. In a world that rushes from meal to meal, this soup invites you to slow down, to simmer, and to care for yourself the way generations before us understood: through food that feeds both the body and the spirit.

Whether you are new to TCM or returning to a familiar tradition, we hope this recipe becomes a staple in your cold-weather rotation. Share it with the people you love, adapt it to your needs, and let the warmth of these herbs do what they have done for centuries — help you feel whole again.
