Cinnamon (Rou Gui) — Warming Qi, Boosting Circulation

🌿 Ingredient Deep Dive · 7 min read

Introduction

Few spices are as beloved — and as misunderstood — as cinnamon. In kitchens around the world, it is the warm, sweet note in baked apples, holiday pastries, and spiced lattes. But in Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM), cinnamon occupies a far more profound role. Known as Rou Gui (肉桂), it is one of the most important warming herbs in the Chinese apothecary, prized not for its sweetness but for its ability to reach deep into the body and kindle the body's inner fire.

Rou Gui has been used in China for over two thousand years. It appears in the Shen Nong Ben Cao Jing (Divine Farmer's Materia Medica), the oldest surviving Chinese pharmacopoeia, where it is classified as a superior herb — one that is both nourishing and safe for long-term use. Its reputation as a herb that "warms the middle" and "returns fire to its source" makes it a cornerstone of many classic formulas, particularly those addressing cold conditions and weak digestive fire.

In this deep dive, we will explore the energetics of Rou Gui, its traditional uses, how to choose and prepare it, and how to incorporate it safely into your wellness routine.

TCM Properties of Rou Gui

In the TCM framework, every herb and food has a distinct nature (气, qì), taste (味, wèi), and meridian affinity (归经, guī jīng). These properties determine how the substance interacts with the body's energetic landscape.

Nature and Taste

Rou Gui is classified as strongly warming in nature with an acrid-sweet taste. The acrid (pungent) quality gives it the ability to move and disperse — it pushes stagnant energy outward and helps the body expel cold. The sweet quality, meanwhile, is nourishing and harmonising, lending a mild tonic effect that prevents the warmth from becoming too harsh.

This combination of warm-acrid-sweet makes Rou Gui uniquely suited to conditions of Cold and Deficiency — when the body lacks enough Yang energy to keep itself warm and the digestive system struggles to transform food into vital energy.

Meridian Affinity

Rou Gui primarily enters three meridians:

  • Heart (心): Warming Heart Yang helps circulate blood and calm the spirit. In TCM, the Heart houses the Shen (spirit), and when Heart Yang is sufficient, one feels grounded, clear-headed, and emotionally steady.
  • Kidney (肾): The Kidneys are the root of Yin and Yang for the entire body. Rou Gui is one of the few herbs that can warm the Gate of Vitality (命门, Mìng Mén) — the source of primordial Yang energy stored between the Kidneys. This makes it invaluable for Kidney Yang deficiency.
  • Spleen (脾): The Spleen governs digestion and transportation of nutrients. Warming Spleen Yang helps transform food into Qi and Blood, resolving issues like poor appetite, bloating, and loose stools triggered by Cold.

Traditional Uses in TCM

Rou Gui is referenced in dozens of classical formulas, but its most important indications can be grouped into a few key categories. These uses have been documented and refined over centuries of Chinese medical practice.

Warming Yang and Fortifying the Body's Fire

The most celebrated use of Rou Gui is its ability to warm the body's Yang energy, especially Kidney Yang. When Kidney Yang is deficient, a person may feel persistently cold — especially in the lower back and knees — have low energy, frequent urination, a pale tongue, and a weak, deep pulse. Classic symptoms of what TCM calls "declining Fire at the Gate of Vitality."

Rou Gui appears in famous formulas such as You Gui Wan (Restore the Right [Kidney] Pill) and Jin Gui Shen Qi Wan (Kidney Qi Pill from the Golden Cabinet), where it works alongside other warming herbs like Prepared Aconite (Fu Zi) to revive the body's internal warmth. In these formulas, Rou Gui is the herb that "leads fire back to its source" — it guides Yang energy downward to where it belongs, in the lower abdomen and Kidneys.

Boosting Circulation and Moving Blood

Warmth moves. Cold congeals. In TCM, insufficient Yang energy leads to poor circulation because the body lacks the motive force to push Blood and Qi through the channels. Rou Gui's warming, acrid nature helps break up Cold-induced stagnation, allowing Blood to flow freely. This is why it is included in formulas for conditions like cold hands and feet, chest pain due to Cold obstruction, and painful menstruation with dark, clotted blood.

When used in small daily amounts — as in cooking — it gently supports peripheral circulation without being overly dispersing. This makes it an excellent spice for anyone who tends to run cold, especially in the winter months.

Supporting Digestion and the Middle Burner

A cold Spleen and Stomach cannot effectively break down food. This shows up as poor appetite, a sensation of cold in the abdomen, bloating after eating, loose stools, and a craving for warm foods and drinks. Rou Gui warms the Middle Burner (the digestive region between the diaphragm and navel), rekindling the digestive fire so that food is properly transformed into Qi and Blood.

In TCM dietary therapy, Rou Gui is commonly added to congee, soups, and braises for this very reason. A simple cinnamon-ginger tea before a meal can prepare the digestive system to receive and process food more efficiently, especially on cold days or for those with naturally weak digestion.

Practical Guide: Choosing and Using Rou Gui

Now that we understand the theory, let's talk about the practical side — how to choose good-quality cinnamon, the difference between cassia and Ceylon, and how to incorporate it into your daily routine.

Cassia vs. Ceylon: Which One to Use?

This is one of the most common questions in the kitchen apothecary. In TCM, Rou Gui refers specifically to the bark of Cinnamomum cassia (Chinese cassia). It is thicker, darker, and has a stronger, more pungent flavour than Ceylon cinnamon (Cinnamomum verum). Cassia sticks are typically thicker and harder to break, often made from a single layer of bark rolled into a quill.

Ceylon cinnamon is lighter in colour, thinner, and more crumbly, with a milder, sweeter flavour. While both are true cinnamon, they are not interchangeable in a TCM context. For medicinal effect, you want cassia — specifically high-quality Rou Gui from China or Vietnam. Ceylon is better suited for everyday culinary use in larger amounts, especially if you are sensitive to coumarin (which is present in higher levels in cassia).

Our recommendation: Keep both on hand. Use cassia / Rou Gui for therapeutic cooking, congee, and TCM-style preparations. Use Ceylon for baking, smoothies, and everyday sprinkling where you want the flavour without the full medicinal potency.

How to Identify Quality Rou Gui

High-quality Rou Gui has these distinguishing characteristics:

  • Thick, dense quills that are difficult to snap with bare hands
  • Reddish-brown colour on the exterior, with a slightly lighter interior
  • Strong, sweet-pungent aroma that is immediately warming and penetrating
  • Taste: sweet at first, then distinctly pungent and slightly numbing on the tongue
  • Oily sheen when cut or broken, indicating a high concentration of essential oils

Avoid thin, pale, or crumbly sticks — they have likely lost their volatile oils and will have little therapeutic effect. Store Rou Gui in an airtight glass jar away from direct sunlight and heat. Properly stored, whole cinnamon sticks will retain their potency for up to two years.

How to Use Rou Gui in Daily Cooking

Rou Gui is remarkably versatile. Here are some of the simplest and most effective ways to use it:

  • Cinnamon tea: Add a 2-inch piece of cassia stick to a mug, pour boiling water over it, and steep for 10–15 minutes. Drink warm, especially on cold mornings or after meals to aid digestion.
  • Congee: Add a whole cinnamon stick to the pot while cooking rice congee (jook). It infuses the porridge with gentle warmth and pairs beautifully with ginger and dates.
  • Braised dishes: Rou Gui is essential in Chinese braised dishes like Hong Shao Rou (red-braised pork belly). Add a stick to the braising liquid along with star anise, ginger, and soy sauce — it cuts through the richness and aids digestibility.
  • Soups and stews: Drop a stick into any slow-simmered soup or bone broth for subtle warmth and depth.
  • Ground cinnamon: Use sparingly in oatmeal, porridge, baked goods, or smoothies. For therapeutic effect, whole sticks are preferred because the essential oils are better preserved until the moment of use.

Dosage Guidelines

In therapeutic TCM preparations, Rou Gui is typically decocted at 1.5–4.5 grams per day (as a decoction or powder added at the end of cooking, since its volatile oils can be driven off by prolonged boiling). For daily dietary use, a 2-inch stick per pot of tea or soup is a safe and effective amount.

If using ground cinnamon for everyday sprinkling, limit yourself to about ½ to 1 teaspoon per day of cassia. Ceylon cinnamon can be used more freely — up to 1–2 tablespoons per day is considered safe — but always listen to your body's signals.

Cautions and Contraindications

While Rou Gui is generally safe when used in food amounts, it is a potent herb and carries some important cautions:

  • Yin Deficiency with Heat Signs: Rou Gui is strongly warming and can aggravate conditions where there is already too much heat in the body — symptoms include night sweats, a red tongue with little coating, feeling hot in the palms and soles, and a thirst for cold drinks. In these cases, it is best avoided.
  • Excess Heat Conditions: During a fever, acute sore throat, or any hot, inflamed condition, warming herbs like Rou Gui can make things worse. Hold off until the acute phase has resolved.
  • Bleeding or Blood-Heat: Because Rou Gui moves Blood, it is traditionally contraindicated in conditions involving bleeding or a tendency to bruise easily, such as certain menstrual disorders with excessive bleeding.
  • Pregnancy: Small amounts used in cooking are generally considered safe during pregnancy, but concentrated medicinal doses should be avoided, especially in the first trimester. As with any herb used during pregnancy, it is wise to consult a qualified practitioner.
  • Liver Concerns: Cassia cinnamon contains coumarin, which in very high doses may affect liver function. Individuals with existing liver conditions should use cassia sparingly and consider Ceylon as a milder alternative for daily use.

As always, the principle of "the right herb for the right person" applies. If you are unsure whether Rou Gui is appropriate for your constitution, consult a licensed TCM practitioner before using it in therapeutic amounts.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between Rou Gui and cinnamon commonly found in supermarkets?

In TCM, Rou Gui refers specifically to the thick, reddish-brown bark of Cinnamomum cassia, which has a stronger, more pungent warming action than the common Ceylon cinnamon (Cinnamomum verum) used in Western baking. Cassia cinnamon contains higher levels of coumarin and is considered more medicinally potent — this is the variety traditionally used in Chinese herbal formulas. Ceylon cinnamon is milder and safer for daily culinary use in larger amounts.

How do I use Rou Gui in cooking and tea?

Rou Gui is typically used as a whole stick (quill) simmered in broths, congees, and braised dishes, or steeped in hot water as a warming tea. For tea, add a 2-inch piece of cinnamon stick to boiling water and steep for 10–15 minutes. In cooking, add a stick to slow-cooked soups, stews, or braised meats and remove before serving. Ground cinnamon can be sprinkled into oatmeal, smoothies, or baked goods, though the whole stick releases flavour more gradually and is preferred in TCM preparations.

When should I avoid using cinnamon or Rou Gui?

Rou Gui is strongly warming and should be used sparingly — or avoided — in cases of Yin deficiency with heat signs (night sweats, red tongue, feeling overheated), excess heat conditions (fever, severe sore throat), or bleeding disorders. During pregnancy, small amounts used in cooking are generally considered safe, but concentrated medicinal doses should be avoided. People with liver conditions may also wish to limit cassia cinnamon due to its coumarin content. When in doubt, consult a qualified TCM practitioner.

How do I identify good-quality Rou Gui?

High-quality Rou Gui comes in thick, dense quills with a reddish-brown colour, a strong aromatic fragrance, and a distinctly sweet-and-pungent taste. The bark should be thick enough that it is difficult to break by hand. Avoid thin, pale, or crumbly pieces, which indicate lower potency. Premium cassia cinnamon from China or Vietnam is preferred for TCM use. Store in an airtight container away from sunlight to preserve its essential oils.
