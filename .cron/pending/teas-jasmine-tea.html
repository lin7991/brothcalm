Jasmine Tea (Mo Li Hua Cha) — Aromatic Calm for Mind and Spirit

🍵 Tea & Tonic · 6 min read

Introduction

Few teas capture the imagination quite like Jasmine Tea — known in Chinese as Mò Lì Huā Chá (茉莉花茶). Its delicate floral fragrance and subtly sweet taste have made it one of China's most beloved aromatised teas for centuries. But jasmine tea is more than a perfumed beverage. In the tradition of Chinese food therapy and Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM), jasmine tea is valued not only for its exquisite aroma but also for its gently cooling, calming influence on the body and mind.

Jasmine tea is typically made by layering fresh jasmine blossoms (Jasminum sambac) with high-quality green tea leaves. The leaves absorb the floral essence through a repeated scenting process that can take several days, resulting in a cup that is both refreshing and deeply soothing. Whether enjoyed as a morning ritual or an afternoon pause, jasmine tea offers a moment of aromatic tranquillity in a busy world.

This article explores jasmine tea through the lens of TCM — its traditional properties, time-honoured uses, and practical brewing guidance so you can welcome this fragrant tea into your own wellness routine.

TCM Properties of Jasmine Tea

In Traditional Chinese Medicine, every food and herb is classified according to its thermal nature, flavour, and the organ systems (meridians) it influences. Jasmine tea is no exception.

Thermal Nature: Cool

Jasmine tea is traditionally considered cool in nature. This cooling quality makes it especially suitable for individuals who tend to run warm or who experience symptoms of internal heat — such as irritability, restlessness, a red complexion, or a dry throat. In TCM, cool-natured foods and teas are believed to gently clear excess heat from the body and restore a sense of equilibrium. The green tea base, itself cool-natured, contributes to this effect.

Flavour: Sweet

The primary flavour associated with jasmine tea in TCM is sweet (gān). Sweet flavours are traditionally thought to harmonise the body, build energy (Qi), and nourish the middle burner (the digestive system). The gentle sweetness of jasmine tea, imparted both by the tea leaves and the jasmine blossoms, makes it a naturally balancing beverage — neither overly bitter nor sharply astringent.

Meridians: Liver, Spleen, and Lung

Jasmine tea is believed to primarily enter three meridians:

  • Liver (Gān): The Liver is responsible for the smooth flow of Qi throughout the body. When Liver Qi becomes stagnant, it can manifest as irritability, tension, or frustration. Jasmine tea's aromatic nature is traditionally thought to encourage the smooth circulation of Liver Qi, promoting a calm and even emotional state.
  • Spleen (Pí): In TCM, the Spleen is central to digestion and the transformation of food into energy. Jasmine tea's sweet flavour and gentle warmth (despite its cool nature) are said to support the Spleen's functions, aiding in the healthy breakdown and assimilation of nutrients.
  • Lung (Fèi): The Lungs govern the skin and the respiratory system and are closely linked to the emotions of grief and sadness. Jasmine's aromatic compounds are traditionally said to open the Lung meridian, supporting clear breathing and a light, uplifted spirit.

Traditional Uses in Chinese Culture

Jasmine tea holds a special place in Chinese tea culture, particularly in the Fujian province, where it has been produced for over 700 years. Its traditional uses extend far beyond simple refreshment.

Calming the Shen (Spirit)

Perhaps the most cherished traditional use of jasmine tea is its ability to calm the Shen. In TCM, the Shen is the spirit or mind that resides in the Heart. When the Shen is disturbed, one may experience anxiety, insomnia, or mental restlessness. The fragrant, cooling nature of jasmine tea is traditionally believed to gently anchor the Shen, bringing a sense of peace and clarity. A warm cup of jasmine tea in the evening (for those not sensitive to caffeine) is a simple ritual that signals the nervous system to settle — a practice that modern aromatherapy research, which explores the calming effects of linalool and other volatile compounds in jasmine, is beginning to understand on a chemical level.

Aiding Digestion

In Chinese food therapy, jasmine tea is frequently enjoyed after meals. Its pleasant aroma and slightly warming effect on the Spleen meridian are thought to help harmonise the stomach and promote healthy digestion. The green tea base also provides a mild bitterness that traditional Chinese dietary theory associates with supporting digestive function. Sharing a pot of jasmine tea after a rich or heavy meal remains a common practice in many Chinese households.

A Symbol of Welcome and Hospitality

Beyond its medicinal uses, jasmine tea has long been offered as a gesture of welcome. Its beautiful scent and clear, pale liquor represent purity and sincerity. In many Chinese restaurants, a complimentary pot of jasmine tea is served at the beginning of a meal — a tradition rooted in the belief that the tea prepares both the palate and the spirit for nourishment.

How to Brew Jasmine Tea

Brewing jasmine tea well is simple, but a few key details can transform a good cup into an exceptional one. Because jasmine tea is delicate — both in flavour and in the craftsmanship required to scent it — gentle handling is essential.

Water Temperature

The most common mistake when brewing jasmine tea is using water that is too hot. Jasmine tea is typically made with a green tea base, and green tea leaves are easily scorched by boiling water. Aim for 75–80°C (167–176°F). If you do not have a temperature-controlled kettle, simply let boiling water cool for 2–3 minutes before pouring it over the leaves.

Leaf-to-Water Ratio

Use approximately 2–3 grams of loose jasmine tea leaves per 200 ml (7 oz) of water. This is roughly one heaped teaspoon. Adjust to taste — more leaves will yield a stronger floral note and a slightly fuller body.

Steeping Time

Steep for 2–3 minutes for the first infusion. Jasmine tea can be re-steeped 2–3 times, with each subsequent infusion needing a slightly longer steep (30–60 seconds more) and water that is marginally hotter. Discover which infusion delivers the flavour profile you enjoy most — many tea drinkers find the second steep more rounded and fragrant than the first.

Best Practices

  • Use a porcelain gaiwan or a glass teapot — these materials are neutral and allow the jasmine aroma to shine without absorbing or imparting flavours.
  • Pre-warm your vessel by swirling a little hot water inside and discarding it before adding the leaves. This ensures the water temperature remains stable when you pour.
  • Do not over-steep. Leaving the leaves in hot water too long releases bitter tannins from the green tea base, masking the delicate jasmine fragrance.
  • Store your jasmine tea in an airtight container away from light, heat, and strong odours. Unlike some aged teas, jasmine tea is best enjoyed fresh — within 6–12 months of purchase.

Variations of Jasmine Tea

While jasmine tea most commonly refers to jasmine-scented green tea, several delightful variations exist.

Jasmine Green Tea (Classic)

The classic variety, made with green tea leaves (usually from Fujian's famous tan bei gui style). It offers a balanced profile: the grassy, slightly vegetal notes of green tea woven together with the heady sweetness of jasmine blossoms. This is the jasmine tea most people know and love.

Jasmine White Tea

A more delicate variation made with white tea buds instead of green tea leaves. The result is an exceptionally light, sweet, and floral brew with lower caffeine content. Jasmine white tea is ideal for those who find green tea too astringent or who prefer a subtler flavour profile. In TCM terms, white tea is even cooler in nature than green tea, making this version especially suitable for those with significant internal heat.

Cold-Brew Jasmine Tea

For warm weather, cold-brewing jasmine tea produces a remarkably smooth and fragrant iced tea with virtually no bitterness. Simply add 5 grams of jasmine tea leaves to 1 litre of cold, filtered water. Cover and refrigerate for 6–8 hours (or overnight). Strain and serve over ice. Cold brewing extracts the floral aromatic compounds gently while minimising the release of tannins, yielding a cup that is sweet, refreshing, and beautifully clear.

Precautions

Jasmine tea is a gentle, well-tolerated beverage enjoyed by millions daily. However, a few sensible precautions apply.

Caffeine Content: Because the base is typically green tea, jasmine tea contains caffeine — approximately 20–40 mg per 200 ml cup, depending on leaf quantity and steep time. This is roughly a third to a quarter of the caffeine in coffee. Those who are sensitive to caffeine, pregnant, or nursing may wish to limit consumption to one or two cups earlier in the day. Decaffeinated jasmine teas are available but are less common and may lack some of the aromatic complexity of traditionally produced versions.

TCM Considerations: Individuals with a constitution that is already very cold or deficient (feeling cold easily, weak digestion, frequent loose stools) may wish to enjoy jasmine tea in moderation rather than as a daily staple. Its cool nature, while beneficial for those with excess heat, could potentially unbalance someone with pre-existing cold patterns. For such individuals, adding a small piece of ginger or a drizzle of raw honey can warm the cup and temper its cooling effect.

General Advice: As with any tea, it is best to enjoy jasmine tea between meals rather than with meals if you are concerned about iron absorption, as the tannins in green tea can inhibit non-heme iron uptake. A squeeze of lemon or a vitamin-C-rich accompaniment can help offset this effect.

Frequently Asked Questions

What makes jasmine tea different from other floral teas?

Unlike teas that are simply blended with dried flower petals, traditional jasmine tea undergoes a multi-day scenting process where fresh jasmine blossoms are layered with tea leaves. The leaves naturally absorb the floral essential oils, resulting in a deeper, more integrated fragrance that cannot be achieved by simple blending.

Can I drink jasmine tea every day?

Yes, for most people jasmine tea is perfectly safe for daily consumption in moderate amounts (2–4 cups per day). As with any caffeinated beverage, listen to your body and adjust intake based on your individual constitution and sensitivity.

Is jasmine tea the same as jasmine pearl tea?

Jasmine pearl tea is a specific variety of jasmine green tea where the leaves are hand-rolled into small pearls. When steeped, the pearls unfurl, releasing their aroma gradually. Both are jasmine-scented, but pearl teas are usually considered higher-grade and produce a more nuanced flavour.

Is jasmine tea suitable for children?

In moderation, a very light cup of jasmine tea is generally considered safe for older children. However, because of its caffeine content, it is best to offer it in small quantities and not in the evening. Herbal caffeine-free tisanes may be a more suitable option for younger children.
