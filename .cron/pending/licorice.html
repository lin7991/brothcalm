Licorice (Gan Cao) — The Harmonizer in TCM Formulas

🌿 Ingredient Deep Dive · 7 min read

Introduction

If you have ever tasted a traditional Chinese herbal decoction, you know that most are intensely bitter — a complex, earthy bitterness that can be challenging for the uninitiated palate. Yet almost every classical formula contains a secret ingredient that softens the experience, moderates the harshness of stronger herbs, and ties the entire prescription together. That ingredient is licorice — known in Mandarin as Gan Cao (甘草, literally "sweet herb").

In Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM), licorice holds a unique and exalted position. It is called the "Harmonizer" (He Yao, 和药) because of its extraordinary ability to mediate between other ingredients, smooth out aggressive actions, and guide the formula's effects in a balanced way. While it may seem modest compared to more dramatic herbs like ginseng or astragalus, licorice is arguably the most frequently used herb in the entire TCM pharmacopeia — it appears in hundreds of classical formulas and countless folk remedies.

Licorice root (Glycyrrhiza uralensis or Glycyrrhiza glabra) has been employed in both Eastern and Western herbal traditions for millennia. In Egypt, licorice was found in the tomb of Pharaoh Tutankhamun. In ancient Greece, Dioscorides prescribed it for respiratory and digestive complaints. But it is in China that licorice was elevated to an art form — classified as a superior herb in the Shen Nong Ben Cao Jing (《神农本草经》), the foundational Han-dynasty text on herbal medicine, where it was described as a substance that "tones the viscera, strengthens the sinews and bones, increases Qi, and promotes longevity."

In this deep dive, we will explore the TCM personality of licorice — its nature, flavor, and organ affinities — along with practical guidance on how to use it in your daily kitchen and the important precautions every thoughtful user should know.

TCM Properties of Licorice

Licorice has one of the most distinctive energetic signatures in the TCM materia medica. It is simultaneously gentle and powerful, simple and multifaceted.

Flavor: Sweet (甘, Gan)

The sweet flavor (gan, 甘) in TCM is associated with the Earth element and corresponds to the Spleen and Stomach organ system. Sweet-flavored substances are inherently tonifying — they build Qi and Blood, harmonize the middle burner (digestive system), and moderate acute or urgent conditions. Licorice's sweetness is exceptionally potent: a single thin slice can sweeten an entire pot of tea. This intense sweetness is the first clue to its harmonizing nature — it literally "sweetens" whatever it touches, both in flavor and in energetic action.

Nature: Neutral (平, Ping)

Licorice is classified as neutral in temperature, with a slight tendency toward cooling in its raw form. This neutral nature makes it one of the most versatile herbs in TCM. Unlike strongly warming or cooling ingredients, licorice can be incorporated into formulas for virtually any constitution. It neither adds excessive heat nor introduces cold, which is precisely why it works so well as a harmonizer — it does not disturb the thermal balance of the formula it joins.

Meridian Affinity: Spleen, Lung, and Heart

Licorice enters three meridians: Spleen, Lung, and Heart. This multi-meridian reach explains its wide range of traditional applications:

  • Spleen meridian: Supports digestion, strengthens Qi production, and helps the body transform food into usable energy.
  • Lung meridian: Moistens the respiratory system, soothes coughs, and supports the Lung's role in circulating protective Qi.
  • Heart meridian: Calms the spirit (Shen, 神), moderates palpitations, and harmonizes the Heart's function in governing Blood circulation.

Traditional Uses

The Shen Nong Ben Cao Jing describes Gan Cao as a herb that "treats the five viscera and the six bowels, harmonizes the nature of all herbs, and removes toxins from the hundred medicines." This dense classical description encapsulates the three primary roles of licorice in TCM practice.

Harmonizing Formulas (调和诸药, Tiao He Zhu Yao)

This is licorice's most famous role. In TCM, individual herbs have distinct natures, flavors, and directions of action. Some are harsh or toxic in isolation. Others have conflicting actions that need to be reconciled within a single formula. Licorice is believed to mediate between competing energies, reduce the toxicity of stronger herbs, moderate the intensity of aggressive herbs, and prolong the action of the formula so its effects are sustained rather than abrupt. It is the diplomat of the herbal world — it does not dominate but instead creates conditions for harmony among all the ingredients.

A perfect example is the classic formula Ma Huang Tang (Ephedra Decoction), where ephedra's powerful sweating action is moderated by licorice, preventing excessive fluid loss while still allowing the formula to achieve its intended effect. In Si Jun Zi Tang (Four Gentlemen Decoction), licorice harmonizes with ginseng, atractylodes, and poria to gently build digestive Qi without creating stagnation.

Tonifying Spleen Qi (补脾益气, Bu Pi Yi Qi)

Licorice is traditionally considered a gentle Qi tonic, particularly for the Spleen and Stomach. In TCM, the Spleen is responsible for transforming food into Qi and Blood. When Spleen Qi is deficient, symptoms may include fatigue, poor appetite, loose stools, bloating after meals, and a tendency to catch colds easily. Licorice is believed to strengthen the Spleen's transforming and transporting functions, helping the body extract more nourishment from food. This is why it appears in so many foundational Qi-tonifying formulas, often paired with ginseng or astragalus.

For daily food therapy, honey-fried licorice (Zhi Gan Cao) is the preferred form for this purpose, as the honey processing is thought to enhance its spleen-tonifying properties while making it gentler on digestion.

Moistening the Lungs and Stopping Cough (润肺止咳, Run Fei Zhi Ke)

Licorice has a long history of traditional use for respiratory comfort. In TCM theory, the sweet, moistening nature of licorice is believed to nourish Lung Yin and soothe irritated respiratory tissues. It is a classic ingredient in formulas for dry cough, scratchy throat, and hoarseness. Raw licorice is generally preferred for this purpose, as it is considered more effective for clearing Heat from the Lung meridian.

A simple traditional remedy is a tea made from licorice and sliced fresh ginger — the licorice soothes the throat while ginger warms and circulates. For dry, irritable coughs, licorice is often combined with loquat leaf (Pi Pa Ye), apricot kernel (Xing Ren), or Fritillaria bulb (Chuan Bei Mu).

Clearing Heat and Detoxifying (清热解毒, Qing Re Jie Du)

Raw licorice is traditionally used to clear Heat-toxin from the body — a concept in TCM that encompasses inflammatory conditions, skin eruptions, sore throats, and foodborne irritations. It is a common ingredient in traditional remedies for sore throats, mouth ulcers, and digestive inflammation. The classic formula Gan Cao Xie Xin Tang (Licorice Drain the Heart Decoction) uses licorice in combination with other herbs to address what TCM calls "Heat in the Stomach and Intestines."

In food therapy, a small amount of raw licorice is sometimes added to broths and soups during the summer months, as its cooling, detoxifying nature is believed to help the body handle the seasonal heat.

How to Use Licorice in Your Kitchen

Licorice is remarkably easy to incorporate into daily cooking and beverages. A little goes a remarkably long way, and its natural sweetness can reduce or eliminate the need for added sugar in many preparations.

Forms of Licorice

Licorice is available in several forms, each suited to different uses:

  • Raw licorice slices (Sheng Gan Cao, 生甘草): Thin, pale yellow discs of dried root. Best for teas, decoctions, and preparations where you want a cooling, detoxifying effect. Sold in Asian grocery stores and TCM herb shops.
  • Honey-fried licorice (Zhi Gan Cao, 炙甘草): Raw slices that have been stir-fried with honey until golden brown. This processing is believed to enhance the Qi-tonifying and spleen-supporting properties while softening the slightly drying nature of raw licorice. It has a deeper, richer sweetness and is preferred for soups and daily food therapy.
  • Licorice powder: Ground licorice root. Convenient but less common in TCM food therapy — the slices are preferred because they release their properties gradually and can be easily removed before serving.
  • Deglycyrrhizinated licorice (DGL): A processed form with the glycyrrhizin compound removed, often sold in Western health food stores. While DGL may be gentler on blood pressure, many TCM practitioners prefer whole licorice for its full spectrum of constituents.

Licorice Tea

The simplest way to use licorice is as a tea. Place 2 to 3 thin slices of raw or honey-fried licorice in a cup, pour boiling water over them, and steep for 5 to 7 minutes. The resulting tea is naturally sweet, amber-colored, and soothing. For variety, combine licorice with:

  • Ginger slices: A warming, comforting tea for cool weather.
  • Goji berries and red dates: A nourishing, blood-building tea for daily wellness.
  • Chrysanthemum flowers: A cooling summer tea that supports eye health.
  • A single slice of dried tangerine peel (Chen Pi): A digestive tea that harmonizes the Stomach.

In Soups and Broths

Add 3 to 5 thin slices of licorice (preferably honey-fried) to any long-simmered soup or broth. The licorice will infuse the liquid with a subtle sweetness and its harmonizing energy. It pairs beautifully with chicken soup, bone broth, and vegetable stews. Simply add the slices at the beginning of cooking and remove them before serving, just as you would a bay leaf. A pot of licorice-enhanced broth is one of the most accessible entry points into TCM food therapy.

In Congee and Porridge

A single slice of honey-fried licorice added to congee or oatmeal as it cooks imparts a gentle sweetness and digestive-supporting quality. Remove the slice before serving. This is a traditional way to introduce licorice into the diet for those recovering from illness or experiencing digestive weakness.

Dosage Guidelines

In food therapy, licorice is used in small amounts:

  • Tea: 2 to 3 thin slices (about 2-3 grams) per cup, up to 1 to 2 cups per day.
  • Soups and broths: 3 to 5 thin slices (about 3-5 grams) per pot of 4 to 6 servings.
  • Decoctions (herbal preparations): 3 to 9 grams per day, typically in combination with other herbs, under the guidance of a TCM practitioner.

Remember that licorice is potent — more is not better. Its sweetness can become cloying, and its active compounds, when consumed in excess, may lead to unwanted effects (see Precautions below).

Precautions

Licorice is one of the most widely used herbs in TCM and is generally considered safe when used appropriately. However, it is not suitable for everyone, and there are important considerations to keep in mind.

  • Hypertension (high blood pressure): Licorice contains glycyrrhizic acid, which may cause the body to retain sodium and water while excreting potassium. Prolonged use or large amounts of licorice may contribute to elevated blood pressure. If you have hypertension or are taking blood pressure medication, consult your healthcare provider before using licorice regularly.
  • Edema and fluid retention: Because licorice may influence fluid and electrolyte balance, individuals with edema, heart failure, or kidney disease should use it with caution and under professional guidance.
  • Pregnancy and breastfeeding: While small culinary amounts of licorice are generally considered safe, concentrated or daily use during pregnancy is not recommended. Some traditional sources advise against licorice during pregnancy due to its potential effect on fluid balance and hormone regulation. If you are pregnant or nursing, please consult your healthcare provider.
  • Long-term continuous use: Licorice is traditionally used in cycles rather than continuously month after month. Extended daily use — especially at higher doses — may lead to low potassium levels, fluid retention, and blood pressure changes. A general guideline is to use licorice for two to three weeks at a time, followed by a break of at least one week.
  • Potassium deficiency (hypokalemia): Individuals already low in potassium, or those taking diuretics or corticosteroids, should be especially cautious with licorice, as it may further lower potassium levels.
  • TCM constitutional caution: In TCM theory, individuals with Spleen Damp-Heat (symptoms may include a greasy tongue coating, feeling of heaviness, sticky stools, or skin breakouts) should use licorice in moderation, as its sweet, moistening nature may exacerbate Dampness. Pairing it with drying herbs or ingredients such as aged tangerine peel (Chen Pi) can help balance this effect.

FAQ

Q: What is the difference between raw licorice (Sheng Gan Cao) and honey-fried licorice (Zhi Gan Cao)?
Raw licorice (Sheng Gan Cao) is dried but otherwise unprocessed. It is traditionally believed to be more effective for clearing Heat, detoxifying, and addressing sore throats and swellings. Honey-fried licorice (Zhi Gan Cao) is stir-fried with honey until golden. The honey processing is thought to enhance its spleen-tonifying and Qi-supplementing properties while moderating its drying nature. For food therapy and daily use, honey-fried licorice is generally preferred as it is gentler on the digestive system.

Q: Can I drink licorice tea every day?
Occasional licorice tea is generally considered safe, but daily long-term use is not recommended. Licorice contains glycyrrhizin, a compound that may affect blood pressure and potassium levels when consumed in large amounts or over extended periods. It is traditionally advised to use licorice in cycles — for example, two to three times per week for a few weeks, followed by a break. If you have high blood pressure, kidney disease, low potassium, or are pregnant, it is best to consult a healthcare provider before consuming licorice regularly.

Q: Is licorice the same as anise or star anise?
No, they are entirely different plants. Licorice (Glycyrrhiza uralensis or Glycyrrhiza glabra) is a leguminous root with a naturally sweet taste. Star anise (Illicium verum) is an eight-pointed spice from a tree in the magnolia family, and anise (Pimpinella anisum) is a seed from the Apiaceae family. They are sometimes confused because all share a sweet, aromatic licorice-like flavor compound (anethole), but they belong to different botanical families and have very different TCM properties and uses.

Q: How much licorice is safe to use in cooking and tea?
For culinary and tea purposes, a small amount of licorice goes a long way. A typical dose is 2 to 5 grams (roughly 1 to 3 thin slices) per day when used as a tea or in soups. In TCM decoctions, licensed practitioners may use up to 6 to 12 grams in combination with other herbs. As a general guideline, use licorice sparingly — its sweetness is potent, and a single thin slice is often enough to sweeten an entire pot of tea or to harmonize a pot of soup.

Q: Can licorice help with coughs and sore throats?
Licorice has a long history of traditional use for respiratory comfort. In TCM, raw licorice is believed to clear Heat and soothe the throat, making it a classic ingredient for dry coughs and sore throats. It is often combined with other ingredients in traditional formulas. The soothing, moistening quality of licorice may help calm irritated throat tissues. However, if you have persistent coughing or a chronic sore throat, please consult a healthcare professional.
