Cooling Foods for Internal Heat (清热) — A TCM Guide

🥗 Food Therapy · 7 min read

What Is "Internal Heat" in TCM?

In Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM), the concept of internal heat — or nèi rè (内热) — refers to a pattern of imbalance in which the body's Yang energy is believed to become excessive relative to its Yin energy. This is not the same as a fever caused by an infection. Rather, it is a functional description of how certain symptoms cluster together from a TCM perspective.

When internal heat builds up, it is traditionally understood to disrupt the body's natural equilibrium. A person experiencing this pattern may notice signs such as:

  • A persistent feeling of warmth or heat in the body
  • Increased thirst and a desire for cold drinks
  • Irritability or a short temper
  • Flushed skin or red eyes
  • Acne breakouts or skin rashes
  • Dryness — dry mouth, dry throat, or dry stools
  • Constipation with hard, dry stool
  • A red tongue with a yellow coating (as observed by a TCM practitioner)

Internal heat can arise from many sources. In TCM, dietary habits are a major factor: consuming too many spicy, greasy, or deep-fried foods, along with excessive alcohol or caffeine, is thought to generate heat. Emotional stress, overwork, lack of sleep, and living in a hot climate can also contribute. The goal of using cooling foods is to gently restore balance — not by "fighting" the heat, but by nourishing the Yin and supporting the body's own regulatory mechanisms.

TCM Food Energetics: Cooling vs. Warming

At the heart of TCM dietary therapy lies the concept of food energetics. Every food is believed to carry a thermal nature — one of cold (寒, hán), cool (凉, liáng), neutral (平, píng), warm (温, wēn), or hot (热, rè). These classifications are not based on the physical temperature of the food, but on the effect it is traditionally thought to have on the body after digestion.

Cooling foods (寒凉性质) are believed to clear heat, reduce inflammation, calm the mind, and moisten dryness. Warming foods, by contrast, are thought to invigorate circulation, strengthen digestion, and dispel cold. A balanced TCM diet aims to harmonise these energies according to an individual's constitution and the season.

In the TCM framework, consuming cooling foods when internal heat is present is seen as a way to support the body's natural cooling mechanisms — much like drinking water when you feel thirsty. The key is to match the food's nature to the body's current state, rather than treating any food as universally "good" or "bad."

Cooling Foods to Know

The following foods are traditionally classified in TCM as having cooling or cold thermal natures. They are widely used in Chinese food therapy to help clear heat and promote a sense of calm.

Watermelon (西瓜, Xī Guā)

Watermelon is considered one of the most cooling fruits in TCM. It is traditionally used to relieve summer heat, quench thirst, and support the body's fluid levels. The white part of the rind (just under the green skin) is especially valued and can be stir-fried or pickled.

Cucumber (黄瓜, Huáng Guā)

Cucumber has a cool, moist nature and is believed to help clear heat and promote urination, which in TCM is a pathway for eliminating internal heat. It is often eaten raw in salads or infused in water for a gently cooling drink.

Mung Bean (绿豆, Lǜ Dòu)

Mung bean is a classic cooling ingredient in TCM. A simple mung bean soup (绿豆汤, lǜ dòu tāng) is a traditional summer remedy for heat-related discomfort. It is believed to clear heat and support the body in dealing with hot weather. Mung beans can also be sprouted and used in stir-fries.

Bitter Gourd (苦瓜, Kǔ Guā)

Bitter gourd, also known as bitter melon, has a strong cooling nature. As its name suggests, it has a pronounced bitter flavour, which in TCM is associated with clearing heat and drying dampness. It is often stir-fried with eggs or stuffed and steamed.

Pear (梨, Lí)

Pears are valued in TCM for their moistening and cooling properties. They are traditionally used to soothe a dry, irritated throat and support lung health. Pears can be eaten raw or gently poached with a small amount of rock sugar.

Chrysanthemum (菊花, Jú Huā)

Chrysanthemum flowers are brewed as a tea and are traditionally believed to cool the body, calm the mind, and support eye health. A cup of chrysanthemum tea is a popular choice in Chinese households, especially during warmer months or after a long day in front of screens.

Green Tea (绿茶, Lǜ Chá)

Green tea is classified as cool in TCM and is believed to clear heat, refresh the mind, and support digestion. Unlike black tea or coffee — which are considered warming — green tea is traditionally recommended for those who feel overheated or irritable.

Tofu (豆腐, Dòu Fu)

Tofu, made from soybeans, has a cool and neutral nature. It is believed to clear heat, moisten dryness, and support the stomach. Soft or silken tofu is especially gentle and can be served cold with light seasonings or added to soups.

Seaweed (海带, Hǎi Dài)

Seaweed and kelp are considered cooling and salty in TCM. They are traditionally used to soften hardness, support the thyroid, and help the body move fluids to eliminate heat. Seaweed is commonly added to broths, salads, or simmered with tofu.

Celery (芹菜, Qín Cài)

Celery has a cool nature and is believed to help calm the Liver — which in TCM is closely linked to emotional heat, irritability, and anger. Celery can be eaten raw, juiced, or lightly stir-fried for a mild cooling effect.

How Cooling Foods May Support Wellbeing

From a TCM perspective, cooling foods work by restoring the balance between Yin and Yang when excess heat is present. Here is how they are traditionally believed to relate to common concerns:

  • Inflammation and redness: Cooling foods are thought to help the body reduce inflammatory-type responses by clearing heat from the blood and tissues.
  • Acne and skin breakouts: In TCM, skin issues are often linked to internal heat rising to the surface. Foods like mung bean, cucumber, and green tea are traditionally used to help calm this underlying heat.
  • Excessive thirst: Cooling and moistening foods such as watermelon, pear, and cucumber are believed to nourish body fluids (Jin Ye) and alleviate dryness-related thirst.
  • Irritability and restlessness: Heat in the TCM framework can affect the mind and emotions. Chrysanthemum tea and green tea are traditionally considered calming and cooling for the spirit.
  • Constipation with dry stool: When internal heat dries out the intestines, cooling and moistening foods like pear, tofu, and seaweed may help support regularity and ease discomfort.

It is important to note that these effects are based on the traditional understanding of TCM and individual experiences may vary. Cooling foods are best used as part of a well-rounded diet rather than as isolated remedies.

Simple Cooling Meal Ideas

Incorporating cooling foods into your routine does not have to be complicated. Here are a few simple suggestions inspired by TCM principles:

  • Mung Bean Soup: Simmer a handful of mung beans in water with a slice of dried tangerine peel until soft. Sweeten lightly with rock sugar or a few dried dates (红枣) for a gently cooling dessert or afternoon snack.
  • Cucumber Mint Water: Add thin cucumber slices and a few fresh mint leaves to a jug of water. Let it infuse in the refrigerator for a refreshing, cooling drink.
  • Chilled Tofu Salad: Slice silken tofu and top with chopped scallions, a drizzle of soy sauce, sesame oil, and a pinch of toasted sesame seeds. Serve cold or at room temperature.
  • Pear and Rock Sugar Stew: Core a fresh pear and place it in a small bowl with a few rock sugar crystals and a small handful of goji berries. Steam gently for about 20 minutes until soft.
  • Chrysanthemum and Goji Berry Tea: Steep dried chrysanthemum flowers and a few goji berries in hot water for 5–7 minutes. Strain and enjoy warm or at room temperature.
  • Seaweed and Tofu Broth: Simmer soaked seaweed strips and cubed silken tofu in a light vegetable or chicken stock with a slice of ginger to balance the cooling effect.

Who Should Be Cautious with Cooling Foods?

While cooling foods can be wonderfully supportive for those with signs of internal heat, they are not suitable for everyone in large amounts. In TCM, individuals with certain constitutional patterns are traditionally advised to go easy on very cold-natured foods.

People who may want to limit or balance cooling foods include those who:

  • Experience cold hands and feet even in warm weather
  • Have a tendency toward loose stools or diarrhoea
  • Feel fatigued or low in energy
  • Have a weak appetite or feel bloated after eating small amounts
  • Notice a pale tongue with a thin white coating
  • Are recovering from an illness or have a weak digestive system (Spleen Qi deficiency in TCM terms)

If any of these resonate with you, it does not mean you must avoid cooling foods entirely. Rather, you may benefit from pairing them with warming or neutral ingredients — such as a slice of ginger in your mung bean soup or a pinch of cinnamon in a pear stew — to offset the cold nature. A qualified TCM practitioner can help you understand your unique constitution and offer personalised dietary guidance.

Bringing Cooling Foods Into Your Life

Cooling foods are a beautiful example of how TCM food therapy works with the rhythms of nature and the individual body. By paying attention to how you feel — noticing when you feel hot, thirsty, or irritable — you can learn to choose foods that gently guide your body back toward balance.

In the TCM tradition, the best diet is not one that rigidly follows rules, but one that adapts to the seasons, the environment, and the ever-changing needs of the body. Cooling foods have their place, especially in warmer months or when internal heat builds up. Used wisely, they can be a simple and effective part of a holistic approach to wellness.
