Foods for Headaches — TCM Diet for Tension and Liver Fire

🥗 Food Therapy · 8 min read

Understanding Headaches Through the TCM Lens

Headaches are one of the most common health complaints worldwide, yet the way they are understood varies greatly between medical traditions. In Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM), a headache is rarely seen as a standalone condition. Instead, it is viewed as a signal — a message from the body that something is out of balance. The head is known as the "meeting point of all Yang channels," and it is especially sensitive to disruptions in the flow of Qi (vital energy), Blood, and bodily fluids.

Rather than reaching for a one-size-fits-all remedy, TCM food therapy asks a deeper question: what pattern is driving this headache? The answer determines which foods will help and which may make things worse.

There are three major internal imbalances that commonly give rise to headaches in TCM:

  • Liver Fire rising — When stress, frustration, or dietary excess creates stagnant Liver Qi that transforms into heat. This heat surges upward, producing pounding, throbbing headaches.
  • Dampness — When the Spleen is weakened by rich, greasy foods or damp environments, it fails to transform fluids properly. Dampness accumulates and "fogs" the head, producing a heavy, wrapped sensation.
  • Qi and Blood deficiency — When the body lacks sufficient nourishment, the head receives inadequate support. This often results in dull, lingering headaches that worsen with fatigue.

Understanding which pattern — or combination of patterns — is at play is the key to choosing the right foods for relief.

The Four Common TCM Patterns of Headaches

While every person is unique, most headaches fall into one of four classic TCM patterns. Identifying yours can help you make smarter dietary choices.

🔴 Liver Fire Rising — The Pounding Headache

This is one of the most common patterns in modern life. Liver Fire headaches typically feel like a throbbing or pulsating pain, often concentrated on the sides of the head or behind the eyes. They are frequently triggered by stress, anger, poor sleep, or foods that generate heat — alcohol, spicy dishes, greasy takeout. Accompanying signs may include irritability, a bitter taste in the mouth, red cheeks or eyes, constipation, and ringing in the ears. The TCM approach is to cool and calm the Liver, directing that rising heat downward.

🩸 Blood Deficiency — The Dull, Empty Headache

Blood Deficiency headaches feel different. Instead of pounding, there is a dull, aching sensation — as if the head is empty or under-supported. The pain is often worse with mental or physical exertion and may improve with rest. These headaches commonly affect women around or after menstruation. Other signs include pale complexion, dry skin and hair, dizziness, poor memory, and blurred vision. In TCM, the Liver stores Blood and the Heart governs it — both organs are involved. Nourishing, blood-building foods are the cornerstone of dietary support.

💧 Dampness — The Heavy, Cloudy Headache

If your head feels wrapped in a heavy, damp cloth — foggy, sluggish, and hard to think clearly — Dampness may be the culprit. These headaches often come with a sensation of fullness in the head, nausea, a thick or greasy tongue coating, and a feeling of heaviness throughout the body. Dampness arises when the Spleen's digestive function is compromised by too many rich, oily, sugary, or dairy-heavy foods. The treatment principle is to strengthen the Spleen and drain Dampness through drying, aromatic, or light foods.

❄️ Wind-Cold Invasion — The Back-of-the-Head Ache

This pattern often comes on suddenly after exposure to cold or wind — sitting in a draft, sleeping with a window open, or walking in cold weather without a hat. The pain is typically located at the back of the head and neck, with a tense, tight sensation. There may be chills, a low fever, and a dislike of cold. In TCM, Wind is considered a "carrier" that can bring Cold into the body's channels. Warming, dispersing foods are traditionally used to "expel" the pathogen and restore free flow of Qi.

Foods to Eat — Cooling, Nourishing, and Draining

Once you have a sense of your headache pattern, you can choose foods that gently guide the body back to balance. Below are categories organised by TCM principle.

🧊 Cooling and Calming Foods (for Liver Fire)

These are foods with a cool or cold thermal nature that help clear heat and calm the mind. They are traditionally used for pounding, throbbing headaches linked to stress and Liver Fire.

  • Chrysanthemum (ju hua) — Perhaps the most famous cooling herb for the head. Chrysanthemum tea is a classic TCM remedy for headaches with red, dry eyes or dizziness. It gently clears heat from the Liver and brightens the eyes.
  • Fresh mint (bo he) — Cooling and dispersing, mint helps relieve tension headaches and sinus pressure. A simple handful of fresh mint leaves steeped in hot water can be remarkably soothing.
  • Celery — Cool in nature, celery helps calm Liver Fire and has a natural diuretic effect that may reduce the fluid pressure associated with certain types of headaches.
  • Cucumber — Hydrating and cooling, cucumber is a gentle, everyday food that helps counter internal heat without being harsh on the digestion.
  • Pear and watermelon — These juicy fruits are cooling and nourishing to body fluids (Yin). They are especially helpful when headaches are accompanied by thirst or a dry mouth.
  • Bitter melon (ku gua) — Strongly cooling and Liver-clearing, bitter melon can be cooked into stir-fries or soups for those accustomed to its distinctive taste.

🍲 Blood-Nourishing Foods (for Blood Deficiency)

These foods are warming or neutral in nature and rich in the building blocks of healthy blood. They are traditionally used for dull, empty headaches and overall deficiency.

  • Goji berries (gou qi zi) — A premier blood-nourishing food in TCM, goji berries tonify the Liver and Kidneys while supporting the eyes. They are mild enough to add to teas, soups, or congee daily.
  • Jujube / red dates (hong zao) — Sweet, warming, and blood-nourishing, jujubes strengthen the Spleen and calm the spirit. They are often paired with goji berries in teas and soups for headache-prone individuals.
  • Spinach — A cooling, blood-nourishing green that is rich in iron. In TCM it is believed to support the Blood and moisten dryness.
  • Bone broth — Slow-simmered bone broth is nourishing to the Kidneys and builds Jing (essence), which in TCM forms the foundation of Blood. It is especially helpful after illness or for chronic deficiency headaches.
  • Black sesame seeds — Rich, warming, and deeply nourishing to the Blood and Liver. They can be ground into porridge, added to desserts, or eaten as a daily spoonful of sesame paste.
  • Longan meat (long yan rou) — Sweet and warming, longan is said to nourish the Heart and calm the Shen (spirit). It is excellent for headaches accompanied by anxiety, poor sleep, or forgetfulness.

🌾 Damp-Draining Foods (for Dampness Headaches)

If your headache feels heavy and your digestion feels sluggish, these foods can help clear Dampness and strengthen the Spleen.

  • Barley (yi yi ren / Job's tears) — One of the best-known damp-draining grains in TCM. Barley tea or barley congee can help clear the heavy, foggy sensation of a Dampness headache.
  • Adzuki beans (hong dou) — These small red beans are drying and promote urination, helping the body eliminate excess fluid. They are often cooked into soups with winter melon or combined with rice.
  • Winter melon (dong gua) — Cool and damp-draining, winter melon is gentle enough to use frequently. It is excellent in soups and pairs well with ginger to protect the Spleen.
  • Job's tears (coix seed) — Similar to barley and often used interchangeably, coix seed strengthens the Spleen and leaches Dampness. It can be cooked into congee or taken as a tea.
  • Daikon radish — Helps transform phlegm and move stagnant Qi downward, which can relieve the blocked, heavy sensation of a damp-type headache.

Foods to Avoid When You Have Headaches

Just as important as what to eat is what to avoid. In TCM, certain foods can actively trigger or worsen headaches by generating heat, creating Dampness, or disrupting Qi flow.

  • Greasy and fried foods — Deep-fried items, heavy sauces, and fatty meats create Dampness and phlegm, clouding the head and weighing down the Spleen.
  • Spicy and pungent dishes — Chilli, Sichuan pepper, garlic in excess, and hot curries can fan Liver Fire, turning a mild tension headache into a throbbing episode.
  • Alcohol — Alcohol is strongly heating and generates Dampness. It is a common trigger for both Liver Fire and Dampness-type headaches, especially red wine and beer.
  • Excessive coffee — While a small amount may temporarily constrict blood vessels, in TCM excessive coffee depletes Yin and disturbs the Shen. Over time it can worsen deficiency-type headaches and contribute to Liver Fire.
  • Processed and additive-heavy foods — Artificial preservatives, MSG, and artificial sweeteners are believed to create internal "toxins" that obstruct the channels and provoke headache episodes.
  • Very cold drinks and ice — Iced beverages shock the Spleen and create internal Cold-Dampness, which can paradoxically lead to tension-type headaches in susceptible individuals.

Simple TCM Recipes for Headache Relief

These gentle, time-tested recipes use everyday ingredients found in most Asian grocery stores or well-stocked supermarkets. They are not meant to treat acute attacks but rather to support the body's natural balance over time.

🍵 Chrysanthemum and Goji Berry Tea

This classic TCM tea is cooling, calming, and mildly blood-nourishing. It is traditionally used for headaches associated with Liver Fire — pounding pain, red eyes, irritability, and a bitter taste in the mouth.

Ingredients: 1 tablespoon dried chrysanthemum flowers, 1 tablespoon goji berries, 2-3 slices of fresh ginger (optional, to protect the stomach), 2 cups boiling water, a small rock sugar or honey to taste.

Method: Rinse the chrysanthemum and goji berries briefly. Place them in a teapot or heatproof glass. Pour boiling water over the ingredients and let steep for 5-7 minutes. Strain or sip directly. Add ginger if the tea feels too cooling for your digestion. Drink 1-2 cups a day, preferably in the afternoon when Liver Fire tends to flare.

🥬 Celery and Apple Juice

Fresh celery juice is a well-known home remedy in both TCM and Western naturopathy for calming Liver Fire and reducing fluid retention. Apple adds sweetness and a gentle cooling effect.

Ingredients: 4-5 stalks of fresh celery, 1 apple (cored, skin on), a small knob of ginger (optional).

Method: Wash all ingredients thoroughly. Juice the celery, apple, and ginger together in a juicer or blend with a little water and strain. Drink immediately on an empty stomach in the morning. This juice is strongly cooling — if you feel chilled or have weak digestion, dilute with warm water or add a pinch of cinnamon.

🍗 Goji and Jujube Chicken Soup

This nourishing soup is ideal for Blood Deficiency headaches — the dull, empty kind that come with fatigue, pale complexion, and poor memory. It is warming, rich, and deeply restorative.

Ingredients: 2 chicken thighs or drumsticks (skin on), 15 goji berries, 8-10 red jujube dates (pitted), 3 slices of fresh ginger, 4 cups water, salt to taste.

Method: Blanch the chicken pieces in boiling water for 2 minutes to remove impurities, then drain. Place the chicken, goji berries, jujube dates, and ginger in a pot with 4 cups of water. Bring to a boil, then reduce to a gentle simmer. Cover and cook for 45-60 minutes until the chicken is tender and the broth is fragrant. Season with salt just before serving. Enjoy a warm bowl in the evening, especially during or after menstruation, or whenever you feel depleted.

Lifestyle Tips to Keep Headaches at Bay

Food is powerful, but it works best alongside healthy daily habits. Here are some TCM-inspired lifestyle practices that may help reduce the frequency and severity of headaches.

😴 Prioritise Restorative Sleep

In TCM, the hours between 11 p.m. and 3 a.m. are governed by the Gallbladder and Liver channels — the time when the body performs its deepest cleansing and restoration. Going to bed before 11 p.m. gives the Liver time to cool and regulate. If Liver Fire is contributing to your headaches, late nights will make it worse. Try a simple wind-down routine: dim lights after dinner, avoid screens an hour before bed, and sip a cup of warm chrysanthemum or jujube tea.

🧘 Manage Stress with Gentle Movement

Stress is the single most common trigger for Liver Fire headaches. In TCM, suppressed emotions — especially anger, frustration, and resentment — cause Liver Qi to stagnate and eventually generate heat. Regular gentle movement such as walking in nature, Tai Chi, Qi Gong, or yin yoga helps keep Qi flowing smoothly. Even five minutes of deep abdominal breathing when you feel tension building can make a significant difference.

👐 Try Acupressure for Quick Relief

Acupressure is a safe, drug-free technique you can use on yourself. Three key pressure points are particularly helpful for headaches:

  • LI4 (Hegu) — Located in the webbing between your thumb and index finger. Press firmly with the thumb of your opposite hand for 30-60 seconds. This is a master point for head and face pain.
  • GB20 (Fengchi) — Found in the hollows at the base of the skull, between the two large neck muscles. Press upward with both thumbs for 1-2 minutes. Excellent for tension headaches and Wind-Cold patterns.
  • Taiyang (EX-HN5) — The "sun" point located in the temples, in the depression about one finger-width from the outer corner of the eye. Massage gently in small circles for 1 minute.

When to See a Doctor

TCM food therapy is a gentle, supportive approach to wellness, but it is not a substitute for professional medical care. You should see a doctor if you experience any of the following:

  • Sudden, severe headache unlike any you have had before (sometimes called a "thunderclap" headache)
  • Headache accompanied by fever, stiff neck, confusion, or difficulty speaking
  • Headache after a head injury
  • Headache that wakes you from sleep or is worse when lying down
  • Headache with vision changes, weakness, numbness, or seizure
  • Chronic daily headaches that interfere with your quality of life
  • Headaches that began after age 50 or have changed significantly in pattern

Always consult a licensed healthcare provider — whether a medical doctor, a registered TCM practitioner, or both — to get a proper diagnosis and personalised treatment plan. Dietary changes are best made under guidance, especially if you are managing an existing health condition or taking medication.
