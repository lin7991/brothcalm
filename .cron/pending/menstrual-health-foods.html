Foods for Menstrual Health — Nourishing Blood and Moving Qi

🥗 Food Therapy · 8 min read

For many women, each month brings more than just a period — it brings cramps, fatigue, mood swings, and a general sense of depletion. Western nutrition often addresses these symptoms with iron supplements, magnesium, and anti-inflammatory foods. Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM) takes a broader view, seeing the menstrual cycle as a reflection of the body's overall energetic balance — particularly the health of Blood (Xue) and Qi (vital energy).

In TCM, the Uterus is called the Extraordinary Palace, and its proper function depends on the harmonious flow of Qi and the abundance of Blood. When Blood is deficient, periods may be scanty or delayed. When Qi is stagnant, cramps and clotting appear. When Cold invades the Uterus, pain dominates the cycle. Fortunately, the kitchen offers some of the most effective remedies for each of these patterns. This guide explores the foods that TCM has traditionally relied upon to nourish Blood, move Qi, and bring ease to the menstrual cycle.

Understanding Menstrual Health Through a TCM Lens

TCM does not view menstruation as an isolated event — it is the culmination of a month-long dance between the Kidneys, Liver, Spleen, and the extraordinary vessels known as the Chong and Ren channels. The Kidneys store the Essence (Jing) that underlies fertility. The Liver stores Blood and governs the smooth flow of Qi. The Spleen transforms food into Qi and Blood. When all three work in harmony, the cycle flows smoothly.

Common menstrual complaints map directly to TCM patterns:

  • Blood Deficiency: Light flow, pale complexion, fatigue, dizziness, delayed cycle
  • Qi Stagnation: Severe cramps, dark clotted blood, breast tenderness, irritability before the period
  • Cold in the Uterus: Intense cramping that improves with warmth, lower back pain, craving for hot drinks
  • Damp-Heat: Heavy yellow discharge, pelvic heaviness, acne flare-ups around the cycle

Each pattern calls for a different dietary approach, and the beauty of TCM food therapy is that most women can begin addressing their unique pattern simply by adjusting what goes into their bowl.

Blood-Nourishing Foods (养血 — Yang Xue)

Blood is the foundation of the menstrual cycle. In TCM, Blood deficiency can result from poor diet, overwork, stress, or heavy periods that drain the body's reserves. Building Blood through food is a gentle, sustainable approach that has been practised for centuries.

Red Dates (Hong Zao — 红枣)

Red dates are perhaps the most celebrated blood-tonifying food in TCM. Sweet and warm in nature, they enter the Spleen and Stomach channels and are believed to nourish Blood, calm the spirit, and strengthen digestion. Unlike regular sugar, red dates provide a slow-release sweetness that supports the body rather than spiking insulin.

How to use: Steep 3–5 red dates in hot water for a nourishing daily tea, or simmer them in congee and soups. For a simple post-period restorative, try our goji steamed eggs — a classic Cantonese dessert that combines red dates with goji berries for deep Blood nourishment.

Goji Berries (Gou Qi Zi — 枸杞子)

Goji berries are sweet and neutral in nature, entering the Liver and Kidney channels. They are traditionally used to nourish Liver Blood, improve vision, and support reproductive vitality. Modern research suggests they are rich in polysaccharides, antioxidants, and beta-carotene — all supportive of cellular health and immune function.

How to use: A handful of goji berries can be added to soups, congees, or steeped as goji-jujube tea. They pair beautifully with Chinese yam in yam-goji soup, a nourishing dish that also supports Spleen and Kidney function.

Dark Leafy Greens

In TCM, dark leafy greens — especially spinach, beet greens, and Chinese chives — are cooling and Blood-nourishing. They are believed to enter the Liver channel and help build Blood volume. While Western nutrition values them for their iron and folate content, TCM appreciates their ability to cool excess Heat while replenishing fluids.

How to use: Lightly cook spinach or amaranth leaves and dress with sesame oil and ginger to neutralise their cooling nature. Avoid eating them raw during your period, as raw greens can introduce Cold that worsens cramping.

Black Sesame Seeds (Hei Zhi Ma — 黑芝麻)

Black sesame seeds are considered a premier anti-ageing and Blood-nourishing food in TCM. Roasted black sesame is sweet and neutral, entering the Liver and Kidney channels. It is traditionally used to tonify Liver Blood, moisten the intestines, and support hair and skin health — all markers of healthy Blood in TCM.

How to use: Grind toasted black sesame seeds into a fine powder and stir into warm congee, oatmeal, or smoothies. A teaspoon a day is a gentle, sustainable way to support Blood health over the long term.

Qi-Moving Foods (理气 — Li Qi)

When Qi is stuck, the result is pain — often sharp, stabbing cramps that come and go. Qi stagnation is one of the most common TCM diagnoses for period pain, especially in women who experience pre-menstrual irritability and breast tenderness. Foods that gently move Qi can make a profound difference in comfort.

Ginger (Sheng Jiang — 生姜)

Fresh ginger is one of the most accessible and effective Qi-moving spices. Pungent and warm, it enters the Lung, Spleen, and Stomach channels, dispelling Cold, warming the Uterus, and encouraging the smooth movement of Qi. It is the go-to remedy in TCM households for any kind of pelvic discomfort.

How to use: Slice 3–4 thin coins of fresh ginger and simmer in 2 cups of water for 10 minutes. Add a teaspoon of brown sugar or a few red dates and sip warm throughout the day. For a more substantial remedy, add ginger to herbal chicken soup — a deeply warming dish that combines ginger with astragalus and goji berries.

Mugwort (Ai Ye — 艾叶)

Mugwort is warm and pungent, entering the Liver, Spleen, and Kidney channels. It is one of the most important herbs in TCM for gynaecological health, traditionally used to warm the Uterus, move stagnant Qi, and stop menstrual pain. While often used in moxibustion (a heat therapy), mugwort can also be taken as a tea or added to soups.

How to use: Brew a tea with dried mugwort leaves (1 teaspoon per cup of boiling water), steep for 5 minutes, and sip warm. Alternatively, add a small handful of fresh mugwort to chicken or pork bone broth. Many women find mugwort tea especially helpful in the days leading up to their period.

Saffron (Fan Hong Hua — 番红花)

Saffron is warm and aromatic, known in TCM for its ability to invigorate Blood circulation, resolve Stagnation, and calm the spirit. It is considered stronger than ginger or mugwort for moving Blood, and is best reserved for cases where cramps are accompanied by dark, clotted blood.

How to use: Steep a pinch (3–5 strands) of saffron in hot water for a deeply fragrant tea. Because saffron is potent, use it only during the first day or two of your period and avoid during pregnancy.

Warming and Grounding Soups for Cycle Comfort

Herbal Chicken Soup

There is a reason chicken soup features so prominently in TCM recovery diets. Chicken is sweet and warm, entering the Spleen and Stomach channels. When simmered with Blood-nourishing herbs like astragalus, red dates, goji berries, and ginger, it becomes a comprehensive tonic for depleted Qi and Blood.

Simple recipe: Simmer 2 chicken thighs with 3 slices of ginger, 5 red dates, a handful of goji berries, and 10g of astragalus (huang qi) in 1.5 litres of water for 1.5 hours. Season with a pinch of sea salt. Enjoy a warm bowl daily for 3–5 days after your period ends to rebuild reserves. A full guide is available in our herbal chicken soup recipe.

Pumpkin and Jujube Congee

Congee — a savoury rice porridge — is the ultimate easily digestible meal for the menstrual phase when digestive fire is low. Pumpkin is sweet and warm, building Qi and harmonising the Middle Burner. Jujubes (red dates) add Blood-nourishing sweetness.

Simple recipe: Cook ½ cup of rice with 4 cups of water, 200g diced pumpkin, and 5 pitted red dates on low heat for 40 minutes, stirring occasionally. Serve warm with a drizzle of sesame oil. Our pumpkin-jujube congee recipe provides exact instructions.

Foods to Avoid During Your Period

Equally important as what to eat is what to avoid. TCM is very clear about foods that can disrupt menstrual health:

  • Raw and cold foods: Salads, ice water, cold-pressed juices, frozen yoghurt, and sushi introduce Cold into the body. The Uterus is especially vulnerable to Cold, which causes blood vessels to constrict and Qi to stagnate.
  • Greasy and fried foods: These create Dampness and Phlegm, which can lead to heavy, clotted periods and pelvic heaviness.
  • Excessive dairy: In TCM, dairy is considered Damp-producing and can contribute to heavy discharge, bloating, and sluggish digestion — especially when consumed cold.
  • Caffeine and alcohol: Both disrupt the Liver's ability to maintain smooth Qi flow. Caffeine can aggravate irritability and disrupt sleep, while alcohol can create internal Heat that flares inflammatory symptoms.

During your period, aim to eat warm, cooked foods that are easy to digest. A bowl of warm congee or soup is far more supportive than a raw salad or smoothie, no matter how healthy it seems.

Building a Monthly Cycle-Supportive Diet

TCM food therapy is not about rigid rules — it is about listening to the body and eating in harmony with its natural rhythms. Here is a simple framework to follow across your cycle:

  • During the period: Warm, simple foods. Ginger tea, congee, chicken broth, and mugwort tea. Rest and keep meals small and easily digestible.
  • Post-period (follicular phase): The best time to build Blood. Red dates, goji berries, black sesame, dark leafy greens, and nourishing soups like pork trotter soup.
  • Mid-cycle (ovulation): Light, fresh foods. Steamed vegetables, fish, and moderate portions. Avoid overeating.
  • Pre-period (luteal phase): Qi-moving foods. Ginger, mugwort, saffron, fennel seeds, and warming spices. This is the time to encourage smooth flow and prevent stagnation.

The Bigger Picture: Beyond the Plate

While food is a powerful tool, TCM emphasises that menstrual health is influenced by the whole of a woman's life — her stress levels, sleep quality, emotional wellbeing, and movement habits. Eating well supports a healthy cycle, but it works best alongside adequate rest, gentle exercise like walking or qigong, and emotional self-care. A warm bowl of goji-jujube tea is even more effective when sipped in a quiet moment of rest.

As always, consult a qualified TCM practitioner for personalised advice, especially if you experience severe pain, very heavy bleeding, or cycle irregularities that persist. Food therapy is a beautiful complement to professional care — not a replacement for it.
