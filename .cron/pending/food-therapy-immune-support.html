Pork Trotter and Peanut Soup — Collagen for Joints and Skin

🥘 Recipe · 8 min read

Introduction

In the world of Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM), few dishes are as revered for their restorative power as pork trotter and peanut soup (猪蹄花生汤). This humble yet deeply nourishing broth has been simmered in Chinese kitchens for centuries, valued not just for its rich, savory flavor but for its ability to support joints, bones, and skin health from the inside out.

The star ingredient — pork trotter — is one of the richest natural sources of gelatin and collagen. When slowly simmered, it releases these compounds into the broth, creating a thick, silky texture that TCM practitioners associate with building strong connective tissue and replenishing the body's structural foundation. Combined with peanuts, red dates, and ginger, this soup becomes a complete tonic for the kidneys, spleen, and blood.

Whether you are recovering from an illness, managing joint discomfort, or simply looking for a warming winter meal, pork trotter and peanut soup is believed to offer gentle, cumulative support for the body's deeper layers.

Supporting Ingredients

Red Dates (红枣 — Hóng Zǎo): A classic blood tonic that harmonizes the formula. Red dates strengthen the spleen, nourish the blood, and add a subtle sweetness that balances the savory broth. In TCM, they are believed to calm the spirit (安神) when taken regularly.

Ginger (生姜 — Shēng Jiāng): Slices of fresh ginger warm the spleen and stomach, aid digestion, and help the body absorb the rich gelatin more easily. Ginger's warming nature also balances the neutral-to-slightly-cool nature of the pork.

Goji Berries (枸杞 — Gǒu Qǐ): Often added near the end of cooking, goji berries nourish the liver and kidneys, support vision, and add a gentle antioxidant boost to the finished soup.

Health Benefits in TCM

Kidney and Joint Support

The kidneys store essence (精) and govern the bones. Pork trotter is believed to supplement this kidney essence, helping to maintain strong bones and flexible joints. For those experiencing age-related stiffness or recovering from joint injuries, this soup is traditionally recommended as a gentle, restorative food.

Skin Health and Collagen

In TCM, the lungs govern the skin. The gelatin-rich broth is said to "moisten the lungs" and, by extension, hydrate the skin from within. The collagen released during slow cooking provides the building blocks for skin elasticity, and the red dates and goji berries help nourish the blood, giving the complexion a healthy glow.

Postpartum Recovery

Pork trotter soup is a traditional postpartum tonic in Chinese culture. It is believed to support lactation, replenish blood lost during childbirth, and help the mother regain strength. The easy-to-digest gelatin broth is gentle on the digestive system while providing concentrated nutrition.

Digestive Health

The gelatin in pork trotter is known to soothe the lining of the digestive tract. Ginger aids in breaking down the fats, and the slow-simmered broth is easily assimilated, making this soup a traditional choice for those with weak digestion or recovering from illness.

Complete Recipe: Pork Trotter and Peanut Soup

Ingredients

  • 2 pork trotters (about 1-1.5 kg / 2-3 lbs), cleaned and split lengthwise — ask your butcher to do this
  • 1 cup raw peanuts (with or without skin — skin-on is preferred in TCM for blood nourishment)
  • 8-10 red dates (jujubes), pitted
  • 4-5 slices fresh ginger
  • 1-2 tablespoons goji berries (optional, add in last 15 minutes)
  • 1-2 scallions, cut into 2-inch pieces (optional)
  • Salt to taste (add at the very end)
  • Water — enough to cover all ingredients by 2 inches (about 3-4 liters)

Equipment

  • Large stockpot (at least 5-6 quarts) or slow cooker / Instant Pot
  • Fine-mesh skimmer for removing impurities
  • Sharp knife

Instructions

Step 1: Prepare the pork trotters

Rinse the trotters thoroughly under cold water. Place them in a large pot and cover with cold water. Bring to a boil over high heat and let them boil for 5-8 minutes. This blanching step removes impurities, excess fat, and any strong odors. Drain the trotters and rinse them under warm water, scrubbing off any residue.

Step 2: Soak the peanuts

While the trotters are being blanched, soak the raw peanuts in warm water for at least 30 minutes. This softens them and shortens the cooking time. If using skinless peanuts, a shorter soak is fine. In TCM, the red peanut skin is believed to have blood-nourishing properties, so try to keep it on if possible.

Step 3: Start the soup

Place the blanched trotters and soaked peanuts into your clean stockpot. Add the ginger slices, red dates, and scallions (if using). Pour in enough fresh cold water to cover everything by about 2 inches — roughly 3-4 liters. Do not add salt at this stage, as it will toughen the meat.

Step 4: Simmer slowly

Bring the pot to a gentle boil over medium-high heat, then immediately reduce to the lowest simmer. Skim off any foam that rises to the surface during the first 15 minutes. Cover and let it simmer gently for 3 to 4 hours. The broth is ready when the trotters are very tender and the liquid has turned milky-white and thickened from the released collagen. Stir occasionally to prevent sticking.

Step 5: Add goji berries and season

In the last 15 minutes of cooking, add the goji berries (if using). Taste the broth and add salt to taste — start with 1 teaspoon and adjust. The soup should be savory but not salty. Remove the ginger slices and scallion pieces before serving.

Step 6: Serve

Ladle the soup into bowls, making sure each serving gets some trotter meat, peanuts, and a red date or two. The meat will be fall-off-the-bone tender. Garnish with chopped scallions or cilantro if desired. Serve hot.

How to Select and Store Ingredients

Choosing Pork Trotters

Look for trotters with skin that is smooth, firm, and pale pink to white. Avoid any with dark spots, discoloration, or a strong off-odor. Fresh trotters have a very mild, clean scent. If buying frozen, thaw completely in the refrigerator before using. Chinese or Asian grocery stores almost always carry pork trotters — ask the butcher to chop them into 2-3 inch pieces for easier cooking.

Selecting Peanuts

Raw, unsalted peanuts with their red skins intact are best for this recipe. The skins are rich in antioxidants and are traditionally valued in TCM for their blood-nourishing properties. Avoid roasted or salted peanuts, as they will alter the flavor and cooking behavior. Store peanuts in an airtight container in a cool, dark place for up to 3 months.

Storing Leftover Soup

This soup stores beautifully. Refrigerate in an airtight container for up to 5 days. The broth will turn into a thick, wobbly gel when cold — this is a sign of excellent collagen extraction, not spoilage. Simply reheat gently on the stovetop or in the microwave until liquid again. You can also freeze the soup for up to 3 months in portion-sized containers.

Precautions and Contraindications

While pork trotter and peanut soup is generally safe and nourishing for most people, there are some considerations to keep in mind:

  • High purine content: Pork trotters are rich in purines, which may aggravate gout or hyperuricemia. If you have a history of gout, consult your healthcare provider before consuming this soup regularly.
  • Fat content: The skin and connective tissue are naturally fatty. If you are watching your fat intake, refrigerate the finished soup and skim off the solidified fat layer before reheating.
  • Peanut allergy: This is a significant allergen. Omit peanuts entirely if serving anyone with a known peanut allergy, or substitute with white beans or lotus seeds.
  • Digestive sensitivity: The rich, gelatinous broth can be heavy for some people. Start with a small bowl and see how your digestion responds. Adding extra ginger can help mitigate this.
  • Pregnancy: Generally safe in moderate amounts. However, due to the rich nature of the soup, pregnant women should consult their healthcare provider, especially if they have gestational hypertension or digestive concerns.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I use pig feet instead of pork trotters?

Yes. Pig feet are essentially the same cut — the ankle and hoof area. Trotters refer to the entire foot including a small portion of the leg, while "pig feet" often refers to just the hoof section. Either works well. The key is to have plenty of skin, connective tissue, and bone for collagen extraction.

Do I need to remove the peanut skins?

Not necessarily. In TCM, the red skin of the peanut is actually prized for its blood-nourishing properties. However, some people find the skins slightly bitter and prefer to remove them after cooking — they will float to the surface and can be skimmed off easily. For the most TCM benefit, keep them on.

Why is my broth not turning milky white?

A milky-white broth comes from emulsified fat and collagen. If your broth stays clear, try one of these: first, make sure you are simmering at a gentle rolling boil (not too low); second, ensure the pork trotters have enough fat and skin — very lean cuts won't produce a white broth; third, if using an Instant Pot, a natural release (not quick release) helps emulsify the fat better.

Can I reuse the pork trotters for a second batch?

After 3-4 hours of simmering, most of the collagen has been extracted. The meat will be very soft but edible — you can shred it and add it back to the soup. For a second batch of broth, the bones and skin will produce a much weaker gelatin content. It's better to use fresh trotters each time.
