Pumpkin and Jujube Congee — A Sweet Spleen Tonic

🥘 Recipe · 7 min read

The Gentle Sweetness of Autumn Healing

As the leaves turn and the air carries the first chill of autumn, the body begins to crave something warmer, more grounding. In the tradition of Chinese medicine, this seasonal shift signals a time to support the Spleen and Stomach — the organs that transform food into the energy that sustains us. And few dishes answer that call as beautifully as pumpkin and jujube congee.

This is not the savoury, topping-laden congee you might find at a Cantonese diner. It is a sweet, creamy, soul-warming porridge — gentle enough for a child recovering from a stomach bug, nourishing enough for an elder rebuilding their strength, and satisfying enough for anyone who simply wants to feel, well, held.

Pumpkin and jujube congee (南瓜红枣粥, nánguā hóngzǎo zhōu) brings together two of TCM's most beloved food ingredients in a single pot. The result is a bowl of warm, golden nourishment that tastes like comfort and works like a tonic.

Why Pumpkin and Jujube?

In TCM, every food carries a thermal nature, a flavour, and a specific affinity for certain organ systems. Pumpkin and jujube are complementary partners: both are sweet and warming, both enter the Spleen and Stomach meridians, and together they create a balanced, synergistic tonic that is greater than the sum of its parts.

Pumpkin (南瓜, nánguā) — The Spleen Fortifier

Pumpkin is classified in TCM as sweet and warm in nature, entering the Spleen and Stomach meridians. Its primary actions include:

  • Strengthens the Spleen and Stomach — Pumpkin is traditionally used to support healthy digestion and transform food into Qi. Its warm, sweet nature is believed to gently tonify the Spleen without creating the stagnation that heavier foods can cause.
  • Nourishes the Middle Burner — The middle burner (中焦, zhōngjiāo) is the TCM term for the digestive region. Pumpkin is considered deeply harmonising to this area, helping to resolve feelings of lethargy, bloating, and poor appetite.
  • Moistens the Lungs — During the dry autumn months, pumpkin is also believed to benefit the Lungs, making it a seasonal ally against dryness and cough.
  • Regulates the Intestines — Pumpkin is thought to support regular bowel movements and ease mild digestive discomfort.

Modern nutritional science echoes these traditional insights: pumpkin is rich in beta-carotene, vitamin C, potassium, and dietary fibre, all of which support digestive health, immunity, and cellular repair. The deep orange flesh is a sign of its abundant antioxidants, which help the body manage inflammation and oxidative stress.

Jujube (红枣, hóngzǎo) — The Qi and Blood Harmoniser

Jujube dates — often called Chinese red dates — are among the most frequently used ingredients in the TCM food therapy pantry. They are classified as sweet and warm, entering the Spleen and Stomach meridians, with these key actions:

  • Tonifies Spleen Qi — Jujube is considered a premier food for boosting the Spleen's digestive energy. It is traditionally recommended for fatigue, low appetite, and loose stools — all signs of Spleen Qi deficiency.
  • Nourishes Blood and Calms the Shen — In TCM, blood and spirit are connected. When blood is abundant, the mind is calm. Jujube is believed to nourish the blood and settle the shen (神, the spirit), making it helpful for irritability, restlessness, and insomnia.
  • Harmonises the Formula — In classical Chinese herbalism, jujube is so valued for its harmonising properties that it appears in more formulas than almost any other ingredient. It gently moderates strong herbs and protects the Stomach — in this congee, it does the same for the pumpkin, creating a smooth, balanced energy.

The natural sweetness of jujube means you need little to no added sweetener in this congee — the dates themselves impart a rich, honeyed flavour that deepens as they simmer.

Traditional Roots and Classical References

Pumpkin itself is a New World crop that arrived in China via trade routes during the Ming dynasty, but its medicinal use was quickly absorbed into the TCM food therapy tradition. Classic TCM texts such as Compendium of Materia Medica (《本草纲目》, Běncǎo Gāngmù) describe pumpkin as "sweet in flavour, warm in nature, and non-toxic — it supplements the Middle Burner and boosts Qi."

Jujube, by contrast, has been a cornerstone of Chinese medicine for thousands of years. It appears in the oldest surviving Chinese medical text, the Shennong Bencao Jing (《神农本草经》), where it is praised for "nourishing the Spleen and Stomach, boosting the Qi, generating fluids, and harmonising the nature of all herbs."

The combination of pumpkin and jujube in congee is a more recent culinary innovation, but it draws on a deeply rooted TCM principle: that sweet, warm, cooked foods create the ideal environment for the Spleen to thrive. This congee is a modern expression of an ancient wisdom — a bowl that carries thousands of years of dietary therapy in every spoonful.

The Complete Recipe

This recipe makes a generous 4 servings. It is intentionally simple — the quality of the ingredients and the patience of the simmer are what make this congee special.

Pumpkin and Jujube Congee

Prep time: 10 minutes
Cook time: 50–60 minutes
Serves: 4

Ingredients

  • ½ cup (100 g) white rice — short-grain, jasmine, or medium-grain
  • 6 cups (1.4 L) water (or a light vegetable broth for extra depth)
  • 200 g (about 1½ cups) pumpkin, peeled and diced into 1 cm cubes — any orange-fleshed variety works: kabocha, butternut, or sugar pumpkin
  • 6–8 dried red jujube dates (红枣, hóngzǎo) — rinsed, pitted, and halved
  • 1 tablespoon rock sugar (冰糖, bīngtáng) or honey, to taste (optional — the jujubes already provide natural sweetness)
  • Small pinch of sea salt

Step-by-Step Instructions

  1. Rinse the rice: Place the rice in a fine-mesh strainer and rinse under cold running water until the water runs clear. This removes excess starch and prevents the congee from becoming overly thick.
  2. Prepare the pumpkin: Peel the pumpkin, remove the seeds and fibrous centre, and cut the flesh into small 1 cm cubes. Smaller cubes will break down more completely into the congee, creating a smoother, creamier texture.
  3. Prepare the jujubes: Rinse the dried jujubes. Slice each date in half and remove the pit. Scoring the flesh with a knife helps the sweetness and medicinal properties infuse into the congee more fully.
  4. Combine everything in the pot: In a heavy-bottomed pot (a Dutch oven or stainless steel pot works well), combine the rinsed rice, cubed pumpkin, halved jujubes, and 6 cups of water. Bring to a boil over medium-high heat.
  5. Reduce to a low simmer: Once boiling, reduce the heat to the lowest setting. The congee should barely bubble — a gentle, lazy simmer. Too vigorous a boil and the liquid will evaporate too quickly and the texture will become uneven.
  6. Cook uncovered for 40–50 minutes: Leave the pot uncovered (or slightly ajar) and cook, stirring every 10 minutes to prevent the rice and pumpkin from sticking to the bottom. As the congee thickens in the final 20 minutes, stir more frequently — every 5 minutes or so.
  7. Check the pumpkin: As the rice breaks down into creamy porridge, the pumpkin cubes will soften and begin to melt into the congee. By the end, you want most of the pumpkin to have dissolved, leaving a naturally sweet, golden-orange porridge. If you prefer some texture, add the pumpkin cubes 20 minutes into the cooking rather than at the start.
  8. Season and sweeten: Once the congee has reached a creamy consistency and the pumpkin has broken down, stir in a small pinch of sea salt and — if you'd like it sweeter — rock sugar or honey to taste. The jujubes alone may provide enough sweetness for most palates, so taste first before adding sugar.
  9. Serve warm: Ladle into bowls. The congee is best enjoyed warm, not piping hot — the TCM principle is that gentle warmth is most restorative for the Spleen.

📝 Cook's Notes

  • Texture adjustment: For a thinner congee, add another cup of water during cooking. For a thicker, more porridge-like consistency, use 5 cups of water instead of 6.
  • Leftover rice shortcut: Already-cooked rice breaks down in about 20 minutes. Use 1½ cups of leftover rice and reduce the water to 5 cups — add the pumpkin and jujube at the same time and simmer until creamy.
  • Slow cooker method: Combine all ingredients in a slow cooker and cook on low for 6–8 hours or high for 3–4 hours. This yields an exceptionally creamy congee with minimal effort.
  • Instant Pot method: Combine all ingredients in the Instant Pot. Cook on high pressure for 20 minutes, then allow natural pressure release for 15 minutes. Stir well before serving.

Variations to Suit Your Season and Constitution

This congee is wonderful as written, but part of the beauty of congee is how easily it adapts. Here are three variations that each shift the energy of the dish:

With Ginger (姜汁南瓜红枣粥)

Add to the base recipe: Place 3–4 thin slices of fresh ginger in the pot at the beginning, alongside the rice, pumpkin, and jujube. Ginger is warming and pungent in TCM — it helps the Spleen transform fluids, dispels internal cold, and supports digestive fire. This variation is ideal on chilly autumn mornings, during the early stages of a cold, or for anyone who tends to feel cold in the hands and feet. The ginger adds a subtle spicy warmth that offsets the sweetness of the jujube beautifully.

With Goji Berries (枸杞南瓜红枣粥)

Add to the base recipe: During the last 10 minutes of cooking, stir in 1 tablespoon of dried goji berries (枸杞, gǒuqǐ). Goji berries are sweet and neutral in TCM, entering the Liver, Lung, and Kidney meridians. They nourish the blood, support healthy vision, and supplement vital essence (jing). Combined with the Spleen-nourishing pumpkin and jujube, this variation becomes a three-ingredient tonic that supports both digestion and deeper vitality. The goji berries also add a pop of colour and a mild tartness that balances the bowl perfectly.

With Astragalus (黄芪南瓜红枣粥)

Add to the base recipe: For a more medicinal, Qi-building version, add 10 g (about 4–5 slices) of dried astragalus root (黄芪, huángqí) to the pot at the start, tied in a small piece of cheesecloth or a reusable spice bag. Simmer the astragalus with the congee, then remove and discard the bag before serving. Astragalus is one of the premier Qi tonics in TCM — it is sweet and warm, enters the Spleen and Lung meridians, and is traditionally used to support immune function, boost energy, and protect the body's defensive Qi (wei qi). This variation is especially recommended for those recovering from prolonged illness or feeling deeply depleted. Note: Astragalus should be avoided during acute infections with fever. Consult a TCM practitioner for personalised guidance.

Best Time to Eat Pumpkin and Jujube Congee

In TCM, eating with the seasons is one of the most powerful ways to maintain health. Here is when this congee shines brightest:

Autumn — The Ideal Season

Autumn is the season of the Lungs in TCM's Five Element framework, and it is also a time when the Spleen begins to prepare for the cold months ahead. The dry, cooler air of autumn calls for warm, moistening foods that are easy to digest. Pumpkin and jujube congee is a perfect seasonal dish: the pumpkin moistens the Lungs and supports the Spleen, while the jujube's warmth protects against the creeping cold. A bowl of this congee for breakfast during the autumn months helps the body transition smoothly from summer's abundance to winter's stillness.

For Spleen Qi Deficiency

If you recognise any of these signs, you may have a pattern of Spleen Qi deficiency, and this congee may be supportive:

  • Fatigue, especially after eating
  • Loose stools or a tendency toward bloating
  • Poor appetite or a feeling of heaviness after meals
  • Weak limbs and a sallow or pale complexion
  • A tendency to catch colds easily

Eating warm, cooked, sweet foods like this congee is traditionally recommended in TCM to gently rebuild Spleen Qi over time. Even one bowl a day, consistently, can make a noticeable difference.

During Recovery

When recovering from illness, digestive energy is often the first system to be depleted and the last to return. This congee — gentle, warming, and requiring minimal digestive effort — is an ideal transitional food. It provides steady energy without taxing the body.

As a Gentle Breakfast

A warm bowl of congee for breakfast is a wonderful alternative to cold cereals, pastries, or heavy breakfast meats. In TCM, the morning is when the Spleen's digestive fire is just waking up — cold or raw foods at breakfast are believed to weaken it. This congee offers a warm, supportive start to the day.

When to Eat Congee in TCM: A Quick Guide

Congee in general has specific applications in TCM food therapy. Here is a quick reference for when to turn to congee as a healing food:

  • When you are sick. Any illness — cold, flu, fever, digestive upset — is a time for congee. It is the easiest food to digest and provides hydration and gentle energy when your body is focused on healing.
  • When recovering from illness or surgery. The body needs building materials but cannot handle heavy digestion. Congee is the ideal bridge food.
  • When your digestion feels weak. Bloating, poor appetite, loose stools — these are signs that the Spleen needs support. Congee is the quintessential Spleen tonic.
  • During seasonal transitions. The body works harder during the shift between seasons. Congee provides easy, warming nourishment during these transitional periods.
  • When you feel depleted. Emotional stress, grief, overwork, and exhaustion all drain Qi. Congee helps rebuild without demanding more than you have to give.
  • For the elderly and young children. Both groups benefit from the soft, easily digestible nature of congee. It provides steady nutrition when digestive capacity is limited.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I eat pumpkin and jujube congee every day?

Yes, this congee is gentle enough for daily consumption, especially during autumn and winter. Its mild, warm nature makes it a suitable everyday breakfast or light meal for most people. However, those with strong internal heat, excess phlegm, or diabetes should consume pumpkin in moderation and consult a healthcare provider about appropriate portion sizes.

Is this congee good for digestion?

Yes. In TCM, congee is considered one of the most digestible foods available, and pumpkin further supports the Stomach and Spleen with its warm, sweet nature. The long simmering breaks down the rice starches into simple sugars, while the cooked pumpkin is soft and easy to assimilate. This congee is traditionally recommended for those recovering from illness, experiencing poor appetite, or dealing with weak digestion.

Can I add meat or protein to this congee?

Absolutely. While the pumpkin and jujube version is delicious on its own as a sweet, nourishing porridge, you can add shredded chicken, minced pork, or silken tofu in the last 20 minutes of cooking for extra protein. In TCM, adding chicken is believed to boost Qi, making the congee more substantial for recovery. Just note that adding savoury protein will shift the congee's flavour profile away from the sweet side.

Is this congee suitable for children and the elderly?

Yes, this congee is particularly well suited for both children and the elderly. Its soft, creamy texture requires minimal chewing and digestion effort. The natural sweetness from the pumpkin and jujube makes it appealing to children without added sugar. For elderly individuals with weakened Spleen function or reduced appetite, this congee provides gentle nourishment that is easy to eat and digest. Always ensure the congee is sufficiently cooled and that any large jujube pieces are broken up for young children.

When is the best time to eat pumpkin and jujube congee?

This congee is especially recommended during autumn, when the Spleen and Stomach are believed to benefit from warm, grounding foods according to TCM seasonal principles. It also makes an excellent breakfast or light lunch year-round, providing sustained energy without taxing the digestive system. It is less ideal late at night due to its carbohydrate content, though a small bowl can be soothing if you need a gentle, warming snack.

A Bowl of Autumn Gold

What makes pumpkin and jujube congee so special is not any single ingredient or effect — it is the quiet, cumulative power of the combination itself. The warm sweetness of the pumpkin, the honeyed depth of the jujube, and the gentle creaminess of the rice come together in a bowl that nourishes without fanfare, heals without force, and comforts without excess.

In a world that often asks us to do more, be more, and push harder, this congee asks only that you sit down with a spoon and receive. It is a bowl of autumn gold — and it carries within it the wisdom of centuries.

Whether you make it for yourself on a quiet morning, for a loved one who needs gentle rebuilding, or simply because the season calls for warmth, know that you are participating in a tradition that has nourished people for thousands of years. The Spleen knows what it needs. And sometimes, what it needs is as simple as a bowl of sweet, warm congee.
