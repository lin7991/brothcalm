Foods for Healthy Skin in TCM — Nourishing Blood and Moisture

🥗 Food Therapy · 8 min read

Introduction: The TCM View of Skin as an Internal Mirror

In Traditional Chinese Medicine, the skin is far more than a protective barrier — it is regarded as a vivid reflection of what is happening inside the body. A radiant, even-toned complexion is believed to signal harmony among the internal organs, while dryness, dullness, or breakouts may point to deeper imbalances. This holistic perspective sets TCM apart from conventional dermatology: rather than treating the skin in isolation, TCM food therapy seeks to nourish the underlying systems that govern skin health.

Three organ systems play a central role. The Lung (Fei) is said to govern the skin and body hair — when Lung Qi is strong and Lung fluids are abundant, the skin is believed to appear supple and well-hydrated. The Spleen (Pi) is responsible for transforming food into Qi and Blood — a healthy Spleen produces ample Blood, which in turn nourishes the complexion. The Liver (Gan) stores and regulates Blood, ensuring smooth circulation to the skin and extremities. When these three systems work in concert, the result is the healthy, luminous look that TCM texts describe as having "flourishing appearance."

One of the earliest Chinese materia medica, the Shen Nong Ben Cao Jing (Divine Farmer's Materia Medica, ~200 CE), already classified foods and herbs by their effects on complexion and vitality. Many of the ingredients discussed in this article — goji berries, white fungus, Chinese yam — have been prized for centuries for their beautifying properties. Today, modern research is beginning to confirm what TCM practitioners have long observed: that diet significantly influences skin health from the inside out.

TCM Principles for Healthy Skin

Nourishing Blood (Yǎng Xuè — 养血)

Blood (Xuè) in TCM is more than the red fluid that circulates through vessels — it carries nourishment, moisture, and warmth to every tissue. When Blood is abundant and flowing freely, the skin receives sufficient nutrients and appears rosy, plump, and resilient. Blood deficiency (xuè xū) is commonly associated with a pale or sallow complexion, dry skin, brittle nails, and fatigue.

Blood-nourishing foods are typically rich, sweet, and deeply nourishing. They are believed to support the Spleen and Liver in producing and storing Blood, thereby improving the skin's tone and texture from within.

Moistening the Lungs (Rùn Fèi — 润肺)

The Lung is considered the "delicate organ" — it is especially vulnerable to dryness, whether from weather, indoor heating, or internal heat. In TCM, the Lung regulates the skin's moisture by dispersing fluids to the body surface. When Lung fluids (fei yin) are depleted, the skin may become dry, flaky, or prone to fine lines.

Moistening foods — often gelatinous, slippery, or mucilaginous in texture — are traditionally used in TCM to replenish Lung fluids. White fungus (tremella), snow fungus, and pear are classic examples. These foods are believed to hydrate the skin from within, offering what TCM calls "internal moisturising."

Clearing Heat (Qīng Rè — 清热)

Excess internal Heat can manifest on the skin as redness, breakouts, blemishes, or a flushed complexion. In TCM, Heat may arise from diet (spicy foods, alcohol, fried foods), emotional stress, or environmental factors. Clearing Heat does not mean lowering body temperature in the Western sense — it means gently cooling inflammatory tendencies so the skin can maintain a calm, even appearance.

Cooling foods such as green tea, mung beans, cucumber, and pear are commonly recommended. These ingredients are believed to help soothe the skin and reduce the appearance of blemishes when Heat is the root cause.

Key Foods for Skin Health

The following foods are among the most celebrated in TCM for their skin-supporting properties. Each works through one or more of the principles above — nourishing Blood, moistening the Lungs, or clearing Heat.

Goji Berries (Gǒu Qǐ Zǐ — 枸杞子)

Goji berries are perhaps the best-known blood-nourishing superfood in TCM. Traditionally in TCM, they enter the Liver and Kidney channels and are believed to nourish both Blood and Yin. The Shen Nong Ben Cao Jing lists goji berries as a "superior" herb, noting their ability to "brighten the eyes and strengthen the body." For the skin, goji berries may support a healthy glow by enriching the Blood that feeds the complexion. They are rich in antioxidants, including zeaxanthin, which modern studies suggest may protect the skin from oxidative stress.

Red Dates / Jujube (Hóng Zǎo — 红枣)

Red dates are a staple in TCM kitchens. They are classified as sweet and warm, and are believed to tonify the Spleen and nourish Blood. When the Spleen is strong, it can efficiently transform food into Qi and Blood — the foundation of healthy skin. Red dates are often paired with goji berries in teas and congees for a synergistic blood-nourishing effect. They also contain vitamin C and iron, which may support collagen synthesis and circulation.

White Fungus / Tremella (Yín'ěr — 银耳)

White fungus is one of TCM's most treasured beauty ingredients. Its gelatinous, translucent texture is said to be a natural analogue to the body's own fluids. Traditionally in TCM, white fungus moistens the Lungs, nourishes Stomach Yin, and generates body fluids (jīn yè). It is believed to be particularly beneficial for dry, dehydrated skin and is sometimes called the "poor woman's bird's nest" for its collagen-like properties. Modern research indicates that tremella polysaccharides have significant water-holding capacity, which may help the skin retain moisture.

Chinese Yam (Shān Yào — 山药)

Chinese yam is a neutral, sweet food that enters the Spleen, Lung, and Kidney channels. It is believed to strengthen the Spleen's digestive function, which in turn supports Blood production, and to directly moisten the Lungs. For skin health, Chinese yam may improve hydration and elasticity by supporting the body's fluid metabolism. It can be used in soups, congees, or steamed as a side dish.

Lily Bulb (Bǎi Hé — 百合)

Lily bulb has a cooling, moistening nature and is traditionally used to nourish Lung Yin and calm the mind. Its mucilaginous quality makes it a natural moisturiser for the skin. Lily bulb is often combined with white fungus and goji berries in sweet soups and congees for a triple-action skin tonic: Blood nourishing, Lung moistening, and gently cooling.

Sesame Seeds — Black Sesame (Hēi Zhī Ma — 黑芝麻)

Black sesame seeds are prized in TCM for their ability to nourish the Liver and Kidneys — the organs responsible for Blood storage and essence (jing). When Kidney essence is abundant, the hair is lustrous and the skin appears vibrant. Black sesame seeds are rich in healthy fatty acids, vitamin E, and calcium. They may be ground into a paste, sprinkled over congee, or used in desserts.

Spinach (Bō Cài — 菠菜)

Spinach is a cooling, sweet vegetable that enters the Intestines and Stomach channels. It is believed to nourish Blood and moisten dryness, making it a valuable addition to the diet for those with Blood deficiency or dry skin. Its high iron and chlorophyll content align with the TCM principle of building Blood through green, leafy vegetables.

Green Tea (Lǜ Chá — 绿茶)

Green tea is cooling and slightly bitter. In TCM, it is used to clear Heat, resolve Dampness, and support healthy digestion. For skin health, green tea may help calm inflammatory skin conditions and reduce excess oiliness when Heat is involved. Its catechins are well-studied for their antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties.

Pearl Barley (Yì Yǐ Rén — 薏苡仁)

Pearl barley, also known as coix seed or Job's tears, is a cool, sweet grain that is believed to resolve Dampness and clear Heat. In TCM, when Damp-Heat accumulates, the skin may develop acne, rashes, or a greasy texture. Pearl barley helps the body drain excess moisture while gently cooling the system. It is commonly used in congees, teas, or soups for a clearer complexion.

Recipe: Beauty Congee with White Fungus, Goji Berries, and Red Dates

This classic TCM beauty congee combines three of the most powerful skin-nourishing ingredients in one warming, easily digestible bowl. It is traditionally enjoyed for breakfast or as a light evening meal.

Ingredients

  • ½ cup short-grain white rice (or jasmine rice)
  • 10 g dried white fungus (yin er), soaked until soft and torn into bite-sized pieces
  • 10-15 dried goji berries
  • 5-6 red dates (jujube), pitted and sliced
  • 5 g dried lily bulb (bai he), optional
  • 6 cups water or light vegetable broth
  • A pinch of rock sugar or honey, to taste (optional)

Method

  1. Soak the dried white fungus in warm water for 30 minutes until fully softened. Drain, remove the hard yellow base, and tear into small florets.
  2. Rinse the rice and place it in a heavy-bottomed pot with the 6 cups of water. Add the white fungus, red dates, and lily bulb (if using).
  3. Bring to a boil, then reduce the heat to low and simmer uncovered for 40-50 minutes, stirring occasionally, until the congee is thick and creamy and the white fungus has softened into a gelatinous consistency.
  4. Add the goji berries in the last 10 minutes of cooking — this preserves their flavour and nutrients.
  5. Sweeten with a small pinch of rock sugar or a drizzle of honey if desired. Serve warm.

TCM tip: This congee is believed to nourish Blood, moisten the Lungs, and support the Spleen. Regular consumption — 2-3 times per week during cooler months or whenever the skin feels dry — may help restore internal moisture and promote a healthy, even complexion over time.

Foods to Avoid for Better Skin in TCM

Just as certain foods build healthy skin, others are believed to contribute to skin problems by generating internal Heat, Dampness, or Blood deficiency.

Spicy and pungent foods — chilli, garlic, ginger (in excess), curry, and pepper — are considered warming and drying in TCM. Overconsumption is thought to create internal Heat that can rise to the face, resulting in redness, breakouts, or a flushed appearance. Those with acne-prone or sensitive skin may benefit from reducing these foods.

Greasy and fried foods — deep-fried snacks, fatty meats, and heavy oils — generate Dampness and Heat in the Spleen and Stomach. In TCM, Damp-Heat rising to the skin can manifest as oily skin, cystic acne, or puffiness. Lighter cooking methods such as steaming, boiling, and stir-frying with minimal oil are preferred.

Alcohol and excessive sugar — alcohol is warming and drying, and is believed to deplete Yin fluids and injure the Liver. Sugar, especially refined sugar, contributes to Dampness and Heat. Both may dull the complexion and reduce the skin's natural resilience.

Excessive raw and cold foods — while fresh salads and fruit are generally healthy, TCM suggests that consuming too many raw or cold foods can weaken the Spleen's digestive function. A weakened Spleen produces less Blood, which may leave the skin looking pale or tired. A balanced approach — warm, cooked meals with occasional raw elements — is recommended.

Precautions

TCM food therapy is a gentle, supportive approach to wellness, but it is not a substitute for professional medical care. Always keep the following in mind:

  • Consult a qualified healthcare provider before making significant dietary changes, especially if you have a diagnosed medical condition, are pregnant, or are nursing.
  • Some TCM ingredients may interact with medications. For example, goji berries may interact with blood thinners such as warfarin. Discuss any herbal foods with your doctor or pharmacist.
  • Individual constitutions vary. What works for one person may not work for another. In TCM, dietary choices are ideally tailored to your unique pattern of imbalance — consulting a licensed TCM practitioner is recommended for personalised guidance.
  • Start with small amounts when trying a new ingredient to see how your body responds. Some individuals may experience digestive sensitivity to white fungus or goji berries.
  • Skin changes take time. TCM food therapy is a gradual, cumulative approach. Consistent dietary habits over weeks and months are more effective than short-term adjustments.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the TCM view of skin health?

In Traditional Chinese Medicine, the skin is considered a mirror of internal organ health. The Lung governs the skin and body hair, the Spleen produces Blood from digested food, and the Liver stores and regulates Blood. Healthy, radiant skin is believed to reflect abundant Blood (xuè) and sufficient Moisture (jīn yè) throughout the body.

Which foods are best for nourishing blood and improving complexion in TCM?

TCM traditionally recommends blood-nourishing (养血) foods such as goji berries (gou qi zi), red dates (hong zao), black sesame seeds, spinach, and cooked liver. For moistening the Lungs and skin, white fungus (yin er), Chinese yam (shan yao), lily bulb (bai he), and pear are frequently used. These foods may support circulation, hydration, and a healthy glow.

Can TCM dietary therapy help with dry or dull skin?

Yes. Dry or dull skin is often attributed in TCM to Blood deficiency (血虚) or Lung Yin deficiency (肺阴虚). Foods that moisten the Lungs and nourish Blood, such as white fungus, goji berries, and Chinese yam, are believed to help restore internal moisture and support a more luminous complexion over time. However, results vary and TCM food therapy is a complementary approach.

What foods should I avoid in TCM for better skin?

In TCM, spicy and greasy foods, alcohol, and excessive sugar are thought to generate internal Heat and Dampness, which may contribute to breakouts, redness, and puffiness. Fried foods, chili, lamb, and strong spices are often minimised when addressing skin concerns. A cooling, balanced diet is generally recommended.

How can I use goji berries and red dates for skin health?

Goji berries and red dates are staple blood-nourishing ingredients in TCM. They can be simmered in congee (rice porridge) with white fungus and lily bulb to create a nourishing 'beauty congee,' or steeped as a tea. Traditionally in TCM, goji berries benefit the Liver and Kidneys, while red dates tonify the Spleen and Blood — together they may support a healthy, radiant complexion.
