Longan (Long Yan Rou) — The Sweet Heart-Calming Fruit

🌿 Ingredient Deep Dive · 6 min read

What Is Longan in TCM?

Longan (Dimocarpus longan) is a small, round fruit native to southern China and Southeast Asia. In Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM), the dried flesh of the fruit is known as long yan rou (龙眼肉), literally translating to "dragon eye meat" — a name inspired by the fruit's translucent flesh surrounding a dark central pit, resembling a dragon's eye. Fresh longan has a sweet, honey-like flavor and a juicy, jelly-like texture similar to lychee. Once dried, it becomes chewy, concentrated, and intensely sweet. Longan has been used in Chinese food therapy for over two thousand years, prized not as a refreshing snack but as a gentle yet potent blood-nourishing and mind-calming tonic.

TCM Properties of Longan

Nature and Taste

In the TCM framework of food energetics, longan is classified as warm in nature and sweet in taste. The sweet flavor enters the digestive system and harmonizes the body's energies, while the warming quality gently invigorates circulation and metabolism. This warm-sweet combination makes longan particularly suitable for individuals who feel cold, fatigued, or depleted.

Meridian Affinity

Longan primarily targets two organ systems: the Heart and the Spleen. In TCM theory, the Heart houses the shen (spirit or mind) and governs blood circulation, while the Spleen is responsible for transforming food into energy (qi) and blood. By entering both meridians, longan addresses the root of many common imbalances — insufficient blood production (Spleen) and poor blood utilization (Heart).

Core Functions

According to classical TCM texts, longan's primary actions are:

  • Nourishes Heart blood (养心血) — supports the material foundation of the mind
  • Calms the shen (安神) — settles nervous energy and promotes tranquility
  • Tonifies Spleen qi (补脾气) — strengthens digestion and energy production
  • Benefits memory (益智) — traditionally considered a brain tonic

Traditional Uses in Chinese Medicine

Classical References

Longan appears in the most important TCM materia medica. The Shen Nong Ben Cao Jing (Divine Farmer's Materia Medica, ~200 CE) describes it as a top-grade herb — safe for long-term use — that "calms the mind, sharpens the intellect, and dispels melancholy." The Ming dynasty Ben Cao Gang Mu (Compendium of Materia Medica, 1590 CE) by Li Shizhen expands on these functions, noting longan's ability to "nourish the Heart and Spleen, generate blood, and settle the spirit."

Heart Blood Deficiency Pattern

In TCM diagnosis, Heart blood deficiency is a common pattern characterized by:

  • Palpitations or a feeling of "empty" fluttering in the chest
  • Insomnia — especially difficulty staying asleep or restless dreams
  • Poor memory and reduced concentration
  • Anxiety, nervousness, or a tendency to startle easily
  • A pale complexion and pale tongue body
  • Thin, thready pulse

Longan is considered one of the key food-grade remedies for this pattern. Its warm, sweet nature gently replenishes the blood without being overly stimulating, making it suitable for long-term dietary use.

Spleen Qi Deficiency Pattern

Because longan also enters the Spleen meridian, it is used for Spleen qi deficiency — a state of low digestive energy. Classic signs include:

  • Chronic fatigue and heavy limbs
  • Poor appetite or feeling full after small meals
  • Loose stools or easy bruising
  • Pale or sallow complexion

TCM practitioners often combine longan with Chinese red dates (hong zao) and dang shen (Codonopsis) to boost its Spleen-tonifying effect — a classic formula known as Gui Pi Tang (Restore the Spleen Decoction) in modified form.

How to Use Longan in Your Daily Diet

Eating Raw (Dried Longan)

The simplest way to consume longan is to eat the dried fruits as a snack. Dried longan has a concentrated sweetness and a chewy, raisin-like texture. A serving of 10-15 fruits (about 6-10 grams) makes an excellent afternoon pick-me-up. Keep a small jar in your bag for quick energy when feeling mentally drained.

Longan Calming Tea

Ingredients:

  • 10-15 dried longan fruits
  • 6-8 dried red dates (jujubes), pitted
  • 1 teaspoon goji berries
  • 2 cups filtered water

Instructions: Rinse the dried ingredients. Place them in a small saucepan with the water. Bring to a gentle boil, then reduce heat and simmer for 15-20 minutes until the liquid turns amber and sweet. Strain into a cup and drink warm — ideally in the evening or when feeling anxious. This tea is naturally sweet and requires no added sugar.

Longan Congee

Longan congee is a classic TCM breakfast that nourishes both the Spleen (for digestion) and the Heart (for calm energy throughout the day).

Ingredients:

  • 1/2 cup short-grain rice (or jasmine rice)
  • 15-20 dried longan fruits
  • 5-6 red dates, pitted and sliced
  • 1 tablespoon rock sugar (or to taste)
  • 5 cups water

Instructions: Rinse the rice and soak for 30 minutes. In a large pot, combine rice, water, longan, and red dates. Bring to a boil, then reduce to a gentle simmer. Cook for 45-60 minutes, stirring occasionally, until the congee is thick and creamy. Stir in rock sugar until dissolved. Serve warm for breakfast or as a light evening meal.

Longan in Soups and Desserts

Dried longan can be added to a wide range of TCM-style soups and desserts. It pairs beautifully with snow fungus (yin er) for a collagen-rich dessert that also moisturizes the lungs, or with chicken soup for a warming, blood-nourishing winter broth. Simply toss 10-15 dried longan fruits into the pot during the last 20 minutes of cooking.

Daily Recommended Amount

In TCM practice, the standard daily dose of dried longan meat is 6 to 15 grams — roughly 10 to 25 fruits. As with all warming foods, it is wise to start with a smaller amount and observe how your body responds. Those new to longan may begin with 6-10 fruits per day and increase gradually.

Health Benefits (Traditionally in TCM)

May Support Sleep Quality

Longan's most celebrated use in TCM is for sleep support. By nourishing Heart blood, it helps settle the wandering mind that keeps people awake at night — especially those who wake frequently between 1-3 AM (associated with the Liver and Gallbladder hours in TCM chronobiology). A warm cup of longan tea before bed is a gentle, non-habit-forming sleep aid.

Is Believed to Help Calm Anxiety

When the Heart is deficient in blood, the shen has no stable residence — manifesting as restlessness, worry, and a sense of unease. Longan's nourishing action is believed to "root" the spirit in the body, fostering a natural sense of calm. It is particularly valued for mild, stress-related anxiety in people who tend to feel physically drained alongside their worry.

Traditionally Used for Memory Support

The classic TCM text describes longan as "benefiting the intellect," and it has been used for centuries by students and scholars preparing for examinations. In modern terms, the iron, potassium, and B-vitamin content of longan may support cognitive function, while its blood-nourishing action in TCM theory indirectly sharpens mental clarity.

May Help with Anemia

Dried longan is a good source of iron (approximately 1.5-2mg per 100g) and vitamin C, which enhances iron absorption. In TCM, it is traditionally prescribed for blood deficiency anemia — especially in women after menstruation or childbirth. The combination of longan, red dates, and goji berries is a classic postpartum blood-building trio.

Supports Postpartum Recovery

In Chinese postpartum tradition (zuo yue zi, "sitting the month"), longan features prominently in soups, teas, and congees. Its warm, sweet nature is believed to help restore the blood and energy lost during childbirth, while its calming action supports the emotional adjustment of new motherhood.

Precautions and Contraindications

Excess Heat Conditions

Because longan is warm in nature, individuals with excess heat patterns — indicated by persistent thirst, yellow tongue coating, red face, sore throat, or acne outbreaks — may find that too much longan aggravates these symptoms. Balance it with cooling foods like mung bean, cucumber, or chrysanthemum tea.

Acute Illness

During acute febrile illnesses (colds with fever, flu, sore throat, or infections), TCM generally recommends avoiding tonifying foods like longan until the acute phase passes. The body needs to expel pathogens first, and overly nourishing foods may "trap" the pathogen. Stick to light, neutral foods during illness and add longan back during recovery.

Blood Sugar Concerns

Dried longan has a concentrated natural sugar content (approximately 60-70% carbohydrates by weight). Those managing diabetes or blood sugar sensitivity should consume it in moderation — perhaps 5-8 fruits per serving — and monitor their glucose response. Combine with protein-rich foods (like nuts) to blunt blood sugar spikes.

Pregnancy and Breastfeeding

Longan is traditionally considered safe and even beneficial during and after pregnancy — particularly for blood building and calming anxiety. However, because of its warming nature, some TCM practitioners counsel moderation in the first trimester or for women with a tendency toward internal heat. As with any dietary change during pregnancy, consult your healthcare provider.

Dosage Guidance

Longan is classified as a top-grade herb in TCM — safe for long-term use. However, as with all foods, balance is key. Stick to the recommended 6-15g range daily and observe how your body responds. Those new to TCM food therapy may wish to consult a qualified practitioner for personalized advice.

FAQ

How much longan should I eat daily?

The typical TCM recommendation is 6 to 15 grams of dried longan meat per day (about 10-25 fruits). Start on the lower end and increase gradually.

Can I eat longan during pregnancy?

Yes, longan is traditionally used during and after pregnancy for blood nourishment. However, its warming nature means moderation is advised — especially for those prone to internal heat. Always consult your healthcare provider.

Who should avoid longan?

Those with excess heat patterns (fever, sore throat, acne, yellow tongue coating) should limit intake. Diabetics should consume in moderation due to its natural sugar content.

What is the difference between longan and lychee in TCM?

Both are warm and sweet, but longan specifically targets the Heart and Spleen to calm the shen and nourish blood, while lychee more strongly warms the Liver and Spleen and is less calming.

What is the best way to eat longan for sleep?

Simmer 10-15 dried longan fruits with 5-6 red dates in 2 cups of water for 15-20 minutes. Drink warm about an hour before bedtime. For stronger effect, combine with suan zao ren (sour jujube seed).
