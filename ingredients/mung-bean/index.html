Mung Bean (Lu Dou) — The Ultimate Cooling Ingredient for Summer Heat

🌿 Ingredient Deep Dive · 6 min read

Introduction: Nature's Air Conditioner

When summer temperatures soar and the humidity leaves you feeling sluggish, irritable, and overheated, Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM) reaches for one ingredient above all others: the humble mung bean. Known as lǜ dòu (绿豆) in Chinese, these small green legumes have been used for thousands of years as a cooling, detoxifying food that helps the body weather the hottest months of the year.

Unlike many so-called "superfoods" that arrive via the latest wellness trend, mung bean's reputation is anything but new. It is one of the most widely recognized cooling foods in the TCM pantry — a staple of summer kitchens across China, Southeast Asia, and beyond. A bowl of simple mung bean soup is the traditional answer to a scorching afternoon, believed to draw heat out of the body and restore a sense of calm.

In this article, we explore how mung bean is used in TCM food therapy, its energetic properties, traditional applications, and practical ways to incorporate it into your daily life.

TCM Properties of Mung Bean

In TCM, every food carries a thermal nature, a flavor, and a meridian affinity. Mung bean's profile makes it uniquely suited for its cooling role.

Nature and Flavor

Mung bean is classified as cold in nature with a sweet flavor (gān, 甘). The cold nature gives it the ability to clear heat, while the sweet taste harmonizes and tonifies the body. In the TCM framework, sweet-flavored foods are considered gentle and balancing — they nourish without overwhelming, making mung bean suitable even for extended use during hot seasons.

Meridian Entry

Mung bean is said to enter the Heart and Stomach meridians. The Heart meridian association explains why mung bean is traditionally used to address restlessness, irritability, and a feeling of heat in the chest — all symptoms of "heart fire" in TCM. Its connection to the Stomach meridian makes it effective for digestive issues related to heat and damp-heat, such as feeling heavy, bloated, or experiencing poor appetite in summer.

Traditional Uses in Chinese Medicine

Mung bean appears in many classical Chinese medical texts. The most famous reference comes from the Ben Cao Gang Mu (《本草纲目》), or Compendium of Materia Medica, written by the Ming dynasty physician Li Shizhen. In this monumental work, mung bean is described as sweet in taste and cold in nature, capable of clearing summer heat, relieving toxins, and soothing irritability.

The three primary traditional uses of mung bean are:

  • Clearing summer-heat (shǔ shǔ, 暑暑): This is the most well-known application. Mung bean soup is the classic remedy for heatstroke, heat exhaustion, and the general malaise that comes with prolonged exposure to high temperatures. It is believed to help the body dissipate internal heat and restore fluid balance.
  • Detoxification (jiě dú, 解毒): Mung bean has a long history of use for food poisoning, alcohol toxicity, and even drug overdoses in classical Chinese emergency medicine. The decoction was traditionally administered to help the body expel toxins through the urinary system.
  • Resolving damp-heat (qīng rè lì shī, 清热利湿): In TCM, summer often brings a combination of heat and dampness (humid heat). Mung bean is believed to resolve this pathogenic combination, helping with symptoms like heavy limbs, sticky stools, skin rashes, and urinary discomfort.

Medicinal Benefits in TCM

While mung bean is primarily a food, its medicinal applications in TCM are well-documented. Here are the key areas where it is traditionally believed to be most beneficial:

Cooling and Hydrating

The most immediate effect of mung bean soup is a feeling of coolness spreading through the body. This is more than anecdotal — TCM holds that the cold-natured broth directly counteracts pathogenic heat. The high water content and natural electrolytes in mung bean soup also make it a gentle hydrating beverage, particularly valuable when sweating heavily in summer.

Supporting Detoxification Pathways

Mung bean is traditionally considered to support the body's natural detoxification processes. It is believed to work through the urinary system, helping the kidneys and bladder flush out metabolic wastes and heat toxins. In folk medicine, a strong decoction of mung beans was sometimes used externally as a wash for skin irritations and rashes caused by heat.

Damp-Heat Relief

For those experiencing the sluggish, sticky discomfort of summer damp-heat — a common pattern in humid climates — mung bean is believed to gently clear both heat and dampness simultaneously. This dual action makes it more effective than many simple hydrating drinks for the kind of oppressive summer fatigue that ordinary water does not resolve.

How to Use Mung Bean

Mung bean is remarkably versatile in the kitchen. Here are the most common forms and preparations:

Mung Bean Soup (Lǜ Dòu Tāng)

This is the classic preparation — a simple broth made by boiling whole mung beans in water until the liquid turns pale green. The soup is typically served warm (not ice-cold, as that would shock the digestion) with a touch of sugar or rock sugar for palatability.

Mung Bean Congee

A thicker porridge made by cooking mung beans with rice (or other grains) until soft and creamy. This is a more substantial meal than the soup and is often eaten for breakfast or as a light dinner during summer.

Mung Bean Sprouts

When allowed to sprout, mung beans become yá cài (芽菜) — a crisp, cooling vegetable widely used in stir-fries and salads. The sprouting process transforms the bean's energy from dense and grounding to light and upward-moving, offering a gentler cooling effect than the whole bean.

Mung Bean Tea

A very light infusion made by pouring hot water over lightly roasted or raw mung beans and steeping. This is less intense than the soup and can be sipped throughout the day.

Recipes

Simple Mung Bean Soup

  • ½ cup whole mung beans, rinsed
  • 6 cups water
  • 1 slice fresh ginger
  • Rock sugar or honey to taste (optional)

Instructions: Bring the mung beans and water to a boil in a pot. Reduce heat to a gentle simmer and cook for 20–30 minutes, until the beans are tender and the liquid turns a pale green-gold. Add the ginger slice in the last 5 minutes. Do not overcook until the beans burst open — the cooling properties are traditionally believed to be strongest in the clear broth and the skin. Sweeten lightly if desired and serve warm or at room temperature.

Mung Bean and Rice Congee

  • ½ cup whole mung beans, rinsed
  • ¼ cup white or jasmine rice, rinsed
  • 6 cups water
  • 1 slice fresh ginger
  • Pinch of salt

Instructions: Combine the mung beans, rice, and water in a pot. Bring to a boil, then reduce to a low simmer. Cook for 45–60 minutes, stirring occasionally, until the beans are soft and the mixture has thickened into a creamy porridge. Add the ginger slice and salt in the last 10 minutes. Serve warm with a drizzle of sesame oil or a sprinkle of scallions.

Mung Bean Tea

  • 2 tablespoons whole mung beans
  • 4 cups hot water
  • A few goji berries (optional)

Instructions: Lightly crush the mung beans to open the skins. Place in a teapot or heatproof pitcher and pour hot water over them. Steep for 10–15 minutes. Strain and drink warm throughout the day. You can reuse the beans for a second, lighter infusion.

Safety and Precautions

While mung bean is a safe, well-tolerated food for most people, its cold nature means it is not suitable for everyone in large amounts.

  • Cold Spleen and Stomach: Individuals who regularly experience cold hands and feet, loose stools, weak digestion, or a sensation of cold in the abdomen should consume mung bean sparingly. Balancing it with warming ingredients like ginger, cinnamon, or jujube dates can help.
  • Not to Be Eaten Raw: Raw mung beans contain lectins and other compounds that can cause digestive upset. Always cook mung beans thoroughly before consuming.
  • Moderation During Pregnancy: Mung bean is traditionally considered safe in moderation during pregnancy, but its cold nature means it should be balanced with warm ingredients and consumed only occasionally. Consult a qualified practitioner for personalized advice.
  • Medication Interactions: In TCM theory, mung bean is believed to reduce the potency of certain herbal medicines, particularly warm-natured tonics. If you are taking herbal formulas, check with your TCM practitioner about timing mung bean consumption.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the TCM nature of mung bean?

Mung bean is classified as cold in nature with a sweet flavor. It enters the Heart and Stomach meridians, making it a go-to cooling food for clearing summer heat and supporting gentle detoxification.

Can I eat mung bean every day?

For most people, moderate consumption of mung bean soup or congee during hot weather is safe. However, because mung bean is cold in nature, those with a weak or cold spleen and stomach — who tend to experience loose stools, poor digestion, or a feeling of coldness — should limit their intake and balance it with warming ingredients like ginger or dates.

Is mung bean safe during pregnancy?

Mung bean is traditionally considered safe in moderate amounts during pregnancy, especially to relieve summer heat. However, due to its cold nature, pregnant women are advised to consume it in moderation and pair it with warming ingredients such as ginger or jujube dates. It is always recommended to consult a qualified healthcare provider familiar with TCM food therapy during pregnancy.

How should I prepare mung bean soup for maximum benefit?

For a traditional mung bean soup (lǜ dòu tāng), rinse ½ cup of whole mung beans and boil them with 6 cups of water. Once boiling, reduce to a simmer for 20–30 minutes until the beans are tender but not completely broken down — the liquid should take on a pale green hue. Do not overcook until the beans burst, as the strongest cooling properties are believed to reside in the skin and the clear broth. Add a slice of fresh ginger to balance the cold nature. Serve warm or at room temperature.
