Jujube Dates (红枣) — The Sweetest Qi Tonic

🌿 Ingredient Deep Dive · 7 min read

Introduction: The Humble Superfruit

If you have ever wandered the aisles of a Chinese herbal shop or peeked into the pantry of a Cantonese grandmother, you have seen them: small, shrivelled, deep-red fruits that look like a cross between a date and a prune. These are jujube dates — known as hong zao (红枣) in Mandarin, da jujube in botanical circles, and simply "red dates" in many Western groceries. But do not let their modest appearance fool you. In Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM), jujube dates are one of the most frequently prescribed food-herbs in the entire materia medica.

Jujubes have been cultivated in China for more than 4,000 years, making them one of the oldest domesticated fruits on the planet. They appear in classical TCM texts such as the Shen Nong Ben Cao Jing (Divine Farmer's Materia Medica), where they are classified as a top-grade, non-toxic herb — the highest designation an herb could receive. The text praises jujube for "nourishing the five viscera, calming the spirit, and prolonging life."

Today, as interest in functional foods and ancestral nutrition surges, the jujube date is enjoying a well-deserved renaissance. In this deep dive, we will explore the TCM energetics of jujube, its health benefits, and — most importantly — practical, delicious ways to bring this sweet qi tonic into your daily bowl, mug, and pot.

What Are Jujube Dates? A Botanical Snapshot

Botanical name: Ziziphus jujuba (family Rhamnaceae)

Common names: Jujube, Chinese date, red date, hong zao, da zao

Appearance: When fresh, jujubes are smooth-skinned, olive-to-date-shaped fruits that ripen from green to a mottled yellow-red. Dried, they become deeply wrinkled, mahogany-red, and intensely sweet — like a date, but with a slightly more floral, apple-like undertone.

Taste: Sweet, with a subtle tang when fresh; intensely sweet and sticky when dried.

Dried jujubes are the form most commonly used in TCM and cooking. They are available year-round in Asian grocery stores, herbal shops, and increasingly in mainstream health-food retailers. You will typically find them sold in plastic bags or bulk bins, pitted or whole. (More on the pit question later.)

TCM Energetics: Warm, Sweet, and Deeply Nourishing

In TCM, every food has a thermal nature, a flavour, and a meridian affinity. Jujube date's profile reads like a prescription for deficiency and depletion:

  • Nature: Warm (slightly warm, to be precise — not aggressively heating like ginger or cinnamon)
  • Flavour: Sweet (the "sweet" of TCM is not just sugar — it refers to a harmonising, moistening, and building quality)
  • Meridians: Spleen, Stomach, and Heart
  • Actions: Tonifies qi, nourishes blood, harmonises the middle jiao (digestive region), calms the spirit (shen), and moderates the harsh effects of other herbs

Let us break down each of these actions, because they are the key to understanding why jujube appears in so many classic TCM formulas — and why you should consider adding it to your own kitchen.

Qi Tonification: Building Your Internal Battery

In TCM, qi (pronounced "chee") is the vital life force that animates every cell and organ. Think of it as your internal battery. When qi is abundant, you feel energised, your digestion hums along smoothly, your immune system is robust, and your mind is clear. When qi is deficient, you feel tired, catch every cold that goes around, speak in a soft voice, and struggle with bloating after meals.

Jujube dates are classified as a qi tonic. They especially support the Spleen qi — and here, "Spleen" refers not just to the physical spleen organ but to the entire digestive-and-transformative function of the body. In TCM, the Spleen is responsible for extracting gu qi (food qi) from what you eat and transforming it into usable energy and blood. A strong Spleen means strong digestion, steady energy, and healthy flesh. A weak Spleen means fatigue, loose stools, poor appetite, and a tendency toward worry and overthinking.

The sweet flavour of jujube enters the Spleen and Stomach meridians, providing a gentle, steady source of qi-building energy. Unlike a sugar rush — which spikes and crashes — the complex carbohydrates and polysaccharides in jujube offer sustained nourishment. This is why jujube is often used in convalescence, after illness, and for anyone who feels "run down."

Blood Nourishing: Building Rich, Vital Fluids

If qi is the captain of the ship, blood (xue) is the crew and cargo. In TCM, blood has a broader meaning than in Western physiology — it includes the physical blood, but also the nutritive fluids that moisten tissues, nourish the nerves, and support healthy menstruation and sleep.

Jujube dates are considered one of the best food-grade blood tonics. They are rich in iron, vitamin C (which enhances iron absorption), and B vitamins — all essentials for healthy red-blood-cell production. But in TCM terms, their blood-nourishing action is attributed to their warm, sweet nature and their affinity for the Spleen and Heart meridians. The Spleen is the source of blood production (it transforms food qi into blood), and the Heart governs blood circulation. By tonifying both, jujube builds the raw material for healthy blood and supports its smooth circulation.

This makes jujube an excellent daily food for anyone who experiences:

  • Pale complexion or dizziness
  • Poor memory or difficulty concentrating
  • Dry skin or brittle nails
  • Scanty or irregular menstruation
  • Insomnia or restless sleep (the Heart houses the shen, and blood deficiency can agitate the spirit)

Harmonising the Middle Jiao: A Friend to Digestion

One of jujube's most practical qualities — and the reason it appears in roughly one-third of all classical TCM herbal formulas — is its ability to harmonise the middle jiao. This simply means it balances and protects the digestive system.

When used alongside stronger herbs (especially harsh, draining, or dispersing ones like ephedra or rhubarb), jujube moderates their intensity and protects the stomach from damage. Think of it as a diplomatic envoy that sits between different ingredients and makes sure everyone plays nicely together.

For everyday eaters, this harmonising quality means jujube can soothe an upset stomach, reduce bloating, and support the slow, steady digestion of congee, soups, and other gentle foods. It is particularly helpful for people with weak digestive fire — those who feel cold after eating, struggle with loose stools, or feel that food "sits like a rock" in their stomach.

Calming the Shen: Sweet Dreams

Because jujube nourishes blood and the Heart, it also has a gentle calming effect on the shen (spirit). In TCM, when blood is deficient, the shen has no "home" to rest in, leading to anxiety, palpitations, and insomnia. The classic formula Suan Zao Ren Tang (Sour Jujube Decoction) uses the seed of a related plant — Ziziphus spinosa — specifically for insomnia, but the fruit of Ziziphus jujuba also contributes to calm, restful sleep through its blood-nourishing action.

A warm cup of jujube tea before bed is a lovely, gentle way to invite the body into rest. It is sweet, comforting, and free of caffeine — a perfect nightcap.

Jujube in the Modern Kitchen: Three Essential Ways to Use Them

The beauty of jujube dates is their versatility. They are equally at home in a morning tea, a savoury soup, or a sweet congee. Here are three foundational preparations that will turn you into a jujube aficionado.

1. Jujube Tea (Hong Zao Cha)

The simplest and most rewarding way to start working with jujube is to make tea. This is the "gateway preparation" — it requires nothing more than a few dried jujubes and hot water.

Basic Jujube Tea:

  • 5–7 dried jujube dates
  • 500 ml (2 cups) water
  • Optional: 3–4 goji berries, 1 slice fresh ginger, a small piece of astragalus root, or a pinch of licorice root

Method:

  1. Rinse the dried jujubes. If they are whole (with pits), score the skin with a knife or gently crush them with the flat of a knife blade so the flavour can escape. Some people pit them entirely; others leave the pit in — both are fine, but pitting makes the tea slightly easier to drink.
  2. Place the jujubes in a small pot or thermos flask. If using a pot, add the water, bring to a boil, then reduce to a simmer for 15–20 minutes. If using a thermos, pour boiling water over the dates and steep for at least 20 minutes (longer is better — some people leave it overnight).
  3. Strain, or simply fish out the jujubes with chopsticks. The cooked jujubes themselves are edible and delicious — eat them as a sweet, chewy snack after drinking the tea.

The resulting brew is a clear, amber-red liquid with a deep, caramel-like sweetness and a subtle floral note. It requires no sweetener — the jujube provides all the sugar you need.

Variations:

  • Jujube-Ginger Tea: Add 3–4 slices of fresh ginger for a warming, circulation-boosting twist. Excellent for cold mornings or when you feel a chill coming on.
  • Jujube-Goji Tea: The combination of jujube (qi tonic) and goji (jing tonic and eye nourisher) is a classic TCM pairing. Add a heaping teaspoon of dried goji berries during the last 5 minutes of simmering.
  • Red Date and Longan Tea: Add 4–5 dried longan (dragon eye) fruits for extra blood-nourishing and calming benefits. This is a beautiful evening tea.

2. Jujube Congee (Hong Zao Zhou)

Congee — a savoury or sweet rice porridge — is the quintessential TCM recovery food. When the digestion is weak, congee is easy to assimilate, providing qi-rich nourishment without taxing the Spleen. Jujube congee takes this to another level.

Basic Jujube Congee:

  • 1 cup short-grain white rice or jasmine rice (or ½ cup rice + ½ cup sticky rice for a creamier texture)
  • 8–10 cups water or bone broth
  • 8–10 dried jujube dates, rinsed and scored
  • A pinch of salt
  • Optional: 1 tablespoon goji berries, 1 small piece dried tangerine peel (chen pi), a few slices of astragalus root

Method:

  1. Rinse the rice until the water runs nearly clear. Drain.
  2. In a large heavy-bottomed pot, combine rice, water or broth, jujubes, and any optional additions. Bring to a boil over medium-high heat, then reduce to a gentle simmer.
  3. Cook uncovered, stirring occasionally, for 60–90 minutes. The congee is ready when the rice grains have broken down completely and the porridge has a silky, almost creamy consistency. Add more water if it becomes too thick.
  4. Remove the jujube pits (they will float to the surface or can be fished out). Stir in the goji berries and salt in the last 5 minutes if using.
  5. Serve warm. Drizzle with a little toasted sesame oil and garnish with chopped spring onions if you like a savoury version, or add a drizzle of honey and a sprinkle of cinnamon for a sweet breakfast bowl.

Why this works: White rice is naturally sweet and enters the Spleen and Stomach meridians. Simmered for a long time, it becomes an easily digestible qi tonic on its own. Adding jujube deepens the sweet, nourishing quality and adds a mild blood-tonifying element. The result is a bowl that feels like a warm hug for your digestive system.

3. Jujube in Soup (Hong Zao Tang)

Savory soups enriched with jujube are a staple of Cantonese and other regional Chinese cuisines. The sweet fruit balances savoury broths beautifully, adding depth and complexity — much the way a small amount of carrot or tomato adds sweetness to a Western stock.

Classic Chicken and Jujube Soup:

  • 2 bone-in chicken thighs or 4 chicken drumsticks (or 500 g bone-in chicken pieces)
  • 10–12 dried jujube dates, rinsed and scored
  • 3–4 slices fresh ginger
  • 2–3 dried shiitake mushrooms, rehydrated (reserve the liquid)
  • 1 small piece dried astragalus root (huang qi) — optional but recommended
  • 1.5 litres (6 cups) water
  • Salt to taste

Method:

  1. Blanch the chicken pieces in boiling water for 3 minutes to remove impurities. Drain, rinse, and discard the blanching water.
  2. Place the blanched chicken, jujubes, ginger, shiitakes (plus their soaking liquid), and astragalus in a large soup pot. Cover with 1.5 litres of fresh water.
  3. Bring to a boil, then reduce to a gentle simmer. Skim off any foam that rises to the surface.
  4. Simmer, partially covered, for 1.5–2 hours. The broth should reduce slightly and deepen in colour, taking on a warm amber tone from the jujubes.
  5. Remove the astragalus root and ginger slices before serving. Fish out the jujube pits. Season with salt to taste.
  6. Serve the broth with the chicken meat and mushrooms. You can also add leafy greens like bok choy or spinach in the last 5 minutes of cooking.

Vegetarian version: Replace chicken with soaked dried shiitake, fresh lotus root (cut into thick rounds), and a handful of peanuts (hong yi hua sheng — red-skinned peanuts are traditional). Simmer for 1–2 hours for a deeply nourishing, mineral-rich broth that is equally qi- and blood-building.

Choosing, Storing, and Preparing Jujube Dates

How to Choose Good Jujubes

When buying dried jujubes, look for fruits that are plump, glossy, and deep red-brown. They should feel soft-ish but not sticky. Avoid jujubes that are overly hard and shrivelled (too dry, likely old), or have visible mould or a fermented smell. The best quality jujubes come from China's Xinjiang or Shaanxi regions, where the climate produces exceptionally sweet, fleshy fruits.

Pitted vs. Whole

You can buy jujubes already pitted, which is convenient. However, many TCM practitioners prefer whole jujubes (with pits) for cooking, as the pit is said to have its own mild medicinal properties and the fruit holds its shape better during long simmering. Just remember to warn diners about the pits, or remove them before serving.

Storage

Dried jujubes keep well in an airtight container in a cool, dark pantry for up to a year. In humid climates, store them in the refrigerator or freezer to prevent mould. Properly stored jujubes will remain pliable and intensely sweet.

Are There Any Precautions?

Jujube dates are generally considered one of the safest foods in TCM — the Shen Nong Ben Cao Jing classified them as "top grade," meaning they are non-toxic and suitable for long-term daily use. However, a few considerations apply:

  • Excess phlegm-dampness: Because jujube is sweet and nourishing, people with signs of dampness (a swollen-feeling tongue, thick white coating, sticky phlegm, bloating, a heavy sensation in the body) should use it in moderation. Too much sweet, dampening food can exacerbate phlegm.
  • Diabetes and blood sugar: Dried jujubes are high in natural sugars. If you are managing diabetes or insulin resistance, use them sparingly — a couple of dates in a pot of tea or soup is fine, but handfuls of dried jujube as a snack will spike blood sugar.
  • Heat conditions: Jujube is warm in nature. People with full-heat conditions (red face, fever, intense thirst, constipation, irritability) should not overconsume it. Pair it with cooling ingredients like mung beans or chrysanthemum if needed.
  • Pregnancy: Jujube is generally safe and even beneficial during pregnancy (it nourishes blood for both mother and baby), but as with all herbs, consult your midwife or healthcare provider.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I eat jujube dates raw (fresh)?

Yes, fresh jujubes are a delight — crisp, sweet, and apple-like, with a small central pit. They are increasingly available at farmers' markets and Asian grocery stores in the late summer and early autumn. Dried jujubes can also be eaten straight out of the bag as a chewy, candy-like snack.

What is the difference between red dates and jujube dates?

In common usage, "red dates" and "jujube dates" refer to the same fruit — Ziziphus jujuba. The term "red date" is more common in spice aisles and Asian supermarkets, while "jujube" is the botanical name. True dates (Phoenix dactylifera, the Medjool or Deglet Noor variety) are a completely different plant family and have different TCM energetics.

Can I use jujube in baking or desserts?

Absolutely. Pitted jujubes can be chopped and added to muffins, oatmeal cookies, energy bars, or date-based desserts in place of Medjool dates. They are less sweet than Medjools, so you may want to add a touch of honey or maple syrup to compensate. Jujube purée also makes a lovely natural sweetener for cakes and puddings.

How many jujube dates should I eat per day?

There is no strict rule, but a typical daily serving is 5–10 dried jujubes (about 20–40 g). This is enough to provide noticeable qi-supporting and blood-nourishing benefits without overdoing the sugar or warm nature. In a soup or congee for the whole family, 10–15 dates for a large pot is standard.

The Bottom Line: Your Sweetest Health Ally

Jujube dates are a rare gem in the world of functional foods — they are simultaneously a delicious ingredient, a gentle medicine, and a bridge between ancient food wisdom and modern wellness. Their warm, sweet energetics tonify the Spleen and Stomach, nourish the blood, calm the spirit, and protect the digestive system — all while adding a caramel-like sweetness to teas, congees, and soups that the whole family will enjoy.

Whether you are recovering from an illness, navigating a stressful season, looking to support healthy menstruation, or simply wanting to deepen your connection to the nourishing traditions of TCM, jujube dates are a wonderful place to start.

Add a few to your morning tea. Toss a handful into your next pot of congee. Simmer them gently in a bone broth with ginger and shiitake. Let their gentle, sweet warmth seep into your bones.

Your qi will thank you.
