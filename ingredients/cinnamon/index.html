Cinnamon (肉桂) — Warmth for the Kidneys

🌿 Ingredient Deep Dive · 6 min read

In Traditional Chinese Medicine, cinnamon holds a special place as one of the most important warming herbs. Known as ròu guì (肉桂) in Chinese, it is the dried bark of the cassia tree (Cinnamomum cassia) — distinct from the milder Ceylon cinnamon found in most Western kitchens. While both come from the same botanical family, TCM practitioners have always preferred cassia for its stronger, deeper warming action.

Cinnamon's primary role in TCM is to warm the Kidneys and strengthen Yang — the body's internal "fire" that drives metabolism, circulation, and vitality. When Kidney Yang declines, signs like cold hands and feet, lower back ache, frequent urination, and a general lack of warmth appear. Cinnamon is the classic remedy for these patterns.

Cassia vs. True Cinnamon: A TCM Perspective

This distinction matters. The cinnamon used in Chinese herbal medicine is almost always cassia cinnamon (Cinnamomum cassia), not true or Ceylon cinnamon (Cinnamomum verum).

  • Cassia (ròu guì) — Thicker, darker bark; stronger, spicier flavor; higher essential oil content; more warming. This is the medicinal form.
  • Ceylon (true cinnamon) — Thinner, lighter bark; milder, sweeter flavor; lower oil content; gentler. Better for daily culinary use.

For therapeutic purposes in the TCM tradition, choose cassia. For everyday cooking, Ceylon is milder and contains less coumarin.

The Warming Yang Concept

Kidney Yang is like the pilot light of a furnace — it provides warmth and drives transformation throughout the body. Cinnamon enters the Kidney and Spleen meridians and is classified as:

  • Nature: Hot (rè)
  • Flavor: Pungent (xīn) and Sweet (gān)
  • Meridians: Kidney, Spleen, Liver, Heart

Its pungent flavor moves and circulates, while its hot nature provides deep, lasting warmth. Cinnamon is said to "kindle the fire at the Gate of Life (Ming Men)" — the space between the Kidneys where the body's original Yang resides.

Cooking with Cinnamon for Wellness

🥘 Braised Dishes

Cassia bark is a staple in Chinese braised dishes. Add 1-2 sticks to red-braised beef, pork belly, or lamb stews with star anise, ginger, and soy sauce. Long, slow cooking extracts its warming properties into the broth.

🍵 Warming Cinnamon Tea

Simmer 1 small cassia stick (or ½ tsp powdered cassia) in 2 cups of water for 10 minutes. Add 2 slices fresh ginger and 3 jujube dates. Strain and sip warm. Used for cold hands and feet, sluggish digestion, and early signs of cold.

🍎 Desserts

A sprinkle of cinnamon on baked apples, oatmeal, or rice pudding provides warming energy. Combine with goji berries and walnuts for a Kidney-nourishing treat.

Precautions & Contraindications

  • Heat patterns: Those with signs of internal heat (red face, thirst, night sweats) should avoid cinnamon.
  • Pregnancy: Culinary amounts are generally safe; avoid medicinal doses.
  • Coumarin: Cassia contains coumarin. Limit medicinal use to 1-2g per day.
  • Bleeding disorders: Cinnamon may thin the blood in high doses.
