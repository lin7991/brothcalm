Mulberry (Sang Shen) — The Sweet Blood-Nourishing Berry

🌿 Ingredient Deep Dive · 6 min read

A Sweet Secret from the Mulberry Tree

When you picture mulberries, you might recall a childhood memory of stained fingers and purple-tipped tongues under a wide shade tree. But in Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM), this humble little berry — known as Sang Shen (桑椹) — has been treasured for centuries as one of the most effective blood-nourishing and yin-tonifying herbs in the apothecary.

Unlike the tangier goji berry or the earthy he shou wu, Sang Shen is almost dessert-like in its sweetness. Yet beneath that gentle flavour lies a powerful tonic that TCM practitioners traditionally reach for when the body's foundational fluids — Blood and Yin — run low. Whether you're navigating the dryness of menopause, recovering from illness, or simply feeling worn down by modern life, mulberry may be the gentle support your system is asking for.

What Is Sang Shen? TCM Properties

In TCM, every ingredient has a unique energetic signature — a specific temperature, flavour, and meridian affinity that determines how it interacts with the body. Here is the profile of Sang Shen:

Chinese NameSang Shen (桑椹)
NatureCool (slightly cold)
FlavourSweet, Sour
Meridians EnteredHeart, Liver, Kidney
Key ActionsNourishes Blood, Tonifies Yin, Generates Fluids, Moistens Dryness

The sweet flavour enters the Spleen and builds Qi and Blood, while the sour note constrains and astringes — preventing the fluids it generates from leaking away unhelpfully. Its cool nature makes it especially suited for situations where heat from Yin deficiency is present: think hot palms, dry throat, restless sleep, or a low-grade feeling of internal warmth.

Key Traditional Benefits of Mulberry (Sang Shen)

🩸 Nourishes Liver and Kidney Blood

In TCM, the Liver stores Blood and the Kidneys store Essence (Jing). Sang Shen is said to enter both of these organ systems directly, making it a dual-action tonic. When Liver Blood is sufficient, the complexion is rosy, the vision is clear, and the nails are strong. When Kidney Essence is abundant, the hair is lustrous, the bones are strong, and the mind is sharp.

For those experiencing premature greying, dry or thinning hair, dizziness, or blurred vision, Sang Shen has traditionally been used as a gentle, long-term restorative. It is not a dramatic stimulant — it works slowly and steadily, rebuilding from within.

💧 Generates Fluids and Moistens Dryness

One of TCM's core insights is that health depends on sufficient body fluids — the moistening matrix that keeps our skin supple, our joints lubricated, and our tissues hydrated. When Yin is depleted, dryness shows up everywhere: dry eyes, dry skin, dry cough, dry stools.

Sang Shen's sweet-sour flavour combination is considered especially effective at "engendering fluids" — stimulating the body's own production of healthy moisture. This is why it was traditionally recommended for thirst due to Yin deficiency, persistent dry cough, and chronic constipation, particularly in older adults or those recovering from feverish illness.

🌿 Supports Healthy Bowel Regularity

Dryness in the Large Intestine is a common pattern, especially among those over forty or those whose lifestyle includes long hours in dry environments. Sang Shen is traditionally believed to moisten the Intestines and promote smooth bowel movements without the harsh, gripping sensation that some stronger laxatives can cause.

A small handful of dried mulberries steeped overnight and eaten first thing in the morning is a time-honoured gentle approach for those dealing with Yin-deficient constipation.

🧘 Calms the Spirit (Shen)

When Blood and Yin are deficient, the Spirit (Shen) has no stable "home" to rest in. This can manifest as anxiety, insomnia, restless sleep, heart palpitations, or a tendency toward irritability. By nourishing the foundational fluids, Sang Shen helps settle the mind — not through sedation, but through the deeply grounding effect of a well-nourished body.

This makes mulberry an excellent ally for anyone whose sleep difficulties are linked to chronic stress, overwork, or hormonal transitions.

How to Use Sang Shen in Daily Life

One of the best things about Sang Shen is how easy it is to incorporate into a modern diet. Here are some practical ways to use it:

🍵 Simple Sang Shen Tea

Place 15–30 grams (about 2 tablespoons) of dried mulberries in a mug. Pour boiling water over them, cover, and steep for 10–15 minutes. The water will turn a deep, jewel-like purple. Drink the tea and eat the rehydrated berries. Enjoy once daily, preferably in the morning or early afternoon. For a mild variation, add a few slices of fresh ginger to warm the formula slightly.

🥣 Mulberry Congee

Add 30 grams of dried mulberries to your morning congee (rice porridge) along with 10 goji berries and a teaspoon of honey. Simmer together until the rice is soft and the berries have plumped up. This is a classic tonic breakfast in TCM food therapy for building blood and nourishing yin.

🍷 Sang Shen Tonic Wine

Place 250 grams of dried mulberries in a clean glass jar. Cover with 500 ml of rice wine, sake, or a mild cooking wine. Seal and let steep in a cool, dark place for two to four weeks. Take one small shot glass (about 20–30 ml) per day as a warming blood tonic. This preparation is especially popular in colder months.

🥤 Smoothies and Snacks

Dried Sang Shen can be sprinkled over oatmeal, yogurt, or smoothie bowls. It also blends beautifully into berry smoothies. Because of its natural sweetness, it reduces the need for added sweeteners while delivering concentrated phytonutrients.

Modern Research: What Science Says

While TCM has used Sang Shen for millennia, modern science is beginning to offer intriguing insights into why this little purple fruit is so potent:

  • Anthocyanins — Mulberries are rich in anthocyanins, the pigments responsible for their deep purple-black colour. These are potent antioxidants believed to help protect cells from oxidative stress.
  • Resveratrol — The same compound found in red wine and celebrated for its cardiovascular protective potential is present in mulberries, particularly in the skin of the fruit.
  • Iron and Vitamin C — Mulberries contain notable amounts of iron, along with vitamin C, which enhances iron absorption. This makes the TCM claim of "nourishing Blood" remarkably tangible from a nutritional standpoint.
  • Blood Sugar Support — Some studies have investigated mulberry leaf (Sang Ye) and mulberry fruit for their potential role in supporting healthy blood sugar metabolism, through compounds that may inhibit certain carbohydrate-digesting enzymes.

These findings do not "prove" TCM — the frameworks are too different for that. But they do suggest that the traditional reputation of Sang Shen as a nutrient-dense restorative is built on a very real biochemical foundation.

Who Should Use Sang Shen?

Sang Shen is generally considered safe and is appropriate for most adults. It is particularly well-suited for:

  • People experiencing signs of Yin deficiency: dry skin, dry eyes, night sweats, hot flashes, scanty dark urine
  • Those with Blood deficiency: pale complexion, blurred vision, scanty periods, dizziness, brittle nails
  • Older adults looking for gentle, long-term support for hydration, bowel regularity, and vitality
  • Anyone recovering from illness, especially if accompanied by fever, fluid loss, or general depletion

Cautions and Considerations

Although Sang Shen has a very favourable safety profile, a few considerations are worth keeping in mind:

  • Spleen Qi Deficiency with Dampness — Because Sang Shen is cool and moistening, those with weak digestion accompanied by loose stools, bloating, or a sensation of heaviness should use it in moderation. Pairing it with a small amount of ginger or a pinch of cinnamon can help offset the coolness.
  • Pregnancy and Nursing — While mulberries are a common food, concentrated or therapeutic amounts should only be used under the guidance of a qualified practitioner during pregnancy and lactation.
  • Unripe berries — Raw, unripe mulberries contain compounds that may cause mild digestive upset. Stick to ripe or dried berries, which are far more common and carry no such concern.

Final Thoughts

Sang Shen occupies a special place in the TCM pantry — a food that also heals, a medicine that also delights. In a world that often asks us to push harder and burn brighter, this little purple berry quietly reminds us that sometimes the most profound nourishment comes from the simplest source: a sweet fruit, a warm cup of tea, and the ancient wisdom that knows what the body truly needs.

Whether you enjoy it as a tea, a congee, or a simple snack, let Mulberry be your gentle companion on the journey toward deeper replenishment.
