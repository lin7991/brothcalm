White Fungus (Yin Er) — The Beauty Mushroom for Lung Moisture

🌿 Ingredient Deep Dive · 6 min read

The Delicate Fungus with Ancient Roots

White fungus — known as yin er (银耳) or bai mu er (白木耳) in Chinese — is a translucent, gelatinous fungus that has been cherished in Chinese cuisine and Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM) for centuries. Its delicate, frilly appearance and mild, subtly sweet flavor make it a versatile ingredient in both savory soups and soothing desserts. But beyond its culinary appeal, white fungus holds a special place in the TCM materia medica as a gentle but powerful ingredient for lung health, hydration, and radiant skin.

Often called the "beauty mushroom" or "snow fungus," Tremella fuciformis has been referenced in Chinese medical texts dating back to the Shennong Bencao Jing (Divine Farmer's Materia Medica), one of the earliest and most revered pharmacopoeias in Chinese medicine. In TCM theory, white fungus is classified as sweet in flavor (味甘) and neutral in nature (平性), making it suitable for a wide range of constitutional types when used appropriately.

TCM Profile: Sweet, Neutral, and Moisturizing

In the framework of TCM food therapy, every ingredient carries specific energetic properties. White fungus is believed to:

  • Nourish Lung Yin (润肺): The lungs are considered a "delicate organ" in TCM, particularly vulnerable to dryness — especially in autumn or in dry indoor environments. White fungus is traditionally used to support lung moisture and ease dry coughs.
  • Generate Body Fluids (生津): It is thought to help the body produce its own fluids, which can be beneficial for symptoms of dryness such as dry throat, dry lips, or parched skin.
  • Support the Stomach (益胃): Both the Lung and Stomach meridians are associated with white fungus, and it is traditionally used to support a healthy digestive system.
  • Promote Skin Health (养颜): The famous TCM dictum — "the lungs control the skin and body hair" (肺主皮毛) — connects lung function with skin vitality. By nourishing the lungs, white fungus is traditionally understood to benefit the complexion.

In TCM cosmology, white fungus's pale, almost translucent appearance mirrors the color white, which is the element associated with the Lung and autumn. This makes it a particularly fitting ingredient for seasonal nourishment as the weather turns cooler and drier.

The Beauty Mushroom: Skin and Collagen Connection

White fungus's reputation as a beauty food has attracted growing scientific interest. While it does not contain animal-derived collagen — the structural protein found in bone broth and animal skin — it is exceptionally rich in tremella polysaccharides, a type of water-soluble dietary fiber that can absorb and retain large amounts of moisture.

Some studies suggest that these polysaccharides may have moisture-binding properties that could support skin hydration when consumed regularly. The gelatinous texture that white fungus develops when simmered is visually reminiscent of collagen-rich preparations, which is likely why it earned the nickname "plant collagen" in Chinese wellness circles.

Beyond polysaccharides, white fungus also contains dietary fiber, vitamin D, and various antioxidants. In TCM theory, nourishing the lungs with moistening foods like white fungus supports the skin through the lung-skin meridian connection — a holistic approach to beauty from the inside out.

Selecting and Storing White Fungus

White fungus is most commonly available in dried form at Asian grocery stores, herbal shops, or online. When selecting dried white fungus, look for:

  • Pale golden or cream-colored pieces — a uniform light color indicates quality. Dark spots or uneven browning may indicate age or poor storage.
  • Large, intact fronds rather than broken crumbs. Whole pieces typically rehydrate better and yield a more satisfying texture.
  • A faint, earthy aroma with no mustiness or unusual odor.
  • Packages with minimal dust or debris at the bottom.

Store dried white fungus in an airtight container in a cool, dark, dry place. Properly stored, it can last for up to a year. Avoid storing it near strong-smelling ingredients — like any dried fungus, it can absorb odors.

How to Soak and Prepare White Fungus

Proper preparation makes all the difference between a silky, gelatinous soup and a disappointing one. Here is the step-by-step process:

  1. Rinse the dried fungus briefly under cold running water to remove surface dust.
  2. Soak in cold or warm water for 30 minutes to 2 hours, until the pieces have expanded and softened significantly. The soak time depends on the thickness of the dried pieces — check them periodically.
  3. Drain and inspect. You will notice a hard, yellowish base at the stem end of each piece. Trim this off with kitchen scissors or a knife — it does not soften during cooking and can be unpleasantly crunchy.
  4. Tear the fungus into bite-sized pieces. The frilly edges contribute to that signature gelatinous texture. Smaller pieces release more polysaccharides into the cooking liquid.
  5. Rinse again to remove any remaining debris.
  6. Your white fungus is now ready for soups, desserts, congee, or simmered dishes.

Cooking with White Fungus: Soups, Desserts, and Congee

White fungus's neutral flavor makes it an adaptable ingredient in both sweet and savory contexts. Its primary role in a dish is textural — it absorbs the flavors around it while contributing a silky, almost creamy mouthfeel that thickens the broth naturally.

Some classic Chinese preparations include:

  • White fungus and red date soup — the most beloved preparation, combining the fungus's moisture with the warming, blood-nourishing properties of red dates (jujube).
  • Snow fungus dessert — a sweet, chilled soup often made with rock sugar, goji berries, lotus seeds, and dried longan.
  • White fungus congee — rice porridge enriched with soaked white fungus, ginger, and either sweet or savory toppings.
  • Savory white fungus soup — combined with chicken, pork, or vegetables in a clear broth, sometimes with astragalus or other tonic herbs.

Recipe: White Fungus & Red Date Soup

This classic soup is the best introduction to cooking with white fungus. It is mildly sweet, deeply comforting, and traditionally enjoyed in autumn and winter to support lung moisture and skin health.

Ingredient Amount Notes
Dried white fungus 15–20 g (about 1 large piece) Soaked and torn into pieces
Red dates (jujube) 8–10 Pitted
Goji berries 1 tbsp Rinsed
Rock sugar 30–40 g (to taste) Or coconut sugar / honey
Water 1.5 liters Filtered
Dried longan (optional) 5–6 Adds natural sweetness
Fresh ginger (optional) 2–3 thin slices Balances the sweetness

Instructions

  1. Prepare the white fungus — Soak the dried white fungus in cold or warm water for 30–60 minutes until fully expanded. Drain, trim off the hard yellow stem base, and tear the fungus into bite-sized florets.
  2. Rinse the red dates and goji berries — if using dried longan, rinse those as well. Pit the red dates by slicing them open and removing the stone.
  3. Simmer the fungus and dates — In a large pot or slow cooker, combine the white fungus, red dates, and water. Bring to a boil, then reduce to a gentle simmer. Cover and cook for 40–60 minutes, until the fungus becomes translucent and the liquid turns slightly viscous.
  4. Add the remaining ingredients — Stir in the rock sugar, goji berries, and optional longan and ginger. Continue simmering for another 15–20 minutes.
  5. Adjust sweetness — Taste and add more sugar if desired. The soup should be pleasantly sweet with a hint of fruitiness from the dates and goji berries.
  6. Serve warm or at room temperature — This soup can also be chilled and enjoyed as a cold dessert. The texture will become even more gelatinous as it cools.

Tip: For a thicker, more gelatinous consistency, simmer for up to 90 minutes total or use a slow cooker on low for 3–4 hours. The longer it cooks, the more polysaccharides are released into the liquid.

Seasonal Wisdom: White Fungus in Autumn

In TCM seasonal living, autumn is the season of the Lung and the element of Metal. It is a time of contraction, letting go, and turning inward. The climate becomes dry — both outdoors in the cooler air and indoors with heating — and this dryness is thought to affect the lungs directly. Foods with moistening properties, like white fungus, are traditionally recommended during this season to counteract the drying influences.

Pairing white fungus with other autumn-friendly ingredients creates a synergistic effect. Red dates warm and nourish the blood. Goji berries support liver and kidney yin. Lotus seeds and lily bulb (bai he) are also classic partners, each contributing their own moisture-retaining qualities. A bowl of white fungus soup in autumn is not just a comfort food — it is a seasonal practice rooted in thousands of years of observation.

Precautions and Considerations

While white fungus is generally considered safe for most people when consumed as a food, a few considerations are worth keeping in mind:

  • Digestive comfort: In large amounts, white fungus's gelatinous nature may cause mild digestive discomfort or bloating, especially for those with weak digestion. Start with a small serving and see how your body responds.
  • TCM constitution: Those with a cold-dampness constitution — characterized by chilliness, loose stools, a feeling of heaviness, and poor digestion — should consume white fungus in moderation, as it may exacerbate dampness in sensitive individuals.
  • Fresh vs. dried: Fresh white fungus is less commonly available outside of China. Always use dried white fungus that has been properly rehydrated. Do not eat raw, dried white fungus — it must be cooked.
  • Quality matters: Purchase white fungus from reputable sources. Some cheaper products may be heavily processed or have undergone sulfur bleaching, which can affect both flavor and nutritional quality.

As with any dietary change, especially for therapeutic purposes, it is wise to consult a qualified healthcare provider or TCM practitioner who can offer guidance tailored to your individual constitution and health needs.

The Big Picture: One Ingredient, Many Dimensions

White fungus is a beautiful example of how TCM food therapy approaches wellness — not with a single compound or isolated nutrient, but with an understanding of an ingredient's energetic signature, its seasonal relevance, and its relationship to the whole person. It is a food that hydrates, a fungus that transforms into a silky elixir, and a traditional remedy that has quietly nourished generations.

Whether you are drawn to white fungus for its potential skin benefits, its role in autumn lung care, or simply its comforting texture in a warm bowl of soup, it is an ingredient worth knowing. Start with the classic white fungus and red date soup, experiment with your own additions, and discover why this unassuming mushroom has earned its place as a jewel of Chinese food therapy.
